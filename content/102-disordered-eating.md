# Disordered Eating & Your Relationship With Food

Everything else in this course is about optimisation — more protein, better
timing, lower body fat, higher performance. This chapter exists because **that
same material, applied to the wrong person at the wrong time, becomes a
problem.**

Nutrition education is not neutral. Learning to track intake, evaluate food
quality, and manipulate body composition gives you real tools. It also gives you
the vocabulary and the technique to restrict with far more precision than you
could before. People who develop eating disorders are not uninformed about
nutrition. They are frequently the best-informed people in the room.

> **If you're reading this and recognising yourself, skip to the bottom of the
> chapter.** The Swedish resources are listed there, they're free, and you do not
> need a diagnosis to use them.

## Disordered eating vs. eating disorders

These are different things, and conflating them helps nobody.

**Eating disorders** are diagnosable psychiatric conditions — anorexia nervosa,
bulimia nervosa, binge eating disorder, ARFID, OSFED. Anorexia nervosa has one of
the **highest mortality rates of any psychiatric illness**. These require
professional treatment.

**Disordered eating** is the much larger territory around them — behaviours and
thought patterns that cause distress or harm without meeting diagnostic criteria.
Skipping meals to "make up for" eating. Anxiety when you can't weigh your food.
Exercising to earn or repay calories. Rigid food rules with emotional
consequences when broken.

The relationship between them matters: **disordered eating is the single strongest
predictor of developing a clinical eating disorder.** It's the on-ramp. And it's
extremely common in gyms and sport — normalised, often praised, and frequently
mistaken for discipline.

## The line that actually matters

Not *what* you eat. **How much space it takes up, and what happens when the plan
breaks.**

Two people can eat identically. One has a structure that serves their goals and
adapts when life happens. The other has a cage. The food is the same; the
relationship isn't.

| Healthy structure | Warning territory |
| --- | --- |
| You can eat off-plan at a friend's dinner without distress | Social events are avoided, or cause real anxiety |
| Missing a meal is inconvenient | Missing a meal is frightening |
| Food is one of several priorities | Food and body occupy most of your mental bandwidth |
| Rules can flex for context | Rules are absolute regardless of context |
| Eating "badly" is a shrug | Eating "badly" triggers compensation — restricting, extra cardio, purging |
| You'd stop if it were harming you | You've noticed harm and continued |
| Hunger informs your choices | Hunger is something to defeat |

**The compensation reflex is the clearest single signal.** "I ate the cake, so I'll
skip dinner / add 45 minutes of cardio / fast tomorrow." That transaction —
treating food as debt and exercise as repayment — is the mechanism at the centre
of most disordered eating.

## REDs — when under-fuelling becomes a medical problem

**Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (REDs)** is the syndrome that results when
**energy intake doesn't cover the demands of training plus basic physiology**. The
IOC's **2023 consensus statement** — updating the 2018 version after more than 170
new studies — is the current reference.

The crucial point: **REDs doesn't require an eating disorder, or any intent to
restrict.** A large number of cases are athletes who simply increased training
without increasing food. The body doesn't distinguish between deliberate
restriction and accidental under-fuelling. It just runs out of energy and starts
shutting down non-essential systems.

**What gets shut down:**

| System | Consequence |
| --- | --- |
| **Reproductive** | Loss of menstrual periods; **suppressed testosterone in men** |
| **Bone** | Reduced density, **stress fractures**, lifelong effects if it happens young |
| **Metabolic** | Lowered resting metabolic rate, thyroid suppression |
| **Immune** | More frequent and longer illness |
| **Cardiovascular** | Adverse lipid changes, bradycardia |
| **Psychological** | Depression, anxiety, irritability, obsessive food thoughts |
| **Performance** | Reduced strength, endurance, coordination, training response |

Two things are widely misunderstood:

- **It affects men too.** The old "female athlete triad" framing meant male
  athletes went unrecognised for decades. Suppressed testosterone, low bone
  density, and the full performance decline all occur in men.
- **You do not have to look underweight.** Athletes at entirely ordinary body
  composition develop REDs. The syndrome is about **energy availability**, not
  appearance — which is exactly why it gets missed.

**The signals to watch for:** menstrual periods becoming irregular or stopping
(never normal, never "just what happens when you train hard"), recurring stress
fractures or bone injuries, persistent illness, always feeling cold, loss of
libido, disrupted sleep, stalled or reversing performance despite training hard,
and mood changes.

If several of those are present, that's a **doctor's appointment**, not a
programming tweak.

## Orthorexia — the one that gets praised

**Orthorexia** — a pathological fixation on eating "correctly" — isn't a formal
diagnosis, but it describes something real and something this course could
plausibly contribute to.

It's distinctive because the behaviour is **socially rewarded**. Nobody
congratulates you for restricting calories, but they will absolutely congratulate
you for cutting out seed oils, refusing anything ultra-processed, or maintaining a
perfect streak. The restriction hides inside virtue.

Signs it's crossed over: the list of acceptable foods keeps shrinking; eating
something "impure" produces guilt or self-disgust rather than indifference; you
can't eat food you didn't prepare; nutritional purity has become part of your
identity; and — the telling one — **your health is measurably worse** (weight loss,
fatigue, social withdrawal, nutrient gaps) as a result of eating "perfectly."

## Muscle dysmorphia — the one men miss

Eating disorders in men are systematically under-diagnosed, partly because the
presentation is different. Rather than pursuing thinness, the drive is often
toward **muscularity** — sometimes called **muscle dysmorphia** or "bigorexia."

It looks like: never being big enough regardless of actual size; training through
injury and illness compulsively; extreme rigidity about protein timing and meal
structure; avoiding beaches, changing rooms, or anything requiring a shirt off;
distress when a session is missed; and escalating supplement use, sometimes into
performance-enhancing drugs.

This is the same underlying illness — body-image distortion driving compulsive,
harmful behaviour — pointed in a different direction. It's rarely recognised as a
problem by the person or the people around them, because it's indistinguishable
from "dedication" at a glance.

## Tracking — useful tool, common trap

The tracking-and-labels chapter covers the mechanics. Here's the part that belongs
in this chapter.

**Tracking is genuinely useful** for calibration — most people are poor at
estimating intake, and a few weeks of logging is educational. It's also, for a
meaningful minority, the thing that turns eating into a source of anxiety.

**Signals to stop tracking:**

- You feel anxious when you can't log something
- You avoid eating anything you can't measure
- You log first and eat second — the number decides, not hunger or hunger's absence
- You've continued logging long after learning what you needed to
- The number governs your mood for the day
- You've started reducing the target without deciding to

Tracking is a **temporary measuring instrument**, like using a food scale to learn
portion sizes. If you find yourself unable to stop, that's information — and it's
worth taking seriously rather than pushing through.

## Who's at higher risk

Not to label anyone, but because recognising elevated risk changes how carefully
you should tread:

- **Weight-class sports** (combat sports, rowing, weightlifting) and the culture
  of cutting
- **Aesthetic and leanness sports** — gymnastics, figure skating, dance,
  bodybuilding, endurance running
- A **history of dieting**, particularly starting young
- **Perfectionism and high achievement** — the traits that make people good at
  training are the same ones that drive this
- **Type 1 diabetes** (insulin manipulation carries specific, serious risk)
- Prior **anxiety, OCD, or depression**
- Environments where **body composition is discussed publicly** or weigh-ins are
  done in front of others

## If you're worried about someone else

- **Lead with what you've observed, not with their body.** "I've noticed you've
  been skipping lunch and you seem exhausted" lands very differently from anything
  about weight or appearance.
- **Don't comment on their body at all** — including compliments. "You look great"
  is one of the most common reinforcers of a developing eating disorder.
- **Expect defensiveness.** It's part of the illness, not a sign you were wrong or
  that you should drop it.
- **Don't try to be their treatment.** You can't nutrition-coach someone out of
  this. Your job is to care about them and help them reach someone qualified.
- **Keep including them.** Isolation makes everything worse; keep inviting them to
  things, including things involving food.
- **Follow up.** One conversation rarely does it. Persistent, low-pressure concern
  is what tends to work.

## Getting help in Sweden

You do not need to be "sick enough." Waiting for that is one of the more dangerous
features of these illnesses — **earlier treatment substantially improves
outcomes**.

- **1177** — call for healthcare advice and guidance on where to seek care.
  [1177.se](https://www.1177.se) has dedicated pages on ätstörningar, including
  material for family members.
- **Vårdcentralen** is a normal and appropriate first stop. Ask for a referral to
  a **specialistmottagning för ätstörningar**. Under 18, **BUP** is the route.
- **Frisk & Fri — Riksföreningen mot ätstörningar**
  ([friskfri.se](https://www.friskfri.se)), run by people with lived experience:
  - **Ätstörningslinjen — 020-20 80 18** (for you)
  - **Närståendelinjen — 0200-125 085** (for family and friends)
  - Chat and support email are also available; check the site for current opening
    hours.
- **SHEDO** ([shedo.se](https://www.shedo.se)) — eating disorders and self-harm,
  chat and email support.
- **Ätstörningszonen** — for those who identify as girls, roughly ages 10–25.
- **UMO** ([umo.se](https://www.umo.se)) — youth health information, and
  **ungdomsmottagningen** locally.

**Treatment works.** Full recovery is common, and it's much more likely the earlier
it starts. This is a treatable illness, not a character flaw.

## What this course is trying to do

An honest closing note.

The material in this course is meant to be used **in service of a life** — to
train better, feel better, and stay healthy for decades. It isn't meant to be the
main event. If following it is making you anxious, socially isolated, or
preoccupied, then it's doing the opposite of its job, and the correct response is
to **use less of it**, not more.

Good nutrition should give you **more** capacity for the rest of your life, not
consume it. If the numbers have started running you, put them down. They'll still
be here later.

## The takeaway

- **Disordered eating** is the large, common territory around clinical eating
  disorders — and the strongest predictor of developing one.
- The line isn't *what* you eat. It's **how much space it occupies** and **what
  happens when the plan breaks**. The **compensation reflex** — earning and repaying
  food with exercise or restriction — is the clearest warning sign.
- **REDs** is under-fuelling relative to training. It **requires no intent, affects
  men too, and doesn't require looking underweight.** Lost periods, stress
  fractures, and stalled performance are medical signals.
- **Orthorexia** hides inside praise; **muscle dysmorphia** hides inside
  dedication. Both are frequently missed.
- **Tracking is a temporary instrument.** Being unable to stop is meaningful
  information.
- If you're worried about someone: **talk about behaviour, never their body**, and
  help them reach someone qualified.
- **You don't need to be sick enough to ask for help** — in Sweden, start with
  **1177**, your **vårdcentral**, or **Frisk & Fri on 020-20 80 18**.
