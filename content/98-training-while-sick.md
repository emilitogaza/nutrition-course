# Training While Sick

Most illness advice in gyms is folklore. "Sweat it out" is bad advice that
occasionally kills people. "Never train sick" costs weeks of fitness for no
reason. The real answer depends almost entirely on **which illness** and
**whether it has reached your heart or lungs**.

> **This chapter is general education, not medical advice.** It exists to help you
> ask better questions and recognise danger, not to replace a doctor. If something
> is worrying you, call **1177** or see a physician.

## First, a myth to clear out

You've probably heard that hard training opens an **"open window"** of
immunosuppression for 3–72 hours afterwards, leaving you vulnerable to infection.
The theory rested on lymphocyte counts falling after exercise and salivary IgA
dropping.

**That interpretation has been substantially overturned.** Campbell and Turner's
2018 review in *Frontiers in Immunology* argued that the post-exercise fall in
circulating lymphocytes reflects **redistribution** of immune cells into
peripheral tissues — heightened immune *surveillance*, not suppression — and that
there's limited reliable evidence that vigorous exercise increases opportunistic
infection risk. Their conclusion: regular exercise **improves** immune competency
across the lifespan.

Athletes do get sick around heavy training blocks and competition. But the drivers
look like **travel, crowded venues, sleep loss, psychological stress, and low
energy availability** — not the training itself. That matters practically: the fix
is sleep, food, and hygiene, not training less.

## The neck check — useful, and oversold

The standard heuristic:

| Symptoms **above** the neck | Symptoms **below** the neck |
| --- | --- |
| Runny or blocked nose | Fever |
| Sneezing | Productive/chesty cough |
| Mild sore throat | Chest tightness, wheeze |
| → Light-to-moderate training is usually fine | Aching muscles, deep fatigue |
| | Vomiting, diarrhoea |
| | Swollen glands |
| | → **Do not train** |

This is a reasonable first filter and it's what most sports physicians use. But be
honest about its status: it is **a clinical rule of thumb with very little direct
evidence behind it**, and it has a specific failure mode.

**Some genuinely dangerous infections present entirely "above the neck."**
Streptococcal tonsillitis, adenoviral tonsillitis, enteroviral pharyngitis, and
early **glandular fever (EBV)** all look like a sore throat. Those are exactly the
infections where training can cause real harm.

So treat the neck check as a **filter, not a clearance**. A mild runny nose with no
other symptoms — fine. A sore throat severe enough to notice, with swollen glands
or any systemic feeling of illness — stop and get it looked at.

## Fever is a hard stop

If you have a **fever (≥ 38°C)**, you do not train. Not lightly, not "just
mobility." ACSM and NATA both caution explicitly against exercise with fever or
systemic symptoms. The reasons are cumulative:

- **Cardiac risk.** This is the one that matters, and it gets its own section
  below.
- **Thermoregulation is already compromised** — your set point is raised, and
  adding exercise heat load raises heat-illness risk.
- **Dehydration** from sweating and raised metabolic rate.
- **Protein breakdown.** Fever is strongly catabolic; training on top of it adds
  damage you cannot repair.
- **Reduced strength, coordination and reaction time** — meaningful injury risk in
  anything technical or loaded.

## The real danger: myocarditis

This is why the advice is conservative, and it deserves to be stated plainly.

Many ordinary viruses — including influenza, enteroviruses, adenovirus, parvovirus
B19, and SARS-CoV-2 — can infect **heart muscle**, causing **myocarditis**. Viral
myocarditis is one of the **leading causes of sudden cardiac death in young
athletes**.

The mechanism that makes exercise dangerous: in animal models, **exercise during
active viral myocarditis increases viral replication and worsens myocardial
damage**, and it raises the risk of malignant arrhythmia during the acute phase.
This is the specific reason "sweat it out" is dangerous advice rather than merely
unhelpful.

**Warning signs that need urgent medical attention** — during illness or in the
weeks after:

- **Chest pain or pressure**
- **Palpitations**, or a resting heart rate 10–20 bpm above your normal that
  persists
- **Breathlessness out of proportion** to the effort
- **Fainting or near-fainting**, particularly on exertion
- **Swelling** in the legs or ankles
- Fatigue that is dramatically worse than the illness seems to justify

If myocarditis is **confirmed**, return to sport is not a matter of feeling
better. Current ACC/AHA and ESC sports cardiology guidance calls for a **minimum
of 3–6 months of exercise restriction**, with return contingent on **imaging
(often cardiac MRI), biomarkers, and rhythm monitoring** clearing. That decision
belongs to a cardiologist.

## Illness by illness

| Illness | Train? | Key points |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Common cold** (no fever) | **Light–moderate OK** | Trials show no impairment or worsening with moderate exercise. Drop intensity, keep it short. |
| **Influenza** | **No** | Systemic, febrile, real myocarditis risk. Full rest, then graded return. |
| **COVID-19** | **No while symptomatic** | Rest until symptom-free, then **graded return over ~1 week+**. Any cardiopulmonary symptoms → medical assessment before returning. |
| **Gastroenteritis** | **No** | Dehydration and electrolyte loss dominate. Rehydrate first; strength returns quickly. |
| **Strep throat / tonsillitis** | **No** | "Above the neck" but bacterial and systemic. Needs antibiotics; rest until afebrile and treated. |
| **Glandular fever (EBV/mono)** | **No — and specifically no contact sport** | **Splenomegaly risks splenic rupture.** Avoid contact/collision sport for **at least 3–4 weeks** and until cleared. Fatigue can last months; return very gradually. |
| **Bronchitis / pneumonia** | **No** | Lower respiratory involvement. Needs full resolution and often medical review. |
| **Sinusitis** | **Case by case** | Mild and non-febrile — light training fine. Febrile or severe — rest. |
| **Skin infections** (impetigo, ringworm, herpes gladiatorum) | **No contact sport** | Transmission risk to teammates; mat sports have explicit exclusion rules. |
| **Hay fever / allergy** | **Yes** | Not an infection. Manage symptoms and train normally. |

### A note on antibiotics

**Fluoroquinolones** (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin) carry a recognised risk of
**tendinopathy and tendon rupture**, particularly the Achilles, and the risk
persists for weeks after the course. If you're prescribed one, tell the
prescriber you're a lifter or runner, and **avoid heavy loading and plyometrics**
during and for some weeks after. Most other antibiotic classes don't carry this
concern.

## Returning without relapsing

The most common mistake isn't training while sick — it's coming back at 100% two
days after a fever breaks, feeling flat for a fortnight, and calling it "post-viral
fatigue."

A workable protocol:

1. **Wait until you've been symptom-free and fever-free for 24–48 hours** without
   paracetamol or ibuprofen masking it.
2. **Day 1 back: roughly 50% of normal volume at low intensity.** Easy aerobic
   work or light technical lifting. The goal is to test the system, not train it.
3. **Add ~10–20% per session** if you feel normal the next morning.
4. **Use objective markers.** An elevated **resting heart rate** or suppressed
   **HRV** compared with your baseline is a reason to hold. So is feeling
   disproportionately wrecked by an easy session.
5. **Expect full return in roughly the duration of the illness** — a 3-day cold,
   3ish days back. Influenza or mono, considerably longer.
6. **Any cardiac symptom at any point → stop and get assessed.** This one is not
   negotiable.

**The reassuring part:** detraining is far slower than people fear. A week off
costs you almost nothing in strength or muscle — some glycogen and water, and a
little aerobic sharpness that returns quickly. There is no version of this where
resting a week meaningfully harms your training year. There are versions where
training through a viral illness ends it.

## Eating while ill

Illness raises energy and protein needs at exactly the moment appetite disappears.

- **Don't under-eat.** Fever raises resting metabolic rate by roughly **10–13% per
  °C**. Immune function and tissue repair are both energy-expensive. This is not
  the week to be in a deficit.
- **Protein matters more, not less** — illness is catabolic. Aim for your normal
  target; liquid sources (milk, kvarg smoothies, shakes) are easiest when
  appetite is gone.
- **Fluid and electrolytes**, especially with fever, vomiting, or diarrhoea. Oral
  rehydration solution beats plain water when losses are large.
- **Zinc lozenges** have modest evidence for shortening cold duration **if started
  within ~24 hours** of symptom onset. Doses used in trials are high and cause
  nausea and taste disturbance in some people.
- **Vitamin C** does **not** prevent colds in the general population, but regular
  supplementation may modestly shorten duration, with a clearer effect in people
  under heavy physical stress.
- **Vitamin D deficiency** is associated with more respiratory infections;
  correcting a deficiency helps, taking extra when you're already replete doesn't.
- **Honey** has decent evidence for suppressing cough — better than most
  over-the-counter options.

Everything else marketed for immunity — echinacea, elderberry, high-dose
multivitamins — sits somewhere between weak and null. Sleep and food do more.

## Prevention

The unglamorous list is the effective one:

- **Sleep.** Under ~6 hours substantially raises infection susceptibility in
  controlled inoculation studies. This is the strongest single lever.
- **Adequate energy availability.** Under-fuelling suppresses immune function —
  another cost of REDs.
- **Hand hygiene** and not sharing bottles/towels. Boring, effective.
- **Vaccination**, particularly influenza, if illness during a competitive season
  is costly to you.
- **Manage total load.** It's chronic under-recovery, not any single hard session,
  that correlates with illness.

## The takeaway

- The **"open window" of post-exercise immunosuppression is largely a myth** —
  regular training improves immune function. Illness clusters around **sleep loss,
  travel, stress, and under-fuelling**.
- The **neck check** is a useful filter, not a clearance — and it misses strep,
  adenovirus, and **glandular fever**, which all present as a sore throat.
- **Fever ≥ 38°C means no training.** No exceptions, no "light session."
- The reason is **myocarditis** — a leading cause of sudden cardiac death in young
  athletes, made worse by exercising during the acute viral phase. **Chest pain,
  palpitations, unexplained breathlessness, or fainting → seek care immediately.**
- **Glandular fever** needs specific care: **no contact sport for 3–4+ weeks**
  because of splenic rupture risk.
- Return at **~50% for a session**, build **10–20%** at a time, and use **resting
  HR** as a brake.
- **A week off costs you almost nothing.** Training through a viral illness can
  cost you everything.
