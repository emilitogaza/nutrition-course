# Tracking, Labels & Food Quality

You don't have to weigh every gram to eat well — but understanding what's on the
label and how tracking works gives you control when you want it. This chapter is
the practical toolkit.

## Reading a nutrition label

A few things trip people up:

- **Serving size sets everything.** Every number on the panel is *per serving*,
  and the serving on the package is often smaller than what you'd actually eat.
  Check it before you trust the calories.
- **"Calories from fat" and total macros should roughly reconcile.** Multiply
  protein and carbs by 4 and fat by 9; the sum should land near the stated
  calories. Big gaps usually mean fibre, sugar alcohols, or rounding.
- **Fibre is a carbohydrate but barely a calorie source.** Some labels (and most
  trackers) let you subtract fibre to get "net carbs." Useful for some, optional
  for most.
- **"Sugar-free" and "zero calorie" are legally allowed to round down.** Small
  amounts per serving can be labelled as zero. Rarely matters unless you're
  eating many servings.
- **Ingredients are listed by weight, descending.** If sugar (under any of its
  many names) is near the top, the food is mostly sugar.

## Tracking without losing your mind

Tracking is a **measurement tool**, not a lifestyle sentence. Its value is
*calibration*: most people dramatically under-estimate intake, and a week or two
of honest logging recalibrates your eye. Options, from most to least precise:

- **Weigh and log everything** — highest precision, best for cutting or research
  on yourself. A food scale beats measuring cups by a wide margin.
- **Track protein and calories only** — 80% of the benefit for a fraction of the
  effort. Protein is the macro most worth hitting on purpose.
- **Hand/plate portions** — palm of protein, fist of veg, cupped hand of carbs,
  thumb of fat. No numbers, surprisingly effective for maintenance.

Accuracy caveats worth knowing: label values carry a **legal tolerance of
around ±20%**, restaurant estimates are frequently low, and database entries are
crowd-sourced and error-prone. Tracking gets you *consistency and trend*, not
lab-grade truth — and the trend is what matters.

## "Food quality" without the moralising

There's no such thing as a good or bad food in isolation — only a diet that, on
balance, does or doesn't support your goals. That said, some foods make hitting
those goals much easier:

### Whole vs. processed — the useful distinction

The honest version of the "eat whole foods" advice isn't magic; it's mostly about
**calorie density and satiety**. Minimally processed foods — vegetables, fruit,
potatoes, legumes, lean meats, dairy — tend to be filling per calorie, rich in
micronutrients and fibre, and hard to overeat. Ultra-processed foods are
engineered to be *hyper-palatable* and calorie-dense, so they're easy to
over-consume. A tightly controlled inpatient trial (Hall et al., 2019) found
people ate ~500 kcal/day more on an ultra-processed diet than a
matched-macronutrient whole-food one, without being told to.

### The 80/20 principle

You don't need dietary perfection. A common and workable target: get **~80–90%
of calories from nutrient-dense, satiating whole foods**, and spend the rest on
whatever you enjoy. This covers micronutrient needs and fibre while keeping the
diet sustainable — and sustainability beats optimisation you can't maintain.

## The takeaway

- Labels are per-**serving**; the serving is usually smaller than you think.
- Tracking is for **calibration and trend**, not perfection — protein and
  calories are the highest-value things to log.
- "Quality" mostly means **satiety and micronutrient density**; build the base
  from whole foods and leave room for the rest.
