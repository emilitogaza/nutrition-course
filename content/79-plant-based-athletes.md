# Plant-Based & Vegan Athletes

Plant-based athletes compete at the highest level in essentially every sport, and
the performance evidence does not show a disadvantage. What it does show is that
a plant-based diet is **less forgiving of inattention**. An omnivore who eats
carelessly usually stumbles into adequacy. A vegan who eats carelessly can end up
short on four or five things at once.

This chapter is the checklist. Pieces of it appear across the course — protein
quality, B12, iron, creatine, omega-3 — and this is where they come together.

## First, the good news

Well-planned plant-based diets are associated with **lower blood pressure, lower
LDL, better insulin sensitivity, and lower cardiovascular risk**. They tend to be
high in **fibre, antioxidants, and polyphenols**, and the high carbohydrate
content suits endurance work well.

Studies comparing matched vegan and omnivorous athletes generally find
**equivalent performance and equivalent muscle growth** — provided total protein
and energy are matched. That proviso is the whole chapter.

## The protein maths

Three things stack against plant protein, and each one is individually small:

1. **Lower DIAAS.** Most plant proteins score 0.4–0.9 versus 1.0–1.2 for animal
   sources (see the protein quality chapter).
2. **Lower leucine.** Plant proteins run **6–8% leucine** versus whey's 10–11%.
   Leucine is the trigger for muscle protein synthesis, and there's a threshold to
   clear per meal.
3. **Lower digestibility.** Fibre and antifungal compounds in plant foods reduce
   how much of the protein you actually absorb — often by **5–10%**.

Compounded, the practical adjustment is:

> **Aim for roughly 20–30% more total protein than an omnivore would.** If the
> general recommendation is 1.6–2.2 g/kg, plant-based athletes should target the
> **upper half to upper end** — around **1.8–2.4 g/kg**.

### Per-meal dosing

Because leucine is lower, the per-meal dose has to be higher to clear the same
threshold:

| | Omnivore | Plant-based |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Protein per meal** | ~0.4 g/kg (~30 g) | **~0.5–0.6 g/kg (~40 g)** |
| **Leucine per meal** | ~2.5 g | ~2.5 g (needs more protein to reach) |
| **Meals per day** | 3–4 | **4–5** |

### Best plant protein sources

| Source | Protein per 100 g | Leucine | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Soy protein isolate** | ~90 g | ~8% | DIAAS ~0.9 — the best plant protein, and the cheapest |
| **Seitan (wheat gluten)** | ~25 g | ~7% | High protein but **low in lysine** — pair it |
| **Tempeh** | ~19 g | ~8% | Fermented, better digestibility than tofu |
| **Tofu (firm)** | ~12 g | ~8% | Complete, versatile |
| **Edamame** | ~11 g | ~8% | Complete, high leucine for a whole food |
| **Lentils** | ~9 g cooked | ~7% | Low in methionine — pair with grains |
| **Chickpeas** | ~9 g cooked | ~7% | Low in methionine |
| **Quinoa** | ~4.4 g cooked | ~6% | Complete but dilute |
| **Pea + rice blend (powder)** | ~80 g | ~8% | Deliberately complementary |

**Soy is the anchor.** It's the only common plant food that's genuinely complete,
well-digested, and cheap. The phytoestrogen concern has been examined repeatedly
in men and does not hold up at normal intakes — meta-analyses find no effect on
testosterone or oestrogen.

**Complementing works across the day, not the meal.** Grains are low in lysine,
legumes are low in methionine; eating both over 24 hours covers the gap. You do
not need to engineer every plate.

## The six nutrients that need attention

### 1. Vitamin B12 — non-negotiable

**B12 does not occur in plant foods** in a reliable, bioavailable form. Not in
seaweed, not in tempeh, not in nutritional yeast unless it's fortified. This isn't
an optimisation — a genuine B12 deficiency causes **irreversible neurological
damage**.

- **Supplement:** ~**50–250 µg/day**, or **2 000 µg once weekly**. Absorption is
  inefficient at high single doses, which is why the weekly dose looks so large.
- **Test:** serum B12 alone is unreliable. Ask for **methylmalonic acid (MMA)**
  or **holotranscobalamin** if you want a real answer.
- Liver stores last **years**, which is why deficiency often shows up long after
  the diet changed — and why "I've been fine for two years" means nothing.

### 2. Iron — the most common real deficiency

Plant iron is **non-heme**, absorbed at roughly **2–10%** versus **15–35%** for
heme iron from meat. Recommendations for vegetarians are therefore set at up to
**1.8× the standard intake**.

Athletes have it worse: **foot-strike haemolysis**, sweat losses, and
exercise-induced **hepcidin** elevation (which blocks absorption for hours after
hard sessions) all add up. Menstruating athletes are at highest risk of all.

**What actually moves absorption:**

| Boosts absorption | Blocks absorption |
| --- | --- |
| **Vitamin C** (can triple it) | **Tea and coffee** tannins |
| Organic acids in fruit/veg | **Calcium** (supplements and dairy) |
| Soaking, sprouting, fermenting | **Phytates** in grains and legumes |
| Cooking in cast iron | Excess **zinc** supplementation |

Practically: **lentils with capsicum and lemon, coffee an hour away from the
meal.** Don't supplement iron blind — get **ferritin** tested. Excess iron is
genuinely harmful, and unnecessary supplementation is a real risk.

### 3. Omega-3 (EPA/DHA)

Covered in depth in the omega-3 chapter, but the plant-based version is stark:
**ALA from flax, chia, and walnuts converts to EPA at under 8% and to DHA at
under 0.5%.** Plant-based athletes consistently show lower omega-3 index values.

- **Take an algal oil supplement** — **~250–500 mg EPA+DHA per day** as a floor,
  more if you're using it for recovery. Algae is where fish get it from in the
  first place, so this isn't a compromise.

### 4. Creatine

Creatine occurs **only in animal flesh**. Vegetarians and vegans run measurably
lower muscle creatine stores — and consequently show the **largest response to
supplementation**, both for strength and for cognition.

- **5 g/day of monohydrate.** For a plant-based athlete this is closer to a
  correction than a supplement. It's also vegan — commercial creatine is
  synthesised, not extracted from meat.

### 5. Zinc, iodine, and selenium

- **Zinc:** phytates reduce absorption substantially; vegetarian requirements run
  up to **50% higher**. Sources: legumes, seeds (especially pumpkin), whole
  grains, tofu. Soaking and fermenting help.
- **Iodine:** a genuine Nordic issue. In Sweden the main dietary sources are
  **iodised salt and dairy** — remove dairy and switch to unrefined sea salt (which
  is *not* iodised) and intake can fall sharply. Use **iodised salt**, or a
  supplement at ~150 µg/day. Seaweed is unreliable — kelp can deliver wildly
  excessive doses.
- **Selenium:** Nordic soils are selenium-poor, so plant foods grown here carry
  little. Brazil nuts are extremely dense (**1–2 nuts/day is enough** — more can
  approach toxicity).

### 6. Vitamin D and calcium

- **Vitamin D** is a whole-population issue at Swedish latitudes, not a vegan one —
  but plant-based diets remove oily fish and fortified dairy, two of the few food
  sources. Supplement **~10–20 µg (400–800 IU)/day** through the winter, more if
  tested low. Choose **D3 from lichen** for a vegan source; D2 is less effective at
  raising blood levels.
- **Calcium** intakes are frequently low once dairy goes. Vegans have shown higher
  fracture risk in cohort studies, largely tracking low calcium and low protein
  intake. Sources: **fortified plant milks** (shake the carton — the calcium
  settles), **calcium-set tofu**, kale, bok choy, almonds, fortified juices.
  Spinach is *not* a good source — its oxalates lock the calcium up.

## Two things that just get lower, and probably don't matter

- **Muscle carnosine** is lower in vegetarians because **beta-alanine** intake
  comes from meat. If you compete in the 1–4 minute high-intensity range, a
  beta-alanine supplement is more valuable to you than to an omnivore.
- **Taurine** and **carnitine** blood levels run lower. Neither has been shown to
  impair performance in healthy plant-based athletes. Not worth supplementing on
  current evidence.

## The energy density problem

This is the practical issue nobody warns about. Plant foods are **bulky**. A
plant-based athlete needing 3 500–4 500 kcal can hit genuine physical limits —
feeling stuffed while under-eating, which quietly becomes **low energy
availability** (see the disordered eating chapter — REDs doesn't care about your
intentions).

Countermeasures:

- Lean on **calorie-dense plant foods**: nuts, nut butters, seeds, olive oil,
  avocado, dried fruit, tahini.
- Use **liquid calories** — smoothies with oats, nut butter, and protein powder go
  down when solid food won't.
- **Reduce fibre selectively** around heavy training blocks. Refined grains have a
  role here.
- **Don't fear oil.** Adding 30 ml of olive oil to a meal is 270 kcal with no
  volume cost.

## The supplement shortlist

For a committed vegan athlete, these stop being optional:

| Supplement | Dose | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **B12** | 50–250 µg/day or 2 000 µg/week | **No plant source. Non-negotiable.** |
| **Vitamin D3 (lichen)** | 10–20 µg/day, winter | Latitude + removed food sources |
| **Algal EPA/DHA** | 250–500 mg/day | ALA conversion is too poor |
| **Creatine monohydrate** | 5 g/day | Zero dietary intake; largest responders |
| **Iodine** | ~150 µg/day, or iodised salt | Dairy and iodised salt removed |
| **Iron** | **Only if tested low** | Real deficiency risk — but don't guess |
| **Zinc** | Consider if intake is low | Phytate-driven absorption losses |

## Getting bloodwork

The single most useful thing a plant-based athlete can do is **test rather than
assume**, roughly annually:

**Ferritin** (plus haemoglobin), **B12 with MMA or holotranscobalamin**, **25(OH)
vitamin D**, and — if you can get it — an **omega-3 index**. Ferritin and vitamin
D are routinely available in Swedish primary care; explain that you're a
plant-based athlete and you're generally taken seriously.

## The takeaway

- Plant-based athletes perform **equivalently** — the diet is just **less
  forgiving**.
- Target **1.8–2.4 g/kg protein** and **~40 g per meal** across **4–5 meals** to
  offset lower leucine and digestibility. **Soy is the anchor.**
- **B12 is non-negotiable.** No plant source exists, and deficiency causes
  irreversible nerve damage.
- **Iron** is the most common real deficiency — pair with vitamin C, keep coffee
  away from meals, and **test ferritin rather than supplementing blind**.
- **Algal omega-3** and **creatine** move from optional to strongly recommended;
  vegans are the **biggest responders** to creatine.
- Watch the **Nordic-specific gaps**: iodine (iodised salt), selenium (poor soils),
  vitamin D (latitude).
- The under-recognised risk is **energy density** — bulky food making it hard to
  eat enough. Use fats, liquids, and selectively refined carbs.
- **Test annually**: ferritin, B12 (with MMA), vitamin D.
