# Amino Acids & Muscle Protein Synthesis

"Protein" is a convenient label, but your body doesn't build muscle from protein
— it builds it from **amino acids**, the twenty building blocks that protein
breaks down into. Understanding them explains most of the practical rules from the
last chapter, and clears up a lot of supplement confusion.

## Essential vs. non-essential

Of the twenty amino acids, **nine are essential (EAAs)** — you must eat them,
because your body can't synthesise them. The rest are "non-essential" only in the
sense that you can make them yourself.

| Essential (must eat) | Conditionally essential | Non-essential |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Leucine, isoleucine, valine (the **BCAAs**), lysine, methionine, phenylalanine, threonine, tryptophan, histidine | Arginine, glutamine, glycine, proline, tyrosine, cysteine (essential when ill, stressed, or growing fast) | Alanine, aspartate, asparagine, glutamate, serine |

The EAAs are what drive the muscle-building signal. Non-essential amino acids
matter for the finished tissue but rarely limit it, because supply is plentiful.

## Muscle protein synthesis and the leucine trigger

Muscle is in constant turnover: **muscle protein synthesis (MPS)** builds new
tissue, **muscle protein breakdown (MPB)** tears old tissue down. You gain muscle
when, summed over time, MPS exceeds MPB — the **net protein balance**.

Eating protein flips balance positive by spiking MPS. The switch that flips it is
largely **leucine**, which activates the **mTOR** signalling pathway — the
cell's "build muscle now" sensor. There appears to be a **leucine threshold**:
a meal needs roughly **2–3 g of leucine** to fully trigger the MPS response.

This is why per-meal *dose* matters, and why *quality* matters: you're really
trying to hit a leucine threshold with a full complement of the other EAAs behind
it to supply the construction.

### Anabolic resistance

With **age** (and to a degree, inactivity), muscle becomes less responsive to a
given dose of protein — **anabolic resistance**. Older adults need a *bigger*
leucine hit (and more total protein, ~1.2–1.6 g/kg minimum) to mount the same
response. Resistance training is the most powerful counter to it.

## Why BCAA supplements underdeliver

BCAA supplements (leucine, isoleucine, valine) were marketed hard on the leucine
story — but they leave out the other six EAAs. You can *signal* "build muscle"
with leucine all you like; if the other essential building blocks aren't
available, there's little to actually build with, so the extra MPS can't be
sustained.

- Studies giving BCAAs alone show a **blunted, incomplete** MPS response versus
  whole protein or full EAAs.
- If you already eat enough total protein, BCAAs add essentially nothing.
- **EAAs** (all nine) are more defensible than BCAAs, but for anyone hitting
  their protein target from food or whey, they're redundant.

The verdict: **whole protein beats BCAAs**, and adequate daily protein makes both
supplements unnecessary for most people.

## The takeaway

- Muscle is built from **amino acids**; the **nine EAAs** are the ones you must
  eat.
- **Leucine** triggers muscle protein synthesis via **mTOR**; a meal wants
  ~**2–3 g of leucine** to max the response.
- You grow when **MPS > MPB** over time — favoured by enough protein, training,
  and adequate energy.
- **BCAAs are a poor buy**; whole protein or full EAAs supply the whole toolkit.
