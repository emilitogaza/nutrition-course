# Omega-3 — A Deep Dive

The dietary fat chapter introduced omega-3s as the fat most worth paying
attention to. This chapter goes properly into it — because omega-3 is
simultaneously one of the **best-evidenced** nutrients for some outcomes and one
of the **most oversold** for others, and the difference is entirely in the
details.

## The three omega-3s aren't interchangeable

| Fatty acid | Chain | Source | What it does |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **ALA** (alpha-linolenic) | 18 carbons | Flax, chia, walnuts, rapeseed | The *essential* one — but mostly burned for fuel |
| **EPA** (eicosapentaenoic) | 20 carbons | Fatty fish, algae | Anti-inflammatory signalling, cardiovascular, mood |
| **DHA** (docosahexaenoic) | 22 carbons | Fatty fish, algae | Structural — brain, retina, cell membranes |

Only **ALA** is strictly essential, because the body can't make it. In principle
the body converts ALA into EPA and then DHA. In practice, that pathway is a
bottleneck:

- **ALA → EPA:** roughly **5–8%** conversion
- **ALA → DHA:** typically **under 0.5%** (somewhat higher in women, likely an
  oestrogen effect that supports pregnancy)

The enzymes involved (delta-6 desaturase, elongases) are the **same enzymes** that
process omega-6 linoleic acid, so a high omega-6 intake competes for them and
pushes conversion lower still.

**The practical consequence:** flax, chia, and walnuts are good foods, but they
are **not a substitute for fish**. If you eat no marine omega-3, no realistic
amount of flaxseed will fix your EPA and DHA status.

## Measuring status: the omega-3 index

Unlike most nutrients, omega-3 status has a decent biomarker. The **omega-3
index** measures EPA + DHA as a percentage of total fatty acids in red blood cell
membranes — a stable, ~4-month average rather than a snapshot of yesterday's
dinner.

| Index | Category |
| --- | --- |
| **< 4%** | High risk — typical of low-fish Western diets |
| **4–8%** | Intermediate |
| **> 8%** | Desirable — typical of Japanese and Nordic high-fish populations |

Most people eating a standard Western diet land around **4–5%**. It takes roughly
**3–4 months** of consistent intake to move the index, which is one reason short
supplement trials so often come back null.

## What the big cardiovascular trials actually found

This is where the story gets genuinely complicated, and where most articles
either oversell or dismiss the evidence. Three landmark trials, three different
answers:

**VITAL (2019)** — 25,871 adults, ~840 mg/day EPA+DHA, 5.3 years. The primary
composite cardiovascular endpoint was **not significantly reduced**. But **total
myocardial infarction fell ~28%**, and the benefit was **substantially larger in
people who ate little fish at baseline** and in Black participants. Read
carefully, VITAL suggests supplements help people who are *deficient* — not
everyone.

**REDUCE-IT (2019)** — 8,179 statin-treated patients with high triglycerides,
given **4 g/day of icosapent ethyl** (purified EPA only). A striking **25%
relative reduction** in major cardiovascular events. This is the strongest
positive omega-3 result ever produced. The caveat is real, though: the placebo was
**mineral oil**, and the placebo group's LDL and CRP rose, so part of the apparent
benefit may be the comparator looking worse rather than the drug looking better.

**STRENGTH (2020)** — 13,078 patients, **4 g/day of EPA+DHA**, stopped early for
futility. **No benefit.** The main structural difference from REDUCE-IT: the
inclusion of DHA, and a corn-oil placebo.

The honest synthesis: **high-dose purified EPA in high-risk, statin-treated
patients has real evidence.** A general 1 g fish oil capsule for an otherwise
healthy person who already eats fish has **little to no demonstrated event
benefit**. Where it does help is in the under-consumers.

### The one genuine safety signal: atrial fibrillation

This deserves attention because it's rarely mentioned on supplement labels. A
2021 *Circulation* meta-analysis of cardiovascular-outcome trials found omega-3
treatment raised the risk of **atrial fibrillation by about 25%**.

The picture has since become more nuanced. An updated meta-analysis of **34
trials and over 114,000 participants** found the effect is **dose-dependent and
concentrated in high-cardiovascular-risk patients on high doses** (above
~1 500 mg/day EPA+DHA). Meanwhile, biomarker studies of 17 prospective cohorts
found that people with **higher circulating omega-3** actually had **lower**
rates of atrial fibrillation — suggesting low-to-moderate intakes may be
protective, and only pharmacological doses carry the risk.

> **The practical rule.** Doses at or below **~1–2 g/day of EPA+DHA** carry no
> meaningful AF signal. If you're considering **3–4 g/day**, that's a
> pharmacological dose — do it with a doctor, especially with existing heart
> disease.

### Triglycerides — the least ambiguous benefit

Whatever the debate about hard endpoints, one effect is robust and
dose-dependent: **3–4 g/day of EPA+DHA lowers fasting triglycerides by roughly
20–30%.** This is well established enough that prescription omega-3 exists for
exactly this purpose.

## Omega-3 and muscle

Here the evidence is genuinely **mixed**, and this course won't pretend
otherwise.

A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis in *Nutrition Reviews* examining
omega-3 and **muscle protein synthesis** found **inconsistent effects** on both
basal and stimulus-induced MPS, with the heterogeneity largely explained by
methodological differences. The signal is clearest in **clinical and older
populations** — where mechanistic work suggests omega-3 improves the muscle's
**anabolic sensitivity** to protein and resistance exercise, partly by altering
membrane phospholipid composition.

In **older adults specifically**, meta-analyses of omega-3 combined with
resistance training show **small but consistent gains** in muscle mass and
neuromuscular function versus training alone. In **young, healthy, well-fed
lifters already hitting their protein target**, the added benefit is small at
best.

**Interpretation:** treat omega-3 as a **health and recovery** supplement that may
help muscle at the margins — most usefully after ~50, or when protein intake is
imperfect. It is not a hypertrophy agent.

## Omega-3 and recovery

This is the strongest athletic case. A 2026 meta-analysis in *The FASEB Journal*
pooled **41 human trials from 2011–2025** on omega-3 and post-exercise recovery,
and found **significant, moderate reductions** in:

- **Interleukin-6** and **TNF-α** (inflammatory cytokines)
- **Creatine kinase** (a marker of muscle damage)
- **Delayed-onset muscle soreness (DOMS)**

Most of the effective protocols used **~2 g/day of EPA+DHA for at least six
weeks** — note the *chronic* dosing; this is a saturation effect, not something
you take after a hard session.

The honest caveat: **improved biomarkers have not consistently translated into
improved performance.** You will likely feel less beaten up. Whether you jump
higher is unproven.

There's also a theoretical concern that blunting post-exercise inflammation could
blunt adaptation, in the way high-dose antioxidants appear to. At the doses
above, this has **not** been observed — but it's a reason not to escalate to very
high doses "just in case."

## Brain, mood, and everything else

- **Depression:** meta-analyses support a modest adjunctive benefit for
  **EPA-predominant** formulations (≥60% EPA) at **1–2 g/day**. DHA-dominant
  products do not show the same effect. This is one of the few places the
  EPA/DHA ratio genuinely matters.
- **Cognitive decline:** results are mixed. Trials in people who are already
  cognitively impaired mostly fail; trials in the deficient with good baseline
  cognition are more promising. Prevention looks more plausible than treatment.
- **Pregnancy:** DHA matters for fetal brain and retinal development, and this is
  the one context where a **DHA-forward** supplement is clearly indicated —
  typically ~200–300 mg DHA/day on top of a normal diet.
- **Joint pain:** consistent modest benefit in rheumatoid arthritis; weaker for
  general aches.

## The omega-6 ratio — what changed

For twenty years the advice was to fix your **omega-6 to omega-3 ratio**, on the
theory that linoleic acid drives inflammation. That framing has weakened
considerably.

Large prospective cohorts consistently find that **higher linoleic acid intake
and higher circulating linoleic acid are associated with *lower* cardiovascular
risk and lower type 2 diabetes risk** — the opposite of the prediction. The
conversion of linoleic acid to inflammatory arachidonic acid in humans turns out
to be tightly regulated and not very responsive to intake.

**The modern position:** what matters is your **absolute EPA+DHA intake**, not the
ratio. Raising omega-3 is useful. Aggressively cutting seed oils to improve a
ratio is chasing a number, not an outcome.

## Food sources — how much EPA+DHA you actually get

| Food | Portion | EPA + DHA |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Mackerel** | 100 g | **~2.5–5 g** |
| **Farmed salmon** | 100 g | ~2.0–2.5 g |
| **Herring / sill** | 100 g | ~2 g |
| **Mackerel in tomato sauce (tin)** | 1 tin (~375 g) | **~2.5 g** |
| **Sardines** | 100 g | ~1.5 g |
| **Trout** | 100 g | ~1 g |
| **Canned tuna in water** | 100 g | ~0.3 g |
| **Cod / white fish** | 100 g | ~0.2 g |
| **Shrimp** | 100 g | ~0.3 g |
| **Eggs (regular)** | 2 | ~0.05 g |
| **Flax, chia, walnuts** | any | **0 g** (ALA only) |

Two servings of genuinely fatty fish a week averages out to roughly **500–700 mg
of EPA+DHA per day** — which is the target most health authorities converge on.
Note how far **canned tuna and white fish** fall below salmon and mackerel; tuna
is an excellent protein food and a poor omega-3 food.

### The Swedish fish advice

Livsmedelsverket recommends eating **fish 2–3 times a week, varying the type**.
There is one important exception:

**Herring/strömming from the Baltic Sea and Gulf of Bothnia, and wild-caught
salmon from the Baltic, Gulf of Bothnia, Vänern, Vättern and their rivers** can
carry high levels of **dioxins and PCBs**. For these specific fish:

- **Children, young people, and anyone pregnant, breastfeeding, or planning a
  pregnancy:** at most **2–3 times per year**.
- **Everyone else:** at most **once a week**.

Most herring sold in Swedish supermarkets is *not* from the Baltic, and most
salmon sold is farmed or caught in other waters — so the advice affects
locally-caught and specifically-labelled fish rather than the standard grocery
shop.

## Choosing a supplement without wasting money

If you don't eat fish 2–3 times a week, a supplement is reasonable. Four things
determine whether it's any good:

**1. Read the EPA+DHA number, not the capsule size.** A "1000 mg fish oil"
capsule typically contains only **~300 mg of EPA+DHA** — the rest is other fats.
Hitting 2 g/day from that product means **six or seven capsules**, not one. This
single misunderstanding is why so many people supplement at a fraction of the
dose they think they're taking.

**2. Form affects absorption.**

| Form | Absorption | Note |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Triglyceride (TG/rTG)** | Best | ~50% better absorbed than ethyl ester |
| **Ethyl ester (EE)** | Lower | Cheapest; take with a fatty meal |
| **Phospholipid (krill)** | Very good | Well absorbed, but low EPA+DHA per capsule and expensive per gram |
| **Algal oil** | Good | The vegan option — and where fish get it from originally |

**3. Oxidation is the hidden quality problem.** Long-chain omega-3s are extremely
prone to going rancid, and independent testing has repeatedly found products on
the market exceeding recommended oxidation limits (**TOTOX**). Rancid fish oil is
not just useless — oxidised lipids are plausibly harmful.

Practical checks: buy from **high-turnover retailers**, prefer products with a
**third-party oxidation certificate** (IFOS, or a published TOTOX value), **store
it in the fridge**, and treat **fishy repeats or a strong smell on opening** as a
sign the product is oxidised, not as normal. A little added vitamin E
(tocopherol) as an antioxidant is a good sign.

**4. Take it with food.** Absorption of EPA and DHA rises substantially when
taken with a fat-containing meal — a meaningful difference for ethyl ester
products in particular.

## Dosing summary

| Goal | EPA+DHA per day |
| --- | --- |
| **General health minimum** | 250–500 mg |
| **Matching the fish-eating populations** | ~500–1 000 mg |
| **Athletes: recovery, inflammation** | **~2 g** (chronic, ≥6 weeks) |
| **Pregnancy (DHA-forward)** | ~200–300 mg DHA on top of diet |
| **Depression (EPA-predominant)** | 1–2 g, ≥60% EPA |
| **Triglyceride lowering** | 3–4 g — **medical supervision** |

Blood thinning at typical doses is a **theoretical** concern more than a
practical one, but tell your doctor you take it before surgery, and discuss it if
you're on anticoagulants.

## The takeaway

- **EPA and DHA are the active forms.** ALA from flax and chia converts too
  poorly to replace them — under 8% to EPA and under 1% to DHA.
- The **omega-3 index** (> 8% desirable) is the status marker, and takes **3–4
  months** to shift.
- Cardiovascular evidence is **conditional**, not universal: strong for
  **high-dose EPA in high-risk patients**, real for **low-fish-eaters**, weak for
  well-fed people already eating fish.
- The **atrial fibrillation signal** is real but **dose-dependent** — a reason to
  stay at or below ~2 g/day unless supervised.
- Best athletic case is **recovery**: ~2 g/day for ≥6 weeks reduces CK, IL-6,
  TNF-α, and soreness. Effects on **muscle protein synthesis are inconsistent**,
  and clearest in **older adults**.
- Forget the **omega-6 ratio**; raise absolute EPA+DHA instead.
- **Fatty fish 2–3×/week** covers it — with Livsmedelsverket's exception for
  Baltic herring and wild Baltic/Vänern/Vättern salmon.
- If supplementing: check the **EPA+DHA number**, prefer **triglyceride form**,
  guard against **oxidation**, and take it **with food**.
