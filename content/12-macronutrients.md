# The Macronutrients

Everything you eat that carries energy is one of three **macronutrients** —
protein, carbohydrate, or fat — plus alcohol, which is its own category. "Macro"
because you need them in gram-to-hundreds-of-grams quantities, versus the
milligram-and-microgram **micronutrients** (vitamins and minerals) covered later.

## Energy density

Each macro carries a fixed amount of energy per gram:

| Macronutrient | Calories per gram |
| --- | --- |
| Protein | 4 kcal |
| Carbohydrate | 4 kcal |
| Fat | 9 kcal |
| Alcohol | 7 kcal |

Fat is the most energy-dense, which is why fatty foods add up fast and why fat is
the body's preferred long-term storage form. These numbers (the *Atwater
factors*) are averages — fibre yields less, for example — but they're accurate
enough for planning.

## What each one does

### Protein — the structural and functional macro

Protein is built from **amino acids**, and it's the raw material for muscle,
enzymes, hormones, antibodies, and connective tissue. Unlike carbs and fat, the
body has **no dedicated storage depot** for amino acids — so a steady daily
supply matters. Protein is also the most satiating macro and the hardest to
convert to body fat. For anyone training, it's the macro to anchor first. The
next section is devoted to it.

### Carbohydrate — the performance fuel

Carbs break down to glucose, stored as **glycogen** in muscle and liver, and are
the dominant fuel for moderate-to-high-intensity effort. They are technically
"non-essential" — the body can make glucose from protein and fat via
gluconeogenesis — but for anyone doing hard training, carbohydrate availability
is one of the strongest levers on performance. More on this in Carbohydrates &
Performance.

### Fat — structure, hormones, and slow-burn energy

Dietary fat builds cell membranes, carries the fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K),
and supplies the substrate for steroid hormones including testosterone. Two fats
are **essential** — you must eat them because your body can't make them:
omega-6 (linoleic acid) and omega-3 (alpha-linolenic acid). Fat is also the main
fuel at rest and during low-intensity work.

## How the body prioritises them

When energy is plentiful, the body burns a mix and stores the rest. When energy
is short, it draws on stores in a rough order of preference:

1. **Carbohydrate** first for anything intense — glycogen is fast-access fuel.
2. **Fat** for lower-intensity and resting metabolism — a vast reserve.
3. **Protein** is spared where possible; the body is reluctant to burn its own
   tissue, which is exactly why eating enough protein and lifting weights matters
   in a deficit (see the fat-loss chapters).

## Setting macros: a starting framework

A simple, evidence-aligned order of operations:

1. **Protein first** — roughly 1.6–2.2 g per kg of body weight (detailed next).
2. **Fat next** — enough for health and hormones, usually ≥ 0.5–1 g/kg.
3. **Carbohydrate fills the rest** — scaled up or down with training demand and
   whether you're gaining, maintaining, or losing.

This "protein and fat as floors, carbs as the flexible remainder" approach is the
backbone of most sports-nutrition programming. The chapters ahead put real
numbers on each.
