# Women, Masters & Youth

Most of this course states principles that apply to nearly everyone: energy
balance, protein, training, sleep. But a few groups need the general advice
*adjusted* — not replaced. Much sports-nutrition research has historically been
done on young men, so this chapter deliberately fills the gaps for women, older
athletes, and youth.

## Women

### The menstrual cycle

Hormones fluctuate across the cycle, and it's often assumed this dictates how
women should train and eat. The evidence is more measured. A large meta-analysis
(McNulty et al., 2020) found that, **on average**, menstrual-cycle phase has only
a **trivial-to-small effect on performance** — but with **wide variation between
individuals**. So the practical rule isn't a rigid phase-based protocol; it's to
**track your own symptoms and energy** and adjust where *you* notice a pattern
(e.g. higher perceived effort or appetite premenstrually).

Two nutrition points are solid:

- **Iron.** Menstrual blood loss makes women the highest-risk group for iron
  deficiency — the single most common athletic shortfall (see the micronutrients
  chapter). **Test ferritin** rather than guessing.
- **Energy availability.** Women are more prone to **Low Energy Availability** and
  **RED-S** — under-fuelling that disrupts the menstrual cycle, bone, and
  hormones. A missing or irregular period in an active woman is a **red flag for
  under-eating**, not a badge of leanness. Fix total energy first.

### Pregnancy

Nutrient needs rise, and several are non-negotiable — **folate** (before and in
early pregnancy, to prevent neural-tube defects), **iron**, **iodine**, and
**omega-3 DHA**. Some things must be limited or avoided: **alcohol** (none is the
safe default), **high-dose vitamin A / retinol**, and **excess caffeine**. This is
firmly a **work-with-your-physician** area — the point here is only that pregnancy
changes the rules, not that this course should set them.

### Menopause

The drop in oestrogen accelerates **muscle and bone loss** and shifts fat storage.
The countermeasures are the ones this course already champions, now more urgent:

- **Prioritise protein** at the higher end of the range and **train against
  resistance** — the strongest defence against age- and menopause-related muscle
  and bone loss.
- **Calcium and vitamin D** matter more for bone as protective oestrogen falls.

## Masters (older) athletes

Ageing brings **anabolic resistance** (introduced in the amino-acids chapter):
muscle responds less to a given dose of protein. The adjustments:

- **More total protein.** Guidelines for older adults recommend **~1.2–1.6 g/kg**
  and up, higher than the general adult figure and well above the RDA (Bauer et
  al., 2013, the PROT-AGE consensus).
- **A bigger per-meal dose.** The per-meal threshold rises with age — older adults
  need closer to **~0.4 g/kg (often ~35–40 g)** of high-quality, leucine-rich
  protein per meal to fully trigger synthesis.
- **Resistance training is the master switch.** It's the most effective counter to
  anabolic resistance and to sarcopenia (age-related muscle loss) — nutrition
  supports it but can't replace it.
- **Vitamin D** and adequate **calcium** for bone and muscle become higher
  priorities.

## Youth & adolescent athletes

Young athletes are still **growing**, which changes the priorities entirely:

- **Fuel growth first.** Adequate **total energy** and a genuinely varied
  whole-food diet matter far more than optimisation. Restrictive dieting during
  adolescence risks stunting development and seeding **disordered eating** and
  **RED-S** — real dangers in youth sport.
- **Calcium and vitamin D** build the peak bone mass that has to last a lifetime;
  adolescence is the key window.
- **Skip the supplement aisle.** Performance supplements are studied in adults,
  not children; the default for young athletes is **food, sleep, and coaching**,
  not creatine or caffeine. Caffeine in particular is best minimised in this
  group.
- **Coaches and parents should watch for under-fuelling** — growth plus training
  is a large energy demand that's easy to under-meet.

## The takeaway

- **Cycle:** average performance effects are small but individual; **track your
  own** pattern. Guard **iron** and **energy availability** — a lost period signals
  under-eating.
- **Pregnancy:** needs rise (**folate, iron, iodine, DHA**); some things are
  limited — a **physician-guided** area.
- **Menopause & masters:** fight muscle and bone loss with **higher protein,
  bigger per-meal doses, resistance training, and vitamin D / calcium**;
  anabolic resistance means older athletes need *more*, not less.
- **Youth:** **fuel growth**, keep food varied, protect bone, and **skip
  performance supplements** — watch for under-fuelling.
