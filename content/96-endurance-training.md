# Building Endurance — Adaptations & Training

Endurance is the most trainable quality in the body — the adaptations are large,
reliable, and touch nearly every system. This chapter covers **what changes** when
you train aerobically, **how to train** to drive those changes, and how it ties
back to the fuelling chapters that make it all run.

## What adapts when you train endurance

Consistent aerobic training remodels you from the heart outward:

- **The heart.** Stroke volume rises — the chamber enlarges and fills more
  completely (**eccentric hypertrophy**), so each beat pumps more blood. Result: a
  **lower resting and submaximal heart rate**, and a higher **cardiac output** ceiling
  (the central half of VO₂ max).
- **The blood.** **Plasma volume** expands within days to weeks, and over months you
  build **more red blood cells and haemoglobin** — more oxygen-carrying capacity per
  litre.
- **The muscle.** This is where much of the magic is: **more mitochondria**
  (mitochondrial biogenesis — the aerobic engines), **more capillaries** feeding
  each fibre, more **myoglobin**, and up-regulated **fat-oxidation enzymes**. This
  is the *peripheral* half of VO₂ max — and the main driver of a higher **lactate
  threshold** and better **economy**.
- **Fuel use.** A trained endurance athlete burns a **greater proportion of fat**
  at any given pace, sparing precious glycogen for when it's really needed (see
  Carbohydrates & Performance).

Together these raise your **VO₂ max**, lift your **threshold**, improve your
**economy**, and build **fatigue resistance** — the three-part performance package
from the VO₂ max chapter.

### A note on fibre types

Muscle fibres run from **Type I** (slow-twitch: fatigue-resistant, densely
mitochondrial, aerobic) to **Type II** (fast-twitch: powerful, glycolytic, quick to
tire). Endurance training pushes fibres toward a more **oxidative, fatigue-resistant
profile** (especially the adaptable Type IIa), while sprint/strength work develops
the fast end. Your genetic fibre mix sets a ceiling, but how you train shifts a lot
within it.

## How to train it: mostly easy, sometimes very hard

The best-supported way to structure endurance training is **polarised** — often
called the **80/20** model:

- **~80% of your training is genuinely easy** (Zone 1–2), below the point where you
  can still hold a conversation.
- **~20% is hard** (Zone 4–5), around or above threshold.
- **Little time is spent in the moderate "grey zone" (Zone 3)** — hard enough to be
  tiring, not hard enough to drive top-end adaptation.

This wasn't invented in a lab — Stephen Seiler **measured** what elite endurance
athletes across cycling, running, rowing, and skiing actually do, and it clustered
around this distribution. Controlled trials back it up: polarised blocks produced
**greater VO₂ max and endurance gains** than threshold-heavy or purely
high-intensity programs (Stöggl & Sperlich, 2014).

The most common amateur mistake is the inverse — **too many medium-hard sessions**:
easy days done too hard, hard days not hard enough, everything blurring into a
fatiguing middle that builds neither a big aerobic base nor real top-end power.

### The toolbox

- **Zone 2 / easy steady state** — the foundation. Builds mitochondria, capillaries,
  plasma volume, and fat oxidation, with low fatigue cost so you can do a lot of it.
  This is the aerobic base most people under-do.
- **Threshold / tempo intervals** — sustained "comfortably hard" efforts to push
  the lactate threshold up.
- **VO₂ max intervals** — hard 3–5 minute efforts (with recovery) to raise the
  ceiling.
- **Sprints / anaerobic work** — short, near-maximal efforts for the glycolytic and
  phosphagen systems and power.

Progressive overload and recovery apply exactly as in the training chapters:
build volume or intensity gradually, and let adaptation happen **between** sessions,
not during them (recall the sleep and recovery chapter).

## Endurance and strength together

Many people want both. Two practical notes on **concurrent training**:

- There's a modest **interference effect** — very high endurance volume can blunt
  strength/power gains (and hard lifting can cost endurance sessions) if they
  compete for recovery.
- It's manageable: **separate hard sessions** (by hours or days), keep easy
  endurance genuinely easy, prioritise whichever quality matters most for your goal,
  and eat and sleep enough to recover both.

## Fuelling the engine

Endurance is where nutrition earns its keep, and the relevant chapters already lay
out the specifics — pulled together here:

- **Carbohydrate scales with training load** (Carbohydrates & Performance): more on
  long/hard days, less on easy ones.
- **Fuel long efforts *during* them** (Fuelling Around Training): ~30–60 g carbs/hour
  beyond ~60–90 minutes, up to ~90 g/h with glucose+fructose mixes for the longest
  work — one of the highest-impact strategies in all of endurance.
- **Hydration and sodium** matter more as sessions get long and hot (Hydration &
  Electrolytes), and remember **cardiac drift** makes heart rate climb as you
  dehydrate.
- **Iron** is a common shortfall in endurance athletes, especially menstruating and
  plant-based ones (Micronutrients That Matter) — low iron quietly tanks endurance.

## The takeaway

- Endurance training remodels the **heart** (bigger stroke volume, lower heart
  rate), **blood** (more plasma and haemoglobin), and **muscle** (more mitochondria
  and capillaries, more fat-burning) — raising **VO₂ max, threshold, and economy**.
- Train **polarised / 80-20**: mostly **easy Zone 2** to build the base, a slice of
  **genuinely hard** work to lift the ceiling — and avoid living in the medium
  "grey zone" (Seiler; Stöggl & Sperlich, 2014).
- **Progressive overload + recovery** still rule; adaptation happens between
  sessions.
- **Fuel it** with carbohydrate scaled to load, **in-session carbs** for long
  efforts, hydration/sodium, and enough **iron** — the endurance-specific nutrition
  from earlier chapters.
