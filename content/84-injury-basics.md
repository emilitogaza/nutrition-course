# Understanding Injury — A Field Guide

Train long enough and something will eventually complain. Most of it is minor and
self-limiting; a little of it is serious; and the hard part is telling which is
which. This section builds that judgment. It's not a substitute for a clinician —
it's a map so you can act sensibly, know what's likely happening, and recognise the
few situations that need a professional *today*.

> **This is education, not diagnosis.** Nothing here can assess *your* body. If an
> injury is severe, not improving, or comes with the red-flag signs below, see a
> physiotherapist or physician.

## Two ways tissue gets hurt

Almost every training injury is one of two stories:

- **Acute (macrotrauma).** One identifiable moment — a rep, a sprint, a landing —
  overloads a tissue past its limit. A pulled hamstring, a rolled ankle, a "tweak"
  mid-lift. You usually know exactly when it happened.
- **Overuse (accumulated microtrauma).** No single moment — load applied faster
  than the tissue adapts, over weeks. Tendinopathy, stress fractures, most
  "-itis" complaints. The cause is almost always **too much, too soon, too often,
  with too little recovery.**

The distinction matters because the fixes differ: acute injuries need early
protection then graded return; overuse injuries need the *training load* itself
recalibrated.

## The tissues and their signature injuries

Different tissues fail in different ways and heal at very different speeds (next
chapter). Knowing which tissue is involved tells you a lot.

| Tissue | Acute injury | Overuse injury | Heals… |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Muscle** | Strain / tear ("pull") | Rare true overuse; more often fatigue | Fast (rich blood supply) |
| **Tendon** | Strain / rupture | **Tendinopathy** | Slowly (poor blood supply) |
| **Ligament** | **Sprain** | Laxity from repeated stress | Slowly |
| **Bone** | Fracture | **Stress fracture / bone stress injury** | Weeks–months |
| **Joint / cartilage** | Sprain, meniscus/labral tear | Wear, osteoarthritis | Poorly (cartilage barely heals) |
| **Disc / nerve** | Disc irritation, nerve-root pinch | Gradual disc changes | Variable; nerves slowly |

Two vocabulary fixes that clear up endless confusion:

- **Strain vs. sprain.** A **strain** is a **muscle or tendon** injury (you
  *strain* a muscle). A **sprain** is a **ligament** injury (you *sprain* an
  ankle). Same-sounding, different tissues.
- **"Tendinitis" is usually tendinosis.** Chronic tendon pain is mostly
  **degeneration and failed healing** (tendin-*osis*), not classic inflammation
  (tendin-*itis*). That's why anti-inflammatories often disappoint and *loading*
  is the real treatment — more in the healing chapter.

### Grading strains and sprains

Both muscle strains and ligament sprains are graded by how much tissue is
disrupted:

| Grade | Damage | Typical picture |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **I (mild)** | A few fibres | Mild pain, minimal strength loss, full or near-full range | 
| **II (moderate)** | Partial tear | Clear pain, weakness, swelling/bruising, limited range |
| **III (severe)** | Complete rupture | Major loss of function, sometimes a palpable gap; may need surgery |

Most training "tweaks" are **Grade I** — genuinely minor, dramatic-feeling,
and fine within days to a couple of weeks.

## Pain is a signal, not a strain gauge

The single most useful idea in modern injury science: **pain and tissue damage are
related but not the same thing.** Pain is the brain's *output* — its best guess at
how much protecting a body part needs — built from tissue signals *plus* context,
expectation, fear, sleep, and stress.

Practical consequences:

- **Lots of pain can come from little damage** (a benign back spasm can be
  agonising) and **serious damage can be nearly painless** at first (some stress
  fractures).
- **Hurt does not automatically mean harm.** Especially in *persistent* pain, the
  nervous system can stay protective long after tissue has healed — the subject of
  the chronic-tightness chapter.
- This is *not* "the pain is in your head." It's real. It just means pain is a poor
  ruler for measuring damage, and you shouldn't read every ache as injury — or
  ignore a quiet one.

## Red flags — when to stop reading and get help

Most aches can be managed sensibly at home. A small number **cannot wait.** Seek
urgent medical care if you have:

- **Loss of bladder or bowel control, or numbness around the groin/inner thighs
  ("saddle" area)** — possible **cauda equina syndrome**, a spinal **emergency**
  (go to the ER).
- **Progressive or significant weakness** in a limb (foot drop, a leg giving way).
- **Numbness, tingling, or pain shooting down a limb** that's new or worsening —
  possible nerve involvement (get assessed promptly).
- **A pop with immediate loss of function**, gross swelling, or visible deformity —
  possible rupture or fracture.
- **Pain with fever, unexplained weight loss, or severe unremitting night pain** —
  needs medical work-up, not rehab.
- Trauma with an inability to bear weight or use the joint at all.

When in doubt about any of these, get it looked at. Everything else — the sore
knee, the tight hip, the tweaked back with no leg symptoms — is usually a
manageable problem, and the rest of this section is about managing it well.

## The takeaway

- Injuries are **acute** (one bad moment) or **overuse** (too much, too soon);
  the fix differs.
- Learn the tissue: **strain = muscle/tendon, sprain = ligament**; chronic tendon
  pain is usually **tendinosis** (degeneration), treated with **loading**.
- Strains and sprains grade **I–III**; most training tweaks are mild **Grade I**.
- **Pain ≠ damage.** It's a protection signal shaped by context — useful, but a bad
  measure of tissue harm.
- Know the **red flags** (saddle numbness, bladder/bowel change, real weakness,
  radiating limb symptoms) — those mean *get help now*.
