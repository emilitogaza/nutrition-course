# Dietary Fibre

Fibre is the least glamorous topic in nutrition and one of the highest-leverage.
It has no marketing budget, no supplement industry worth the name, and one of the
most consistent bodies of evidence attached to any dietary factor.

It's also the nutrient most people fall shortest on. Swedish adults average
roughly **16–20 g a day** against a recommendation of **25–35 g** — a gap that
almost nobody notices, because nothing dramatic happens when you miss it.

## What fibre actually is

Fibre is **carbohydrate that human digestive enzymes can't break down**. It
passes into the large intestine intact, where two things can happen: it adds
physical bulk, or it gets **fermented by gut bacteria**.

That definition also covers **resistant starch** — starch that escapes digestion
because of its structure or because it's been cooked and cooled (the
retrogradation that happens in day-old rice, cold pasta, or cooled potatoes).

## The classification that actually matters

You were probably taught **soluble vs insoluble**. That split is real but it's a
poor predictor of what a fibre does. Two properties matter more:

- **Viscosity** — does it form a gel in water? This is what lowers cholesterol and
  blunts blood glucose.
- **Fermentability** — do gut bacteria eat it? This is what produces
  **short-chain fatty acids** and, incidentally, gas.

| Fibre | Solubility | Viscous? | Fermentable? | Main job | Found in |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Cellulose** | Insoluble | No | Poorly | Bulk, transit time | Vegetables, bran, whole grains |
| **Hemicellulose** | Mixed | Some | Partly | Bulk | Whole grains, bran |
| **Lignin** | Insoluble | No | No | Bulk | Seeds, stalks, root veg |
| **Beta-glucan** | Soluble | **Very** | Yes | **Lowers LDL**, blunts glucose | **Oats, barley** |
| **Pectin** | Soluble | Yes | Yes | Lowers LDL, slows gastric emptying | Apples, citrus, berries |
| **Psyllium** | Soluble | **Very** | Poorly | Normalises stool *both* ways | Husk supplement |
| **Inulin / FOS** | Soluble | No | **Highly** | Prebiotic, feeds bifidobacteria | Onion, garlic, leek, chicory, Jerusalem artichoke |
| **Resistant starch** | Insoluble | No | **Highly** | Butyrate production | Cooled potato/rice/pasta, green banana, legumes |
| **GOS** | Soluble | No | Highly | Prebiotic | Legumes |

The practical implication: **"eat more fibre" is too blunt.** If your cholesterol
is the target, you want **viscous** fibre (oats, barley, psyllium). If your
problem is constipation, you want **bulking** fibre plus fluid. If you're feeding
your microbiome, you want **fermentable** fibre and variety. Most people benefit
from all three, which is why "eat a wide range of plants" ends up being the
practical instruction.

## What the evidence shows

Fibre's evidence base is unusually strong for a nutrient, because the effect
sizes are large and the dose-response is clean.

The landmark analysis is **Reynolds et al., *The Lancet* (2019)** — a series of
systematic reviews and meta-analyses covering 185 prospective studies and 58
clinical trials. The findings:

- Comparing the **highest fibre consumers with the lowest**, all-cause and
  cardiovascular mortality was **15–30% lower**.
- Risk reduction across critical outcomes was **greatest at 25–29 g/day**, and the
  dose-response curves suggested **still greater benefit above that**.
- The pattern held for **coronary heart disease, stroke, type 2 diabetes, and
  colorectal cancer**.

The mechanisms are unusually well characterised, which is part of why this looks
causal rather than a health-conscious-people artefact:

1. **Viscous fibre binds bile acids** in the gut, forcing the liver to pull
   cholesterol from circulation to make more — the LDL-lowering effect.
2. **Fermentation produces short-chain fatty acids**, particularly **butyrate**,
   the preferred fuel of colon cells (covered further in the gut microbiome
   chapter).
3. **Slowed gastric emptying and glucose absorption** flatten post-meal blood
   sugar.
4. **Bulk and transit** reduce contact time between the gut wall and potential
   carcinogens.
5. **Satiety** — fibre-rich food is more filling per calorie, which is why fibre
   intake predicts body weight independently of calories in observational data.

## How much you need

The **Nordic Nutrition Recommendations 2023** — the basis for Swedish
advice — set fibre at **at least 3 g per MJ of energy**, which works out to:

| | Target |
| --- | --- |
| **Adult women** | **≥ 25 g/day** |
| **Adult men** | **≥ 35 g/day** |
| **Optimal range from the mortality data** | 25–29 g/day and up |

Note the energy-scaled logic: **if you eat more, you need more.** An athlete
consuming 4 000 kcal has a proportionally higher target than a sedentary person
on 1 800 — though in practice, large eaters usually hit it without trying,
provided the extra food isn't all shakes and white rice.

## Fibre content of common foods

| Food | Portion | Fibre |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Psyllium husk** | 10 g | **~8 g** |
| **Chia seeds** | 25 g | ~8 g |
| **Cooked lentils** | 200 g | **~16 g** |
| **Black beans / kidney beans** | 200 g | ~14 g |
| **Kikärtor (chickpeas)** | 200 g | ~15 g |
| **Rye crispbread (knäckebröd)** | 3 slices | **~6 g** |
| **Oats (dry)** | 80 g | ~7 g |
| **Wholegrain rye bread** | 2 slices | ~6 g |
| **Broccoli** | 200 g | ~5 g |
| **Raspberries** | 125 g | **~8 g** |
| **Pear with skin** | 1 | ~5 g |
| **Potato with skin** | 200 g | ~4 g |
| **Almonds** | 30 g | ~3.5 g |
| **Wholegrain pasta (dry)** | 100 g | ~8 g |
| **White rice** | 100 g dry | ~1 g |
| **White bread** | 2 slices | ~1.5 g |

Sweden has a structural advantage here that's easy to overlook: **knäckebröd,
rye bread, oats, and root vegetables** are all fibre-dense staples already in the
cultural default. Three pieces of rye crispbread with keso is roughly a quarter of
a day's fibre target in a snack that takes 30 seconds.

**Legumes are the single biggest lever.** One 200 g portion of lentils or beans
moves you 14–16 g — over half a day's target from one component of one meal.

## The athlete's dilemma

Here's where general health advice and performance advice pull apart.

**Fibre is excellent for you and terrible immediately before training.** It slows
gastric emptying, draws water into the gut, and adds bulk — all of which cause
cramping, stitch, urgency, and the GI distress that ruins endurance sessions.
Somewhere between **30 and 90% of endurance athletes** report exercise-related GI
symptoms, and fibre timing is one of the most controllable contributors.

The resolution is **timing, not avoidance**:

- **Away from training** (the bulk of your day): eat fibre freely. This is where
  your 25–35 g comes from.
- **3–4 hours pre-session**: shift toward lower-fibre, easily digested carbs —
  white rice, white bread, bananas, sports drink. This is the one context where
  refined carbs are the *correct* choice.
- **Race day / long events**: a **low-residue** approach for 24–48 hours before is
  standard practice for marathon and triathlon. It's a deliberate, temporary
  intervention — not a way of eating.
- **After training**: fibre back to normal. There's no reason to keep it low
  post-session.

Athletes cutting weight face the mirror-image problem: high-fibre, high-volume
food is exactly what makes a deficit tolerable. In a cut, fibre is your ally.

## Raising your intake without misery

The most common failure mode is going from 15 g to 40 g in a weekend and
concluding that fibre "doesn't agree with you." The gas, bloating, and discomfort
are **real, predictable, and temporary** — they're the byproduct of a microbial
population that hasn't adapted yet.

1. **Add ~5 g per week**, not all at once. The bacterial community shifts over
   **two to four weeks**.
2. **Raise fluid alongside it.** Bulking fibre without water is how you turn a
   fibre increase into constipation — the exact opposite of the intent.
3. **Start with better-tolerated sources.** Oats, root vegetables, berries, and
   psyllium are gentler than a large jump in legumes and inulin.
4. **Spread it across meals** rather than one enormous bean-based dinner.
5. **Expect gas to settle.** Persistent, severe symptoms after a month of gradual
   increase are a reason to talk to someone, not to abandon fibre.

## FODMAPs and IBS — a note

For people with **irritable bowel syndrome**, certain fermentable fibres
(**FODMAPs** — the fermentable oligo-, di-, monosaccharides and polyols found in
wheat, onion, garlic, legumes, and some fruit) reliably trigger symptoms, and a
**low-FODMAP diet** has good evidence for short-term relief.

Two things are worth stating clearly:

- **It is a diagnostic protocol, not a way of eating.** The elimination phase runs
  **2–6 weeks**, followed by a **structured reintroduction** to identify personal
  triggers. Staying in the elimination phase indefinitely restricts diet
  unnecessarily and starves the microbiome.
- **It should be done with a dietitian**, and after an actual IBS diagnosis. A
  large fraction of people who self-diagnose and self-treat with low-FODMAP have
  something else going on — including coeliac disease, which needs to be ruled
  out *before* you remove gluten-containing grains.

## Supplements

Food first, genuinely — the whole-food package carries micronutrients and
polyphenols the isolated fibre doesn't. But if you're supplementing:

- **Psyllium husk** is the standout. Strong evidence for **LDL lowering**,
  **glycaemic control**, and — unusually — for normalising stool in **both**
  constipation and diarrhoea, because gel-forming works in both directions. Start
  at ~5 g/day with plenty of water.
- **Inulin / FOS** work as prebiotics but are among the **gassiest** fibres. Start
  very low.
- **Wheat dextrin and methylcellulose** are gentle but do little beyond adding
  bulk.

## Can you overdo it?

Rarely, but yes:

- Above roughly **50–60 g/day**, some people get persistent bloating and
  discomfort with no additional health return.
- Very high fibre intakes carry **phytate**, which binds **iron, zinc, and
  calcium** and reduces absorption. This matters most for people already at risk —
  see the plant-based athletes chapter.
- In athletes with **very high energy needs**, extremely bulky food can make it
  physically hard to eat enough. If you're struggling to hit calories, some
  refined carbohydrate is a feature, not a failure.

## The takeaway

- Fibre is **carbohydrate your enzymes can't digest** — and the Nordic diet is
  well short of the target, averaging ~16–20 g against **25 g (women) / 35 g
  (men)**.
- What matters isn't soluble vs insoluble but **viscosity** (lowers LDL and
  glucose) and **fermentability** (feeds the microbiome).
- The *Lancet* 2019 analysis found **15–30% lower all-cause and cardiovascular
  mortality** in the highest consumers, with the sweet spot at **25–29 g/day** and
  benefit continuing above it.
- **Legumes are the biggest single lever** — one portion is over half a day's
  target. Rye crispbread, oats, and root veg make it easy in Sweden.
- For training, **time it rather than avoid it**: low fibre in the 3–4 hours
  before a session, normal the rest of the day.
- Increase by **~5 g per week with more fluid**. The bloating is real and passes
  in 2–4 weeks.
- **Low-FODMAP is a time-limited protocol with a reintroduction phase**, done with
  a dietitian — not a permanent diet.
