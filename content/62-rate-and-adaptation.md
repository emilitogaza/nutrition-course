# Rate of Loss, Diet Breaks & Adaptation

Setting a deficit is easy. Sustaining fat loss over weeks and months — through the
inevitable slowdown — is where most attempts fail. This chapter is about *managing
the diet over time*.

## How fast should you lose?

The right rate balances speed against muscle retention and sustainability.

| Rate (% body weight/week) | Use case |
| --- | --- |
| ~0.5% | Lean individuals, athletes preserving performance |
| ~0.5–1.0% | Most people — the reliable default |
| > 1% | Higher body fat, short-term, or medical supervision |

Leaner people must go **slower** — with less fat to draw on, an aggressive deficit
pulls more from muscle. Someone carrying more fat can lose faster with less risk.
A useful heuristic: **the leaner you get, the slower you should go.**

## Why fat loss slows: metabolic adaptation

A plateau is not (usually) a broken metabolism — it's **adaptation**, and it's
predictable. As you lose weight, several things reduce your energy expenditure:

- You're a **smaller body** — lower BMR and lower movement cost.
- **NEAT drops** — you unconsciously move less (fidgeting, walking, posture).
- **Adaptive thermogenesis** — hormonal shifts (lower leptin, thyroid,
  sympathetic tone) make you burn somewhat *less* than your new size predicts.
- **The deficit shrinks** — the fixed calorie target that once created a 500 kcal
  deficit now sits closer to your (lowered) maintenance.

So the same intake that drove loss becomes maintenance. To keep losing you must
**re-open the deficit** — eat a bit less or move a bit more — periodically, not
all at once.

## Refeeds and diet breaks

Two tools for managing the physiological and psychological toll of a long diet:

- **Refeed** — a short (1–2 day) planned increase in calories, mostly from
  **carbohydrate**, back toward maintenance. Refills glycogen, can transiently
  nudge leptin, and gives a mental break. Best programmed, not improvised into a
  binge.
- **Diet break** — a longer stretch (1–2 weeks) eating at **maintenance**. The
  **MATADOR study** (Byrne et al., 2018) found that intermittent dieting —
  two weeks on, two weeks at maintenance — produced *more* fat loss and less
  metabolic slowdown than continuous dieting over the same net deficit period.

Neither is magic, and neither is mandatory — but for long or aggressive diets they
improve adherence, protect performance, and may blunt adaptation.

## Coming out of a diet: reverse dieting

Finishing a diet is where a lot of people undo their work — they return to old
habits overnight and rebound. A gentler exit:

- **Raise calories gradually** back toward (or above) maintenance over several
  weeks rather than all at once.
- The theory that slow refeeding "repairs" the metabolism faster is **overstated**
  — adaptation reverses mostly as you regain weight and eat more, on its own
  timeline. The real value of a controlled exit is **behavioural**: it prevents
  the binge-rebound and keeps you in control while appetite (elevated after a
  diet) settles.

Expect a few kilos of **immediate scale rebound** from glycogen and water when you
add carbs back — that's not fat, and it's normal.

## The takeaway

- Target **~0.5–1% body weight/week**; go **slower the leaner** you are.
- Plateaus are **adaptation** (smaller body + lower NEAT + adaptive thermogenesis
  + shrinking deficit), not failure — re-open the deficit periodically.
- **Refeeds and diet breaks** aid adherence and may reduce slowdown on long diets.
- Exit a diet **gradually** to avoid rebound; the benefit is behavioural, not
  metabolic magic.
