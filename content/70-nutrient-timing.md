# Nutrient Timing

Nutrient timing — *when* you eat relative to training — was oversold for years as
the secret to gains. The research has since put it in its place: timing is a
**fine-tuning tool**, not a foundation. Total daily intake does the heavy lifting;
timing earns you the last few percent, and it matters more in some situations than
others.

## The "anabolic window" — mostly a myth

The classic claim: you must slam protein within ~30–45 minutes post-workout or
"waste" the session. The evidence says otherwise.

- The muscle-protein-synthesis response to a workout stays elevated for **~24
  hours**, not 30 minutes. The "window" is a barn door, not a keyhole.
- **Aragon & Schoenfeld's** review, and Schoenfeld's own meta-analysis, concluded
  that once **total daily protein** is equated, the precise post-workout timing
  has little independent effect.
- If you trained fasted (nothing for many hours), eating protein reasonably soon
  after *does* matter more — but "reasonably soon" means within a couple of hours,
  not minutes.

Translation: don't sprint to your shaker. Have a protein-containing meal in the
hours around training and you've captured essentially all the benefit.

## What actually matters: daily distribution

The timing variable with the best evidence isn't the post-workout window — it's
**spreading protein across the day** (covered in the protein chapter). Because the
per-meal MPS response saturates and resets, **3–5 evenly spaced doses of ~0.4
g/kg** beat skewing all your protein into one or two meals. That's the timing rule
worth caring about.

## Pre-workout nutrition

A meal **1–3 hours before** training supports performance and recovery:

- **Carbohydrate** tops up glycogen and blood glucose for the session — most
  useful before long or intense work.
- **Protein** before training leaves amino acids circulating, which partly
  overlaps with any post-workout need — this is *why* the post-workout window is so
  forgiving.
- Keep fat and fibre **moderate** close to training to avoid GI discomfort.

## Post-workout nutrition

Still useful, just not a race:

- **Protein** (~20–40 g) to support the elevated synthesis — any time in the next
  couple of hours.
- **Carbohydrate** to begin refilling glycogen — this becomes **genuinely
  time-sensitive only** when you have to perform again soon (a second session, a
  tournament) within ~8 hours. With 24 h to recover, total daily carbs matter far
  more than speed.

## When timing genuinely matters

Timing moves from "nice" to "important" in specific cases:

1. **Two-a-days or multiple events per day** — rapid glycogen refuelling between
   sessions is real and worth chasing.
2. **Fasted training** — eating around the session matters more when you started
   empty.
3. **Endurance events** — **fuelling during** long efforts (carbs per hour) is one
   of the highest-impact timing strategies in all of sport (next chapter).
4. **Very high-level athletes** squeezing out marginal gains.

For a general trainee lifting a few times a week and eating enough protein daily,
timing is a rounding error next to total intake and training.

## The takeaway

- The **anabolic window is wide (~24 h)** — don't stress the post-workout minutes.
- The timing that matters most is **daily protein distribution** (3–5 doses).
- **Pre- and post-workout** meals help, but total intake dominates for most
  people.
- Timing becomes **important** for two-a-days, fasted training, and **fuelling
  during endurance events**.
