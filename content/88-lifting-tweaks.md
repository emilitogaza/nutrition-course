# Strains, Tweaks & the Deadlift Back

Almost every lifter eventually meets the mid-set "tweak": you're pulling a heavy
deadlift and something in your back suddenly **shoots, grabs, or feels like a
shock**. It's alarming, it's common, and it's usually far less serious than it
feels. This chapter walks through what likely happened, how to read the warning
signs, and how to get back to lifting without fear running the show.

> **Education, not diagnosis.** This describes common patterns. It can't examine
> *you*. Anything with the red-flag features below — or that isn't settling —
> warrants a real assessment.

## What probably happened

A sharp sensation through a back muscle like the **latissimus dorsi**, **erector
spinae**, or **quadratus lumborum** during a deadlift is *usually* one of these:

- **A muscle strain ("pull").** The most common answer. The lats and the
  thoracolumbar fascia are loaded hard in a deadlift — the lat runs from your upper
  arm down into the spine and pelvis via that fascia. A few fibres get overloaded
  and complain sharply. The signature: pain that's **local to the muscle/back**,
  sore to touch or to contract, **no symptoms travelling down a leg.** Most are
  mild (Grade I) and settle in days to a couple of weeks.
- **A protective spasm or "grab."** Sometimes there's little real tissue damage —
  the muscle suddenly **clamps down to protect** the area. It can be intensely
  painful and frightening, feel like something "went," and yet calm down
  surprisingly fast. This guarding response is the nervous system doing its job (a
  theme we return to in the next chapter).
- **A cramp**, if it's a gripping, contracting quality that eases with gentle
  movement.

Any of these can feel like a "shooting" pain *within the muscle* — that's normal
for a strain or spasm and not a cause for alarm on its own.

## The word that changes things: *shock* or *electric*

Here's the distinction worth internalising. **Where** the sensation goes, and
**what** it feels like, separates a muscle problem from a nerve problem:

| Feature | Muscle strain / spasm | Nerve-root irritation (radicular) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Quality | Sharp, aching, "grabbing," sore | **Electric, burning, shooting, "shock"** |
| Location | Stays **local** to the back muscle | **Travels** — down the buttock/leg along a line |
| Extras | Tender, stiff, worse contracting | **Numbness, pins-and-needles, weakness** |
| Cause | Overloaded muscle/fascia | Nerve root irritated (e.g. by a disc) |

So: a shooting feeling **contained in the back muscle**, with no leg symptoms, is
most likely a **strain or spasm**. A true **electric shock that radiates down a
limb**, especially with **numbness, tingling, or weakness**, points toward a
**nerve root** — a different problem that deserves prompt professional assessment.
You can't reliably tell these apart from a description alone, which is exactly why
the neural pattern is a "go get looked at" signal rather than a "rub some dip and
keep pulling" one.

And the **emergency** version, worth repeating: new **bladder or bowel changes,
numbness around the groin/saddle area, or weakness in both legs** = possible cauda
equina = **ER now.** Rare, but never to be sat on.

## What to do in the moment and the first days

- **Stop the set.** Don't grind out more reps to "prove it's fine" — re-loading a
  fresh strain is how a minor one becomes a moderate one.
- **Don't panic.** This matters more than it sounds. Fear and bracing *amplify*
  the protective spasm and the pain; a calm "okay, I tweaked something, let's
  settle it down" genuinely helps. Catastrophising ("I've wrecked my back") makes
  recovery slower.
- **Keep moving gently.** Total bed rest is the old, worse advice. Walk, shift
  positions, move within the range that feels okay. Motion is lotion for an
  irritated back.
- **PEACE & LOVE, not endless RICE** (previous chapter): protect early, then get
  it loading and moving. Reach for anti-inflammatories only if you genuinely need
  the pain relief, not as a healing strategy.
- Expect most acute lifting back tweaks to **improve noticeably within days** and
  substantially over **1–3 weeks.**

## Why it happened — and preventing the rerun

Tweaks rarely come from nowhere. Usual suspects:

- **A load or volume jump** the tissue wasn't prepared for.
- **Form breakdown under fatigue** — the last hard reps, when the brace slips and
  the spine rounds or shifts.
- **Losing the brace / neutral-ish spine** and letting the bar drift away from you.
- **Skipping a real warm-up**, or a bad day (poor sleep, high stress — both lower
  tissue and nervous-system tolerance).

Prevention is mostly load management and technique: build up gradually, **brace**
(think 360° of trunk tension), keep the **bar over midfoot**, engage the **lats**
to hold the bar close, hinge from the hips, and stop sets when form degrades.

## Getting back under the bar

The goal is **graded return**, not indefinite avoidance:

- **Relative rest, then reload.** Once the sharp phase passes, restart light — the
  bar or a fraction of your working weight — and rebuild over sessions. Pain that
  is **mild and settles** is acceptable to work around; sharp, escalating, or
  radiating pain is a stop sign.
- **Rebuild confidence, not just strength.** Backs often stay "sensitive" after a
  scare because the nervous system is guarding (next chapter). Re-exposing it
  progressively teaches it the movement is safe again.
- **Address the why** so it doesn't recur: the load jump, the fatigue, the brace.
- If it's **not improving in a few weeks**, keeps recurring, or ever shows the
  **neural or red-flag features**, get a physiotherapist involved — for both the
  diagnosis and a tailored return plan.

## The takeaway

- A sharp/shooting feeling **contained in the back muscle** during a deadlift is
  usually a **muscle strain or protective spasm** — common, scary, mostly minor.
- A true **electric "shock" that radiates down a leg**, or comes with
  **numbness/tingling/weakness**, suggests a **nerve root** — get assessed
  promptly; **saddle numbness or bladder/bowel changes are an ER emergency.**
- In the moment: **stop the set, stay calm, keep gently moving**; most tweaks ease
  within **1–3 weeks**.
- Prevent the rerun with **load management, bracing, and technique**; return
  **gradually**, rebuilding confidence as much as strength.
