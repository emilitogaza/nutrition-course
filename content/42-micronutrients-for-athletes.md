# Micronutrients That Matter for Athletes

Most micronutrient deficiencies are rare in well-fed people — but a handful show
up often enough in athletes to be worth targeting. Training raises turnover and
losses (sweat, red-blood-cell turnover, oxidative stress), and dieting shrinks
the food that supplies these nutrients. These are the ones to actually check.

## Vitamin D — the near-universal shortfall

Vitamin D acts more like a hormone than a vitamin, influencing bone health,
immune function, and muscle. Deficiency is extremely common — a large fraction of
the population, and of athletes, sits below optimal, especially:

- Anyone at **higher latitudes** or training **indoors**.
- Those with **darker skin** (melanin slows skin synthesis).
- **Winter** in general — the sun is too low to make it.

**Blood testing is the honest way to know.** The marker is serum **25(OH)D**;
common targets are **≥ 75 nmol/L (30 ng/mL)**, with many sports guidelines
preferring the upper end. If low, typical repletion is **~1,000–2,000 IU/day**
of D3 (higher short-term under supervision), rechecked after a couple of months.
This is one of the few supplements with a strong case for many people —
*because* deficiency is so common, not because the vitamin is magic.

## Iron — especially for female and endurance athletes

Iron carries oxygen (haemoglobin) and sits at the heart of aerobic energy
production. Deficiency causes fatigue and tanks endurance. At-risk groups:

- **Menstruating women** — monthly blood loss is the biggest driver.
- **Endurance athletes** — "foot-strike haemolysis," GI losses, and sweat, plus
  a training-induced dilution of blood markers.
- **Vegetarians/vegans** — plant (non-heme) iron is absorbed far less efficiently
  than heme iron from meat.

Don't guess with iron: **test first** (ferritin, plus haemoglobin and
transferrin saturation). Iron *overload* is harmful, so supplement only a
confirmed low. Boost dietary absorption by pairing plant iron with **vitamin C**
and separating it from coffee/tea (tannins) and calcium.

## Magnesium

Involved in **300+ enzyme reactions**, including energy production and muscle
function. Intakes are commonly below the RDA because it's concentrated in foods
people under-eat. Hard training increases losses through sweat and urine. It
won't turn you into a superhero, but correcting a shortfall supports sleep,
muscle function, and recovery. Food sources: leafy greens, nuts, seeds, legumes,
whole grains, dark chocolate. Supplemental forms like **glycinate or citrate**
are better absorbed and gentler on the gut than oxide.

## Calcium & bone health

Critical for bone, plus muscle contraction and nerve signalling. Athletes at
particular risk are those with **low energy availability** and those avoiding
dairy. Low calcium plus low vitamin D plus under-eating is the recipe for **stress
fractures** and long-term bone loss. Dairy, fortified plant milks, tofu, leafy
greens, and small fish with bones cover it.

## Zinc & the B vitamins

- **Zinc** supports immune function, testosterone, and recovery; it's lost in
  sweat and lower in plant-based diets (phytates reduce absorption).
- **B12** is *animal-foods-only* — a genuine supplement priority for **vegans**,
  not optional.
- The energy-metabolism **B vitamins** are rarely deficient with adequate
  calories, but restrictive diets raise the risk.

## The "female athlete" and RED-S caveat

Many of these shortfalls cluster in athletes with **Low Energy Availability** —
not eating enough to cover training. The syndrome **RED-S** (Relative Energy
Deficiency in Sport) links under-fuelling to impaired bone health, hormones,
immunity, and performance, in all sexes. If several micronutrients are low at
once, the root cause is often **not eating enough overall** — fix that first.

## The takeaway

- The real-world athlete shortfalls: **vitamin D, iron, magnesium, calcium,
  zinc** (and **B12** for vegans).
- **Test, don't guess** for **vitamin D and iron** — both can be harmful in
  excess.
- Improve absorption with pairing tricks (iron + vitamin C; better-absorbed
  magnesium forms).
- Clustered deficiencies usually signal **under-eating (RED-S)** — address total
  energy first.
