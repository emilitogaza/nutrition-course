# How Tissue Heals — and How to Help It

Injuries heal on a biological timeline you can *support* but can't rush past. This
chapter covers what actually happens as tissue repairs, why the popular instinct to
"rest it and take an anti-inflammatory" is often exactly wrong, and — this being a
nutrition course — what to eat to give repair the best raw materials.

## The three phases of healing

Nearly all soft-tissue healing runs through three overlapping phases:

1. **Inflammation (≈ day 0–5).** Blood flow rises, immune cells clear debris,
   swelling and pain limit use. This phase feels bad but is **necessary** — it's
   the demolition-and-cleanup crew that *enables* rebuilding. Blindly shutting it
   down can slow the whole process.
2. **Proliferation / repair (≈ day 3 to week 6).** The body lays down new tissue —
   collagen, blood vessels, muscle fibres. It's fast but disorganised; the new
   scaffold is weak and randomly aligned.
3. **Remodelling / maturation (week 6 to many months, sometimes a year+).** The
   messy new tissue is gradually reorganised, aligned along lines of stress, and
   strengthened. **This is where loading does its most important work** — see
   below.

The lesson from the timeline: early healing needs *protection*, but the long tail
needs *progressive stress*. Resting all the way through wastes the phase where
tissue is supposed to get strong again.

## Inflammation is not the enemy

Because inflammation hurts, the reflex is to crush it — ice everything, take
ibuprofen around the clock. The evidence says go gently here:

- **Anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)** help pain and function short-term but may
  **blunt the tissue repair and adaptation** you're trying to build, especially in
  tendon, bone, and muscle. Use them for genuine pain relief when needed, not as a
  default "healing aid."
- **Antioxidant megadoses** (high-dose vitamin C/E around the clock) can dull the
  very signals that drive repair and adaptation — the same effect covered in the
  supplements section, where mopping up all exercise-induced oxidative stress
  *reduced* training gains.
- This is why the classic **RICE** (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation) has been
  updated to **PEACE & LOVE**:

> **PEACE** (first days): **P**rotect, **E**levate, **A**void anti-inflammatories,
> **C**ompress, **E**ducate. **LOVE** (after): **L**oad, **O**ptimism,
> **V**ascularisation (get blood flowing — gentle cardio), **E**xercise.
> *(Dubois & Esculier, 2019.)*

Note what's *in* that framework: **loading, optimism, and movement** — and what's
flagged *against*: reflexive anti-inflammatory use and total rest.

## Different tissues, very different clocks

How long healing takes depends mostly on **blood supply**:

| Tissue | Rough healing time | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Muscle** | Days to a few weeks | Excellent blood supply |
| **Bone** | ~6–12 weeks (longer for stress injuries) | Good supply, but full remodelling continues for months |
| **Tendon / ligament** | Months (can be 6–12+) | Poor blood supply — the frustrating slow ones |
| **Cartilage** | Very poor / often doesn't | Almost no blood supply |
| **Nerve** | Very slow (mm per day) | Regrows slowly if at all |

This is why a tweaked calf muscle is forgotten in two weeks but a cranky Achilles
tendon takes months — and why patience with tendons isn't optional, it's biology.

## Loading is medicine

The most counter-intuitive truth in rehab: after the earliest protective phase,
**controlled loading is the primary treatment**, not rest. Tissues are living and
adapt to demand (this is **mechanotransduction** — cells turning mechanical stress
into biological signals to build). Progressive loading:

- Aligns and strengthens new collagen during remodelling.
- Drives tendon and bone adaptation (the entire basis of tendinopathy and
  stress-injury rehab).
- Prevents the strength, mobility, and confidence losses of prolonged rest —
  **deconditioning is its own injury.**

The art is **dosage**: enough stress to stimulate, not so much it re-injures.
"Relative rest" — reducing load, not stopping — beats the couch almost every time.
For chronic tendon pain specifically, **heavy-slow resistance and eccentric
loading** are the best-evidenced treatments (Alfredson's eccentric protocol; Beyer
et al., 2015), with **isometric holds** useful for calming a painful tendon enough
to train.

## Feeding repair — the nutrition that matters

This is where the rest of the course pays off. You can't out-eat a bad rehab plan,
but under-fuelling a good one is a real, common mistake.

- **Don't crash-diet while injured.** A big calorie deficit and **low energy
  availability** slow healing and accelerate muscle loss during downtime. Eat at
  **maintenance or a slight surplus** while repairing — this is not the time for an
  aggressive cut.
- **Raise protein** to about **1.6–2.5 g/kg/day.** Immobilised muscle becomes
  *anabolically resistant* and wastes quickly; higher protein (well distributed,
  leucine-rich — see the protein chapters) limits the loss.
- **Collagen/gelatin + vitamin C, timed before rehab.** ~**15 g gelatin or
  collagen with ~50 mg vitamin C, taken ~1 hour before** loading the injured
  tissue: in a controlled trial this **doubled markers of collagen synthesis**
  (Shaw et al., 2017). The window matters — the amino-acid building blocks and the
  loading stimulus need to coincide. Most relevant for tendon, ligament, and bone.
- **Creatine** may reduce muscle loss during immobilisation and support the
  strength rebuild afterward (and it's safe long-term — see the ergogenics
  chapter).
- **Omega-3s** may help counter disuse-related anabolic resistance; keep intake in
  the normal food/supplement range rather than megadosing.
- **Micronutrients for the specific tissue:** **vitamin C** (collagen), **vitamin D
  + calcium** (bone), **zinc** (wound repair), and **iron** if you're deficient
  (oxygen delivery). Correct shortfalls; don't megadose.
- **Protect the basics:** **sleep** is when much of repair happens (see the sleep
  chapter), and **alcohol impairs muscle protein synthesis and healing** (see the
  alcohol chapter). The unglamorous inputs move the needle most.

## The takeaway

- Healing runs **inflammation → repair → remodelling**; early phases need
  protection, the long tail needs **progressive loading**.
- **Inflammation isn't the enemy** — go easy on reflexive NSAIDs and antioxidant
  megadoses; think **PEACE & LOVE**, not endless RICE.
- Healing speed tracks **blood supply**: muscle fast, **tendon/ligament slow
  (months)**, cartilage barely — patience with tendons is biology.
- **Loading is the medicine** after the acute phase; relative rest beats total
  rest.
- Feed repair: **don't crash-diet, protein 1.6–2.5 g/kg, collagen + vitamin C ~1 h
  before rehab (Shaw 2017), sleep, and skip the alcohol.**
