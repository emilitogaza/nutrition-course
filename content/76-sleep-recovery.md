# Sleep, Stress & Recovery

You don't adapt to training *during* training — you adapt during **recovery**, and
the biggest lever on recovery isn't a supplement or a meal timing trick. It's
**sleep**. This chapter makes the case that sleep and stress belong in a nutrition
course, because they change what the same diet does to your body.

## Sleep is a body-composition variable

The most striking evidence: Nedeltcheva et al. (2010) put dieters on an identical
calorie deficit and varied only their sleep — **8.5 hours versus 5.5 hours** a
night. Both groups lost the same total weight. But the short-sleep group lost
**~55% less fat** and shed *more lean mass* instead. Same diet, same deficit —
worse sleep flipped the outcome from "losing fat" toward "losing muscle."

Under-sleeping works against you on multiple fronts:

- **Hunger hormones shift.** Sleep restriction **raises ghrelin (hunger) and
  lowers leptin (fullness)**, and cravings skew toward calorie-dense food
  (Spiegel et al., 2004). You end up hungrier, on less food.
- **Insulin sensitivity drops** after even a few short nights, nudging nutrient
  partitioning the wrong way.
- **Muscle protein synthesis and testosterone fall** with sleep debt, while
  cortisol rises — a catabolic tilt.
- **Willpower erodes.** Tired people make worse food choices; the diet fails for
  behavioural reasons before physiological ones.

## Sleep and performance

The effect runs the other way too. When athletes **extend** sleep — Mah et al.
(2011) had basketball players build up to ~10 hours in bed — sprint times,
shooting accuracy, and reaction time all improved. And in young athletes, chronic
short sleep tracks with **higher injury rates**: those averaging under 8 hours
were markedly more likely to be injured than those getting more (Milewski et al.,
2014).

For most people, **7–9 hours** is the target. If fat loss, muscle gain, or
performance is stalling despite a dialled-in diet, sleep is the first place to
look.

## Stress spends the same currency

Chronic psychological stress keeps **cortisol** elevated, which can increase
appetite (especially for hyper-palatable food), promote fat retention, and impair
recovery and sleep — the same failure loop from a different entry point. Managing
training stress matters here too: piling heavy training on top of poor sleep and
high life-stress is how "doing everything right" still stalls. Deload weeks, easy
days, and actual rest are part of the nutrition picture, not separate from it.

## How food helps you sleep

Diet won't fix a bad sleep environment, but a few evidence-backed nudges help:

- **Protect the back half of the night.** **Caffeine** has a ~5-hour half-life —
  an afternoon coffee is still working at bedtime. **Alcohol** helps you fall
  asleep but wrecks sleep quality. Both are covered in their own chapters; both
  are the most common self-inflicted sleep wounds.
- **Don't diet *and* fast into the night.** Going to bed very hungry disrupts
  sleep. A modest **protein-containing** snack (e.g. casein or dairy) before bed
  supports overnight muscle synthesis without a heavy meal.
- **Carbohydrate earlier in the evening** may shorten the time it takes to fall
  asleep for some people; a very low-carb day can leave others wired.
- **Specific foods with modest evidence:** **tart cherry juice** (a natural
  melatonin source — Howatson et al., 2012) and **kiwifruit** (Lin et al., 2011)
  have small sleep-onset and quality benefits in trials. **Magnesium**
  sufficiency supports sleep; correcting a shortfall helps, megadosing doesn't.
- **Keep late meals moderate.** Large, fatty, or spicy meals close to bed can
  cause reflux and discomfort that fragment sleep.

None of these rival simply **going to bed on time in a dark, cool room** — but
they're the dietary levers that exist.

## The takeaway

- Sleep is a **body-composition variable**: matched diets produce very different
  results by sleep alone — short sleep **cut fat loss ~55%** and cost muscle
  (Nedeltcheva et al., 2010).
- Under-sleeping **raises hunger, lowers fullness, drops insulin sensitivity and
  testosterone, and erodes willpower**; aim for **7–9 hours**.
- **Chronic stress** spends the same cortisol currency — rest and deloads are part
  of nutrition, not separate.
- Food can help at the margins — **watch caffeine and alcohol timing**, don't go
  to bed starving, and tart cherry / kiwi have modest support.
