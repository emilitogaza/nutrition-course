# Alcohol & Your Goals

The macronutrients chapter flagged alcohol as its own category — energy-bearing,
but not protein, carb, or fat. This chapter fills that gap. Alcohol isn't
*required* for anything, so the honest framing isn't "good or bad" but **what it
costs** and how to spend that cost wisely if you choose to drink.

## Alcohol is a metabolic priority

Alcohol carries **7 kcal per gram** — closer to fat than to carbs — and those
calories are easy to forget, because drinks don't feel like food and often arrive
*with* food (and with the loosened restraint that leads to more of it).

More importantly, the body treats alcohol as a **toxin to clear first**. It can't
be stored, so metabolism shifts to burning it preferentially and **suppresses fat
oxidation** while it does — in one classic study, a modest dose of alcohol cut
whole-body fat burning by around a third for hours afterward (Suter et al., 1992).
Alcohol doesn't convert efficiently to body fat itself; the damage is more that it
**parks fat-burning** while the *other* calories you ate alongside it get stored.

## What it does to muscle

Drinking after training blunts the very adaptation you trained for. In a
controlled trial, alcohol consumed after exercise **reduced rates of muscle
protein synthesis by ~24–37%** — even when protein was co-ingested (Parr et al.,
2014). The signal to build was there; the alcohol dampened the response.

The effect is dose-dependent, and the doses that clearly impair recovery are on
the heavier side (roughly 1–1.5 g/kg — several drinks). A single drink with a meal
is a very different thing from a night out. But if a session mattered, alcohol
right after it works against you.

## Sleep, hormones, and the knock-on effects

Alcohol's indirect costs often outweigh the direct ones:

- **Sleep.** A nightcap helps you fall asleep but **degrades sleep quality** —
  it suppresses REM and fragments the second half of the night (Ebrahim et al.,
  2013). Since sleep is where much of recovery happens (next chapters), this
  quietly taxes everything else.
- **Hormones.** Heavy acute intake lowers testosterone; chronic heavy drinking
  more so. Light-to-moderate drinking has little measurable hormonal effect.
- **Behaviour.** The biggest real-world cost is usually **downstream** — the late
  food, the skipped morning session, the poor next-day choices. The drink is
  rarely the problem; the evening it anchors often is.

## Drinking around training, intelligently

You don't have to abstain to make progress. If you choose to drink, a few habits
keep the cost low:

- **Hit your protein and roughly your calories first.** A day built on adequate
  protein and whole food absorbs a few drinks far better than an empty one.
- **Put distance between hard training and heavy drinking.** Don't cap a key
  session with a big night; the recovery hit lands squarely on that work.
- **Count the calories honestly.** A few pints or cocktails can be 500+ kcal of
  "invisible" intake — enough to erase a day's deficit.
- **Choose lower-calorie drinks and pace with water.** Spirits with a
  zero-calorie mixer beat sugary cocktails and beer for calorie control, and
  alternating with water limits both volume and the next-day toll.
- **Mind the day after, not just the night.** Under-slept and hungover is when
  intake and training discipline both slip most.

## The takeaway

- Alcohol is a **fourth energy source (7 kcal/g)** the body prioritises burning,
  **suppressing fat oxidation** while it clears.
- Heavy drinking after training **blunts muscle protein synthesis (~24–37%)** and
  **degrades sleep** — the indirect costs are the big ones.
- You can drink and still progress: **protein and calories first, distance from
  key sessions, count it honestly, and protect the next day.**
