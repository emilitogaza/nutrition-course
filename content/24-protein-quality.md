# Protein Quality & Sources

Not all protein is equal. A gram of whey and a gram of wheat protein contain very
different amino acid profiles and are digested differently — so they don't build
muscle equally. "Quality" is the attempt to measure that difference.

## How quality is measured

Two scoring systems matter:

- **PDCAAS** (Protein Digestibility-Corrected Amino Acid Score) — the older
  standard, capped at 1.0, which hides differences between very high-quality
  proteins and slightly overstates some plant proteins.
- **DIAAS** (Digestible Indigestible Amino Acid Score) — the newer, more accurate
  FAO-recommended metric. It measures true digestibility at the end of the small
  intestine and isn't truncated at 1.0, so it separates the top proteins.

A protein scores well when it (a) is **highly digestible** and (b) supplies all
nine EAAs in the right ratios — with no single EAA falling short (the
**limiting amino acid**).

| Source | DIAAS (approx.) | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Whey isolate | ~1.1–1.2 | High leucine, fast |
| Whole milk / dairy | ~1.1 | Casein + whey |
| Egg | ~1.1 | Reference-grade |
| Beef / chicken | ~1.0–1.1 | Complete |
| Soy protein | ~0.9 | Best plant single-source |
| Pea protein | ~0.6–0.8 | Low in methionine |
| Wheat protein | ~0.4 | Low in lysine (limiting) |

## Animal vs. plant protein

Animal proteins are, on average, more **complete**, more **digestible**, and
**higher in leucine** — so gram-for-gram they trigger a bigger MPS response.
That's not a verdict against plant-based eating; it's a set of adjustments:

1. **Eat more total protein.** Plant sources are lower-quality *and* often come
   packaged with more carbs/fat and fibre that reduces digestibility, so aim
   toward the higher end of the daily range.
2. **Combine sources to cover limiting amino acids.** The classic pairing is
   **grains + legumes** (rice + beans, hummus + pita): grains are low in lysine,
   legumes are low in methionine, and together they complete each other. You don't
   need to combine them in the same meal — just across the day.
3. **Lean on the best plant proteins.** Soy (tofu, tempeh, edamame), and
   supplemental **pea+rice blends** designed to fill each other's gaps.
4. **Consider a leucine or EAA top-up around training** if fully plant-based and
   chasing hypertrophy — this offsets the lower leucine content.

Well-planned plant-based athletes build muscle just fine; they simply have to be
more deliberate about totals and variety.

## Whey vs. casein vs. blends

The two milk proteins behave differently:

- **Whey** — fast-digesting, very high leucine, spikes MPS quickly. Ideal
  around training and any time you want a rapid, strong response.
- **Casein** — slow-digesting; it clots in the stomach and releases amino acids
  over hours. A gentler, sustained rise — the classic "before bed" protein to
  support overnight synthesis.
- **Blends / whole foods** — most real meals are effectively blends, giving both
  a spike and a tail. This is perfectly good; the fast/slow distinction is a
  minor optimisation, not a make-or-break.

## Do you *need* protein powder?

No. Powder is **food, not magic** — a cheap, convenient, high-quality way to hit
your target, nothing more. If you get enough protein from meals, you don't need
it. Its real advantages are practicality (a shake is faster than cooking) and
cost per gram. Whey isolate is the default; casein for a slow option; soy or
pea+rice for plant-based.

## The takeaway

- **DIAAS** is the modern quality metric; **whey, dairy, egg, and meat** top it.
- Animal protein is more complete and higher in **leucine**; plant-based works
  well with **more total protein** and **smart combining** (grains + legumes).
- **Whey = fast, casein = slow**; blends and whole foods give you both.
- Powder is **optional convenience**, not a requirement.
