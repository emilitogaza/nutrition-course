# The Training Variables

A workout is a set of dials: how many sets, how heavy, how close to failure, how
often, how much rest. Turn them well and you grow; turn them badly and you spin
your wheels. This chapter is the evidence-based settings guide for each one.

## Volume — the primary driver

**Volume** — practically, the number of **hard sets per muscle per week** — is the
single biggest lever on hypertrophy. The relationship is **dose-response**: within
a sensible range, more challenging sets produce more growth (Schoenfeld, Ogborn &
Krieger, 2017).

- A broadly effective target is **~10–20 hard sets per muscle per week.**
- Below ~10 still works (and is plenty for beginners); pushing well above 20 brings
  **diminishing returns** and rising fatigue, and the ceiling is individual.
- Count sets that are actually *challenging* (near failure) and that hit the muscle
  directly or as a strong secondary.

If you change nothing else, **making sure each muscle gets enough weekly hard sets**
is the highest-value adjustment most people can make.

## Load — a wide effective range

**Load** (how heavy, as a % of your 1-rep max) matters less for *size* than people
think and more for *strength*:

| Goal | Load | Rep range |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Strength** | Heavy (~≥80% 1RM) | ~1–5 |
| **Hypertrophy** | Broad (~30–85% 1RM) | ~6–15 (works ~5–30) |
| **Muscular endurance** | Light | 15–30+ |

The key finding: **hypertrophy is similar across a wide load range *when sets are
taken close to failure*** (Schoenfeld et al., 2017) — because of motor-unit
recruitment (previous chapter). **Strength, however, favours heavy loads**, since
it's specific to lifting heavy. Practically, most people build size most
comfortably in the **~6–15 rep range** (enough reps to accumulate volume, heavy
enough not to take forever) and train **strength in the ~1–5 range.**

## Effort — how close to failure

Growth requires **effortful** sets, but not necessarily *all-out* ones. Proximity
to failure is measured in **reps in reserve (RIR)** — how many more you could have
done.

- Train most sets at about **1–3 RIR** (stopping 1–3 reps short of failure).
- The evidence shows **stopping a couple of reps shy matches training to outright
  failure** for hypertrophy in trained lifters — with **less fatigue** — and the
  benefit of going *closer* than ~2 RIR is small.
- **Failure isn't required**, and taken to excess it's costly to recover from. A
  reasonable rule: keep 1–3 in reserve on most work, save true failure for the
  last set of an exercise or safer isolation moves.
- The opposite mistake is more common: **stopping too early** (5+ RIR), which
  under-stimulates growth. Effort has to be real.

## Frequency — mostly a way to fit volume

**Frequency** (sessions per muscle per week) turns out to matter little *on its
own*: when weekly **volume is equated**, training a muscle once or three times a
week produces similar growth (Schoenfeld et al., 2019). Its real value is
**logistical** — spreading volume across **2+ sessions per muscle per week** lets
you do more total quality sets without any single session becoming a slog. So
"**train each muscle ~2×/week**" is a good default, but as a *volume-distribution*
tool, not a magic number.

## Rest periods — don't rush the big lifts

Short rest was long recommended to "maximise the pump," but the evidence points the
other way: **longer rest periods (~2–3 minutes) produce better strength and
hypertrophy** than short ones (~1 min), because they let you keep load and reps —
i.e. **volume** — high across sets (Schoenfeld et al., 2016).

- **Compound lifts:** rest **~2–3 minutes.**
- **Isolation work:** **~1–2 minutes** is usually fine.
- Don't sacrifice the quality of your hard sets to save a couple of minutes.

## Range of motion & tempo

- **Range of motion:** train through a **full ROM** as the default — controlling the
  stretched (lengthened) position in particular. Full ROM at least matches, and
  often beats, partial-range work for growth; "**lengthened partials**" (emphasising
  the stretched portion) are a newer idea that performs *about the same* as full
  ROM, not clearly better. Use full range; don't cut depth to add weight.
- **Tempo:** **control the lowering (eccentric)** — roughly 1–3 seconds — and lift
  with intent on the way up. There's **no benefit to super-slow grinding**; it just
  cuts the load and volume you can handle.

## The takeaway

- **Volume is king**: aim **~10–20 hard sets per muscle per week** (Schoenfeld
  2017) — the highest-value dial.
- **Load has a wide effective range for size** (~5–30 reps near failure); **strength
  needs heavy** (1–5 reps).
- Keep most sets at **1–3 RIR** — effortful, but **failure isn't required**.
- **Frequency (~2×/week)** mainly distributes volume; **rest 2–3 min on compounds**;
  use **full ROM** and a **controlled eccentric.**
