# Vitamins 101

Vitamins are **micronutrients** — you need them in milligram or microgram
amounts, but you can't live without them. They don't supply energy; they're the
*cofactors and catalysts* that let everything else work: energy metabolism, oxygen
transport, bone building, immune function, and more. This chapter is the map; the
next puts athletic priorities on it.

## The two families

The single most useful distinction is **solubility**, because it governs storage,
dosing, and toxicity risk.

### Fat-soluble: A, D, E, K

- Stored in body fat and the liver, so you build **reserves** and don't need them
  daily.
- Absorbed **with dietary fat** — another reason not to eat too low-fat.
- Because they're stored, **excess can accumulate to toxic levels** (especially A
  and D from supplements). More is genuinely not better here.

### Water-soluble: the B-complex and vitamin C

- Not stored to any real degree (B12 is the exception — the liver holds years'
  worth) — so you need a **regular supply**.
- Excess is largely **excreted in urine**, so acute toxicity is rare — though
  megadoses of a few (B6, niacin) can still cause harm.
- The **eight B vitamins** are the workhorses of **energy metabolism** — they
  help convert food into usable ATP. **Vitamin C** is an antioxidant and is
  needed to build collagen.

## What the key vitamins do

| Vitamin | Main roles | Common food sources |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **A** | Vision, immune function, skin | Liver, dairy, eggs; beta-carotene in orange/green veg |
| **D** | Calcium absorption, bone, immune, muscle | Sunlight, fatty fish, fortified foods |
| **E** | Antioxidant, membrane protection | Nuts, seeds, vegetable oils |
| **K** | Blood clotting, bone metabolism | Leafy greens, fermented foods |
| **B-complex** | Energy metabolism, red blood cells, nerves | Whole grains, meat, eggs, legumes |
| **B12** | Nerve function, red blood cells, DNA | Animal foods only (a vegan priority) |
| **Folate (B9)** | DNA synthesis, cell division | Leafy greens, legumes |
| **C** | Collagen, antioxidant, iron absorption | Citrus, peppers, berries |

## Deficiency vs. toxicity — the U-shaped curve

The intuition that "if a little is good, more is better" fails badly with
vitamins. The relationship between intake and health is **U-shaped**: too little
causes deficiency disease, but too much causes its *own* problems.

- **Vitamin A** — excess causes liver damage and, in pregnancy, birth defects.
- **Vitamin D** — megadoses cause hypercalcaemia (dangerous blood calcium).
- **Vitamin B6** — chronic high doses cause nerve damage (neuropathy).
- **Niacin (B3)** — high doses cause flushing and, long-term, liver stress.

This is why **food-based intake is self-limiting and safe**, while
**supplements are where toxicity happens.** The reference points to know:

- **RDA** — the intake that meets the needs of ~most healthy people.
- **UL (Tolerable Upper Intake Level)** — the ceiling above which risk rises.

Staying between the RDA and the UL is the whole game.

## Do you need a multivitamin?

For someone eating a varied, whole-food diet with enough calories, a multivitamin
is **insurance, not a fix** — the evidence for broad health benefits in
well-fed populations is weak. It becomes more useful when intake is restricted:
low-calorie diets, limited food variety, certain vegan/vegetarian patterns, or
specific life stages. The next chapter covers the nutrients athletes most often
actually fall short on — where targeted supplementation beats a scattershot
multi.

## The takeaway

- **Fat-soluble (A, D, E, K)** are stored and can build up to toxic levels;
  **water-soluble (B, C)** need regular topping up and are mostly excreted.
- B vitamins run **energy metabolism**; the fat-soluble ones handle structure and
  signalling.
- Intake follows a **U-shaped curve** — deficiency *and* excess both harm; stay
  between **RDA and UL**.
- **Food is self-limiting**; a multivitamin is insurance, and **targeted** beats
  broad.
