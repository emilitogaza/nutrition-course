# Food vs Supplements

This is the question the whole vitamins section builds toward: should you get your
micronutrients from **food** or from **supplements**? The honest answer is
"food first, supplements to fill specific gaps" — and the reasons why are more
interesting than the slogan.

## Why food usually wins

### The food-matrix effect

A whole food is not a list of nutrients — it's a **package** in which the parts
interact. An orange delivers vitamin C alongside fibre, flavonoids, potassium, and
water; the vitamin C behaves differently in that context than as an isolated
tablet. This **food-matrix effect** is why:

- Nutrients from food are often **better absorbed and used** than the same
  nutrient in isolation.
- **Synergies** matter — vitamin C boosts iron absorption; dietary fat unlocks
  fat-soluble vitamins; vitamin D and K2 work together on calcium handling.
- Whole foods are **self-limiting** — it's very hard to overdose on a nutrient by
  eating real food, but easy with a pill.

### Isolated nutrients keep disappointing in trials

Time and again, a nutrient that looks protective in *food* fails or even backfires
as an *isolated high-dose supplement*:

- **Beta-carotene** supplements *increased* lung cancer risk in smokers (the
  CARET and ATBC trials) — while beta-carotene-rich vegetables are protective.
- High-dose **vitamin E** supplements showed no benefit and some harm in large
  trials.
- **Antioxidant megadoses** (vitamin C and E) around training can actually
  **blunt training adaptations** — the exercise-induced oxidative stress is part
  of the *signal* that drives adaptation, and mopping it all up dulls the
  response.

The lesson isn't "supplements are bad" — it's that a nutrient's benefit in food
doesn't transfer to a megadose pill, and sometimes reverses.

## When supplements genuinely make sense

Supplements earn their place in specific, defensible situations:

1. **Correcting a diagnosed deficiency** — vitamin D, iron, B12. Test, then treat.
2. **Diet excludes a nutrient class** — **B12** on a vegan diet is non-negotiable;
   omega-3 without fish; iodine, zinc, calcium on restrictive diets.
3. **Requirements exceed practical intake** — e.g. very high training loads, or
   life stages like pregnancy (folate).
4. **Convenience for a nutrient you'd otherwise miss** — vitamin D in winter, a
   fish-oil cap if you don't eat fish.
5. **Ergogenic aids** — creatine, caffeine and friends aren't "filling a gap";
   they're performance tools with their own evidence base (next section).

## How to supplement intelligently

- **Test where you can.** Vitamin D and iron especially — guessing risks both
  under- and over-dosing.
- **Target, don't scatter.** A specific corrected deficiency beats a shotgun
  mega-multi.
- **Respect the upper limits (UL).** Fat-soluble vitamins and minerals like iron
  and zinc are the ones that accumulate.
- **Mind the timing around training.** Skip big antioxidant megadoses right
  around workouts if adaptation is the goal; they're fine away from training.
- **Buy quality.** Supplements are loosely regulated; look for **third-party
  testing** (NSF Certified for Sport, Informed Sport) — this also matters for
  drug-tested athletes, since contamination with banned substances is a real risk.

## The takeaway

- **Food first.** The **matrix**, synergy, and self-limiting nature of whole food
  beat isolated pills for general micronutrition.
- Isolated **megadoses** often disappoint or backfire — including blunting
  training adaptations.
- Supplement for **specific reasons**: diagnosed deficiency, an excluded nutrient
  class, high demand, or as an **ergogenic aid**.
- **Test, target, respect the UL, and buy third-party-tested** products.
