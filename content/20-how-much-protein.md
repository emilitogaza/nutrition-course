# How Much Protein?

Protein is the most-studied macronutrient in sports nutrition, and the numbers
have converged enough to give clear answers. This chapter is the practical dose
guide; the next two explain *why* — the amino acid mechanism and protein quality.

## The daily total

The old **RDA of 0.8 g/kg** is a floor to prevent deficiency in sedentary
people — not a target for anyone training. For athletes and anyone building or
preserving muscle, the evidence supports far more.

| Goal | Protein (per kg body weight/day) |
| --- | --- |
| Sedentary minimum (RDA) | 0.8 g |
| General health / active | 1.2–1.6 g |
| Building muscle / most athletes | **1.6–2.2 g** |
| Fat loss while preserving muscle | **2.0–2.4 g** (up to ~2.7 g/kg *lean* mass) |
| Lean athletes, aggressive cut | up to ~3.1 g/kg fat-free mass |

The landmark meta-analysis here is **Morton et al. (2018)**, which pooled 49
studies and found resistance-training gains in muscle plateaued at around
**1.6 g/kg/day**, with a reasonable upper bound near 2.2 to account for
individual variation. Eating well above that isn't harmful for healthy people —
it's just not doing much extra for muscle. In a **calorie deficit**, aim to the
higher end: protein needs rise when energy is short, because the body is more
tempted to burn amino acids for fuel.

> **Rule of thumb.** For most trained people, **~2 g/kg** (roughly 0.9 g/lb) is a
> simple, defensible target that covers muscle, satiety, and a margin for error.

## Per-meal dose

Total intake matters most — but *how you distribute it* has a smaller,
independent effect. Each meal triggers a burst of muscle protein synthesis, and
that response **saturates**: past a certain dose, extra protein in a single
sitting is oxidised or used elsewhere rather than building more muscle.

- The muscle-building response per meal maxes out around **0.4 g/kg**, or roughly
  **20–40 g** of high-quality protein for most people.
- Larger athletes, older adults, and lower-quality protein sources push toward
  the top of that range (leucine and "anabolic resistance" with age — see the
  next chapter).

## Distribution: 3–5 doses

Because the per-meal response saturates and then resets, spreading protein across
the day beats loading it all at once. **Areta et al. (2013)** compared the same
total protein split into 8 small, 4 moderate, or 2 large doses over 12 hours —
the **4 × 20 g** pattern produced the highest sustained synthesis.

Practical target: **3–5 protein-containing meals**, each with ~0.4 g/kg,
spaced 3–5 hours apart. A dose of slow-digesting protein (e.g. casein or a
mixed-food meal) before an overnight fast is a reasonable bonus, not a
requirement.

## Is high protein safe?

For people with **healthy kidneys**, high-protein diets do **not** cause kidney
damage — this is a persistent myth from misapplied advice for people who already
have renal disease. Reviews out to ~3 g/kg in athletes show no harm to kidney or
bone health; higher protein is, if anything, *protective* of bone. The real
limits are practical: appetite, digestive comfort, and crowding out other foods.

*(If you have existing kidney disease, protein does need medical supervision —
this is the exception, not the rule.)*

## The takeaway

- Target **1.6–2.2 g/kg/day**; go higher (**2.0–2.4+**) when dieting.
- Per meal, ~**0.4 g/kg (20–40 g)** maximises the building response.
- Spread it over **3–5 meals** for a modest additional edge.
- High protein is **safe for healthy kidneys** — the concern is a myth for the
  general population.
