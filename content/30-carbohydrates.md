# Carbohydrates & Performance

Carbohydrate is the most misunderstood macro. It's been demonised in diet culture
and worshipped in endurance sport, and the truth is boringly practical: for
anyone training hard, **carbohydrate availability is one of the biggest levers on
performance** — and for anyone else, carbs are simply a fuel you scale to your
activity.

## Glycogen: your fast fuel tank

Carbs digest to glucose, which is stored as **glycogen** — a branched chain of
glucose molecules held in muscle (~300–500 g) and liver (~100 g). Muscle glycogen
powers the muscle it's stored in; liver glycogen maintains blood sugar for the
brain and the rest of the body.

Glycogen is the dominant fuel for **moderate-to-high-intensity** work. As it
depletes — the marathoner's "wall," the lifter's flat final sets — power and
endurance fall. Fat can't be burned fast enough to fully replace it at high
intensity. This is the core reason carbs matter for performance: **they're the
only fuel that supports hard efforts at full output.**

## How much carbohydrate?

Unlike protein (set by body weight) or fat (set by a health floor), carbs scale
with **training load**. Sports-nutrition guidelines put it roughly at:

| Training demand | Carbohydrate (g/kg/day) |
| --- | --- |
| Light / skill-based | 3–5 g |
| Moderate (~1 hr/day) | 5–7 g |
| High (1–3 hr/day endurance) | 6–10 g |
| Very high (>4–5 hr/day) | 8–12 g |

Strength and physique athletes usually live in the lower-to-middle range and use
whatever carbs are "left" after protein and fat are set. Endurance athletes,
especially in heavy blocks, need the high end to refill glycogen day to day.

## Glycemic index — useful, but overrated

The **glycemic index (GI)** ranks how fast a carb raises blood glucose. It gets
far more attention than it deserves for most people, because:

- Foods are eaten **in mixed meals** — fat, protein, and fibre all blunt the
  glucose response, so a food's GI in isolation rarely reflects what happens on
  your plate.
- For a healthy, active person, the **total amount** of carbohydrate matters far
  more than its speed.

GI *does* become useful in two spots: **fast (high-GI) carbs** are ideal for
rapid refuelling around and during long training, and **slower, higher-fibre
carbs** are more filling and steadier for everyday meals, especially when
dieting. Think of it as a timing tool, not a good/bad label.

## Carbohydrate periodisation

Advanced athletes don't eat the same carbs every day — they **match intake to the
day's demand** and sometimes deliberately manipulate availability:

- **Fuel for the work required.** More carbs around hard/long sessions, fewer on
  rest or light days. This supports performance without unnecessary surplus.
- **"Train low" strategies.** Occasionally training with low glycogen (e.g. fasted
  or after depletion) can amplify some endurance *adaptations* — more
  mitochondria, better fat oxidation — via cell-signalling pathways (AMPK,
  PGC-1α). The trade-off is you can't train as hard, so it's used selectively,
  not as a default.
- **Carb loading** before endurance events (~10–12 g/kg for 1–2 days) supersizes
  glycogen stores to delay the wall.

For most people the simple version is enough: **eat more carbohydrate on hard
training days and less on easy ones.**

## The takeaway

- Carbs fuel **high-intensity performance** via **glycogen** — fat can't replace
  them at full output.
- Intake scales with **training load** (roughly 3–12 g/kg/day).
- **Glycemic index** is a timing tool, not a moral ranking; total carbs matter
  more.
- Advanced athletes **periodise** carbs — fuel the hard days, ease off the easy
  ones.
