# Chronic Tightness, Guarding & the Nervous System

Here's a story many people recognise: you stretched once — an inner-thigh or
hamstring stretch — and something **tweaked and tightened up in a bad way.** Years
later it's *still* tight. Stretching it feels **stuck**, like the muscle refuses to
lengthen or actively resists. This chapter is about what's really going on, because
the usual advice ("just stretch it more") is often exactly what keeps it stuck.

> **Education, not diagnosis.** Persistent, years-old symptoms deserve a hands-on
> assessment from a physiotherapist — partly to rule out a nerve or joint driver
> (below), partly because a tailored loading plan beats generic advice. Read this as
> the *framework*, then get it looked at.

## "Tight" usually isn't "short"

The first reframe: a muscle that *feels* tight is very often **not** anatomically
short or shortened by scar tissue. In your inner leg, the usual suspects are the
**adductors** (adductor longus/magnus) and **gracilis** — and a chronically "tight"
one is usually being held that way by your **nervous system**, not glued short. The
common drivers:

- **Protective guarding / elevated tone.** After a painful event, the nervous
  system can decide a region is vulnerable and keep the muscle **partly switched on
  to protect it** — a guard it never fully stood down from. The muscle *feels*
  tight because it's being held slightly contracted, not because it lost length.
- **Reduced stretch tolerance, not reduced length.** When flexibility improves with
  stretching, research shows it's mostly the nervous system **allowing more range**
  — a change in *sensation and tolerance*, not the tissue getting physically longer
  (Weppler & Magnusson, 2010). A muscle that feels "stuck" is often your protective
  system **capping the range** well before any true tissue limit.
- **An old, under-loaded strain.** A low-grade tear that healed but was never
  **re-strengthened through range** stays weak and feels vulnerable there, so the
  system guards it.
- **A nerve, not the muscle.** Sometimes the "muscle stretch" is really tensioning
  an irritated **nerve** (the obturator or sciatic nerve run through this area).
  Nerves hate being stretched and will resist hard — and this responds to different
  treatment than a muscle.

## What actually happens when you stretch

To your question — *does the body defend itself, and then stay guarded?* Yes, and
here's the machinery:

- **Muscle spindles** inside the muscle sense stretch *and how fast* it's applied.
  Stretch quickly or far and they fire the **stretch reflex (myotatic reflex)** — a
  reflexive **contraction to resist the stretch.** Your body's default response to a
  strong or sudden stretch is literally to *push back against it.*
- **Golgi tendon organs**, at the muscle–tendon junction, sense **tension**. Under
  sustained higher tension they trigger **autogenic inhibition** — a *relaxation*
  signal. This is the escape hatch that lets a muscle "give" after a long, calm
  hold (and the basis of contract-relax stretching below).
- **The brain appraises threat.** This is the key part. If your nervous system
  *remembers* that this stretch once caused pain, it can **pre-emptively increase
  guarding** — bracing before you even reach the old limit. Pain and the *expectation*
  of pain both crank up the protective response.

Put those together and you get the "stuck" feeling: you pull into the stretch, the
system reads **threat** (memory + speed + discomfort), the stretch reflex and
guarding **resist**, it hurts a bit, which confirms the threat — and you're in a
tug-of-war the muscle is designed to win.

## Why it persists for years

A one-off tweak becomes a years-long tightness through a self-reinforcing loop —
the **pain–tension–fear–avoidance cycle**:

1. The original stretch hurt and the muscle guarded (sensible, short-term).
2. Because it stayed sensitive, you either **avoided** loading it or kept
   **yanking** on it — both keep the alarm up.
3. Every painful stretch **re-teaches** the nervous system "this range is
   dangerous," so it guards *earlier* next time.
4. The range is never **re-earned** with calm, progressive, non-threatening
   exposure — so the guard becomes the new normal, sometimes long after any tissue
   has healed (this is **sensitisation**: the system turns up its own gain).

Nothing here requires ongoing tissue damage. The tissue can be fine; the *setting*
on the protection system is what's stuck.

## How to make the body feel safe enough to let go

The whole strategy flips from **forcing** to **reassuring**. You're not tearing
scar tissue apart — you're convincing a protective nervous system that the range is
safe again. In rough order:

1. **Stop the aggressive, painful stretching.** Every hard, painful yank is a
   *threat* input that deepens the guard. Painful stretching is the thing most
   likely to keep it stuck. Ease off first.
2. **Go gentle, calm, and frequent.** Stretch **into mild tension, never pain**,
   hold in a relaxed and *slow-breathing* state, and do it **often in small
   doses** rather than occasionally and hard. A downregulated (parasympathetic)
   nervous system lets go; a braced, stressed one clamps. When you're calm, the
   guard softens.
3. **Load it, don't just stretch it.** This is the biggest lever, and the most
   under-used. **Strength through range** is usually *more* effective than passive
   stretching for chronic "tightness," because it builds capacity, extensibility,
   *and* confidence at the same time. For the adductors specifically, the
   **Copenhagen adduction exercise** builds eccentric adductor strength and has
   strong evidence in groin problems (Harøy et al., 2019); pair it with **loaded
   lengthening** (e.g. controlled Cossack squats, adductor sliders) added
   **gradually**. Strong-through-range tissue stops needing to be guarded.
4. **Use contract-relax (PNF).** Gently **contract** the tight muscle for a few
   seconds against light resistance, then **relax** and ease slightly further. This
   recruits **autogenic inhibition** to lower the protective tone — a direct way to
   ask the nervous system to stand down.
5. **Graded exposure and patience.** The nervous system updates through **repeated,
   safe, non-painful experiences** over weeks and months. Consistency beats
   intensity every time. Each calm, painless rep is a small vote for "this is
   fine."
6. **Rule in/out the nerve.** If the sensation is more *nervy* — tingling, a
   distinct "different" quality, or it changes when you move your **back, neck, or
   ankle** — the driver may be neural, and **nerve glides/flossing** plus a physio
   assessment are the route, not more muscle stretching.
7. **Support the whole system.** Chronic **stress and poor sleep** keep the entire
   nervous system in a guarded, sensitised state (see the sleep chapter). Lowering
   global threat — sleep, stress, and *not fearing* the movement — makes the local
   tissue easier to unwind.

The mindset that ties it together: **safety, not force.** You resolve a guarded
muscle by making it feel safe — gently, repeatedly, and with load that rebuilds
trust — not by winning a fight against your own protective reflexes. And because
this one is years old, get a physiotherapist to confirm the driver and progress the
plan; that's the fastest route to actually unwinding it.

## The takeaway

- Chronic "tightness" is usually **not short tissue** — it's **protective guarding
  and reduced stretch tolerance** held by the nervous system (Weppler & Magnusson,
  2010), sometimes an under-loaded old strain or an irritated **nerve**.
- When you stretch, the body **defends**: **muscle spindles** fire a resisting
  **stretch reflex**, and the brain **guards harder** if it expects pain — that's
  the "stuck" feeling.
- It persists via a **pain–tension–fear–avoidance loop** and **sensitisation**,
  often long after any tissue has healed.
- Resolve it by shifting from **force to safety**: stop painful stretching, go
  **gentle/calm/frequent**, and above all **load it through range** (e.g.
  Copenhagen adduction for adductors) plus **contract-relax** and patience.
- Years-old and nervy symptoms deserve a **physiotherapist** — to check for a nerve
  or joint driver and build a graded plan.
