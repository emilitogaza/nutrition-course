# Fuelling Around Training

This chapter is the practical synthesis — how to actually eat around a session,
pulling together protein distribution, carbohydrate periodisation, timing, and
hydration into a workable plan. The right approach depends on **what you're
training for** and **how long and hard** the session is.

## The universal principles

Whatever the sport:

1. **Arrive fuelled and hydrated** for hard sessions — glycogen topped up, not
   parched.
2. **Protein in the hours around training** — before, after, or both; the window
   is wide.
3. **Match carbohydrate to the session's demand** — more for long/intense work,
   less for short/easy.
4. **Refuel and rehydrate afterward**, with urgency scaled to how soon you train
   again.

## Strength & physique training

For a typical lifting session (~45–90 min), the details are forgiving:

- **Before:** a mixed meal with protein and carbs **1–3 hours prior**, or a
  smaller protein+carb snack closer in. Training fasted is fine if you prefer it —
  just don't expect peak performance on heavy work.
- **During:** water is enough. Intra-workout carbs offer little for a single
  short session unless it's very long or you're already glycogen-depleted.
- **After:** protein (~**20–40 g**) within a couple of hours, plus carbs to
  refill glycogen and a normal meal schedule. No need to rush the shaker.

The biggest levers for lifters are **total daily protein** and **overall
calories**, not micro-managed timing.

## Endurance training

Here fuelling becomes genuinely performance-critical, especially the **during**
phase.

### Intra-workout carbs — the highest-impact timing strategy

For efforts beyond ~**60–90 minutes**, taking in carbohydrate *during* exercise
clearly improves performance by sparing glycogen and maintaining blood glucose:

| Duration | Carbohydrate target |
| --- | --- |
| 30–75 min | Small amounts / mouth rinse may help |
| 1–2.5 h | ~**30–60 g/hour** |
| > 2.5–3 h | up to ~**90 g/hour** using **multiple transportable carbs** |

That top figure requires a **glucose + fructose mix** (roughly 2:1), because
glucose and fructose use *different intestinal transporters* — combining them lets
you absorb more per hour than glucose alone (which caps near 60 g/h). This
"multiple transportable carbohydrates" trick is a cornerstone of modern endurance
fuelling. The gut can be **trained** to tolerate higher intakes with practice.

### Around endurance sessions

- **Before:** carbs are king — a carb-rich meal 1–3 h prior for long/hard work.
- **After:** with another session soon, refuel aggressively (~**1–1.2 g/kg carbs
  per hour** for the first few hours) plus protein. With a full day to recover,
  normal meals suffice.

## A quick decision guide

- **Short, easy session, plenty of recovery time:** don't overthink it — normal
  meals and water.
- **Long or intense session:** fuel before, **carbs during**, refuel after.
- **Two sessions in one day:** treat between-session refuelling as a priority —
  fast carbs plus protein, fluid plus sodium.
- **Fat-loss phase:** the same principles apply within your lower calorie budget —
  prioritise protein and place most carbs around training.

## The takeaway

- **Strength training:** timing is forgiving — protein around the session, total
  daily protein and calories matter most.
- **Endurance:** fuelling **during** long efforts is high-impact — **30–90
  g/hour**, using **glucose+fructose** blends to exceed 60 g/h.
- Scale **post-session refuelling** to how soon you train again.
- **Train your gut** to tolerate the carbs your event demands.
