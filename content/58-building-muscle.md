# Building Muscle — The Gaining Phase

The fat-loss chapters get most of the attention, but the other half of body
composition is **adding muscle on purpose**. This is the "gaining phase" — a
period of eating enough to support growth. Done well, it's a slow, deliberate
build; done badly, it's just getting fat and calling it bulking.

## Why you need a surplus (usually)

Building meaningful muscle is *energetically expensive* — new tissue costs
calories to synthesise and to carry. While beginners and returning trainees can
grow at maintenance or even in a small deficit (see body recomposition), most
people past that stage build fastest with a modest **energy surplus**: enough
extra fuel that the body is comfortable spending on new tissue rather than
conserving.

The catch: **a surplus adds fat and muscle together.** You cannot force the ratio
to be all-muscle, but you *can* keep the fat share small by keeping the surplus
small.

## How big a surplus?

Bigger is not better. Above a modest surplus, muscle growth doesn't speed up —
only fat gain does. The evidence-aligned target:

| Surplus | Rate of gain | Best for |
| --- | --- | --- |
| ~**5–10%** over maintenance (≈ 150–300 kcal) | ~0.25–0.5% body weight/week | Intermediate & advanced lifters |
| ~**10–20%** (≈ 300–500 kcal) | ~0.5–1% body weight/week | Beginners, hard-gainers, returning trainees |

A telling study: Garthe et al. (2013) had athletes gain weight fast versus slow.
The faster group put on **more total weight — but a much larger share of it was
fat**, with no extra lean gain to show for it. The lesson is blunt: past a point,
extra calories build fat, not muscle.

> **Rule of thumb.** For most trained lifters, aim to gain roughly **0.25–0.5% of
> body weight per week** (≈ 0.5–1 kg per month). Slower feels frustrating; it's
> also what keeps a gaining phase *lean*.

## Set the macros for growth

The framework is the same "protein and fat as floors, carbs fill the rest" from
the macronutrients chapter — but now the "rest" is generous.

1. **Protein — same as always.** Muscle needs amino acids, not extra protein
   beyond the usual **1.6–2.2 g/kg** (Morton et al., 2018). More protein is not
   what drives a bulk; adequate protein *plus* the surplus energy is.
2. **Carbohydrate does the heavy lifting.** This is where most of the surplus
   should go. Carbs fuel harder, higher-volume training — and training volume is
   the real driver of growth — while helping recovery between sessions.
3. **Fat at a sensible floor** (~0.5–1 g/kg) for hormones, then let carbs take the
   surplus.

## Train to *earn* the calories

A surplus is permission to grow, not a growth signal by itself. The signal is
**progressive resistance training** — adding reps, load, or quality sets over
time. Without it, extra calories have nowhere to go but fat. The gaining phase and
a well-run training program are a package; neither works alone.

## Why "dirty bulking" backfires

The old advice to "eat big to get big" — huge surpluses, anything goes — reliably
produces more fat than muscle. The costs compound:

- The extra fat gained has to be **cut off later**, and long, large cuts
  themselves risk muscle.
- You spend the year less lean, which may **worsen nutrient partitioning** (leaner
  bodies tend to send a surplus toward muscle more readily).
- It feels productive because the scale moves fast — but the *mirror* and the
  *tape* tell the real story.

## When to stop gaining and cut

A gaining phase isn't forever. Common cues to switch to a cut:

- Fat gain has outpaced the muscle you're adding (rate crept too high, or the
  phase ran long).
- You've reached a body-fat level you're no longer comfortable with.
- Strength progress has stalled for reasons unrelated to fuel.

The classic cycle — **lean bulk to build, controlled cut to reveal** — beats
staying in either phase indefinitely.

## The takeaway

- Most lifters past the beginner stage build best in a **modest surplus** — about
  **5–20% over maintenance**, scaled to experience.
- Target a slow rate: **~0.25–0.5% body weight/week**. Faster mostly adds **fat**
  (Garthe et al., 2013), not muscle.
- **Protein stays 1.6–2.2 g/kg**; put the surplus mostly into **carbs** to fuel
  training, which is the actual growth driver.
- **Dirty bulking backfires** — you just cut it off later. Keep it lean, then
  alternate build and reveal.
