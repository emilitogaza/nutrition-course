# Diet Frameworks Compared

Every few years a diet arrives claiming a **unique mechanism** — a hormonal
loophole, a metabolic switch, an ancestral truth. Most of them work for some
people. Almost none of them work for the reason claimed.

This chapter is the comparison. The goal isn't to crown a winner — it's to give
you enough understanding of what each framework actually does that you can pick
one deliberately, or discard the question entirely.

## The finding that frames everything

The most important trial in this area is **DIETFITS** (Gardner et al., *JAMA*
2018): 609 adults randomised for **12 months** to a healthy low-fat or a healthy
low-carbohydrate diet, with substantial ongoing dietary support and no calorie
targets.

**Result:** both groups lost roughly the same — about **5–6 kg** — with no
significant difference between them. The study also pre-registered two fashionable
hypotheses: that **genotype** and **baseline insulin secretion** would predict who
did better on which diet. **Neither did.**

The second finding is arguably more instructive. By the end of the year, **neither
group was actually following its diet**. The "low-carb" group averaged ~132 g of
carbohydrate a day; the "low-fat" group averaged ~57 g of fat. Both had drifted
toward a moderate, whole-food diet — and both lost weight.

More recent meta-analyses find carbohydrate restriction produces a modest
**short-term** advantage (around **−1.75 kg**, with ketogenic versions around
**−3.36 kg**), but that this **largely disappears by one to two years**. A good
chunk of the early difference is **glycogen-bound water**, not fat.

> **The synthesis.** Once **calories and protein are matched**, macronutrient
> ratio has little effect on fat loss. Diet frameworks are **adherence tools** —
> different packaging for the same underlying mechanism. The best diet is the one
> that gets *you* to eat an appropriate amount of decent food, repeatedly, for
> years.

That's not a dismissal. Adherence *is* the hard problem. A framework that solves
it for you is genuinely valuable — just not for the reason it advertises.

## The frameworks

### Ketogenic

**What it is:** carbohydrate below ~20–50 g/day, forcing the liver to produce
**ketone bodies** as an alternative fuel. Typically ~70–75% of calories from fat.

**What it does well:** powerful **appetite suppression** for many people (which is
how it creates a deficit), rapid early weight loss, strong glycaemic control in
type 2 diabetes, and a genuine therapeutic role in **drug-resistant epilepsy**.
Some people report notable improvements in mental clarity and cravings.

**What it does badly:** **high-intensity performance.** Anything glycolytic —
sprints, intervals, hard team sport, high-rep lifting — depends on muscle
glycogen, and keto reduces it. Louise Burke's work in elite race walkers found
that ketogenic adaptation **increased fat oxidation but impaired exercise
economy**, and performance in the highest-intensity trials suffered. Fat adaptation
is real; it just doesn't pay for what it costs in the events most people train
for.

**Also worth knowing:** the LDL response is highly variable and in some lean,
athletic people it rises dramatically. Fibre intake is easy to wreck. The first
1–2 weeks bring "keto flu" — fatigue, headaches, cramps — mostly sodium and fluid
loss, largely fixable with salt.

**Suits:** people who genuinely feel better without carbs, those with strong
appetite dysregulation, and specific clinical indications.
**Doesn't suit:** most athletes, anyone doing high-intensity work.

### Low-carb (non-ketogenic)

**What it is:** roughly 50–130 g of carbohydrate a day. Carbs reduced, not
eliminated.

Retains most of keto's appetite and blood-sugar benefits with far fewer
performance costs and much better adherence. For someone who feels better on fewer
carbs, this is almost always the better version — it's the setting people
*actually maintain*, as DIETFITS demonstrated by accident.

### Low-fat

**What it is:** fat under ~30% (sometimes under 20%) of calories.

Extensively studied, works about as well as low-carb. The main caution is the
**hormonal floor** covered in the dietary fat chapter — driving fat below ~20% of
calories is associated with reduced testosterone, and it compromises fat-soluble
vitamin absorption. Very-low-fat whole-food versions (Ornish, Esselstyn) have
cardiovascular evidence but demand high commitment.

### Mediterranean

**What it is:** olive oil as the primary fat, high vegetable and legume intake,
fish, nuts, whole grains, moderate dairy, limited red and processed meat.

**This is the framework with the strongest evidence for hard health outcomes**, not
just weight. **PREDIMED** randomised over 7 000 people at high cardiovascular risk
and found roughly a **30% reduction in major cardiovascular events** on a
Mediterranean diet supplemented with extra-virgin olive oil or nuts. It also has
supporting evidence for cognitive decline, depression, and all-cause mortality.

It's not primarily a weight-loss diet, it's flexible enough to fit almost any
athletic requirement, and it has essentially no downside. **If you want one
default recommendation, this is it.**

### The Nordic diet

**What it is:** the regional equivalent — **rapeseed oil** instead of olive,
**rye and oats**, berries, root vegetables, cabbage, fatty fish, game, legumes,
low-fat dairy.

Studies of the **Healthy Nordic Food Index** show benefits comparable to the
Mediterranean pattern: lower cardiovascular risk, improved blood lipids, lower
mortality. It's the basis of the **Nordic Nutrition Recommendations 2023** and
Livsmedelsverket's advice.

Its real advantage in Sweden is **practical**: the ingredients are local,
in-season, affordable, and already in the shops. A Mediterranean diet in
Östergötland in February requires imported produce; the Nordic version doesn't.

### Paleo

**What it is:** meat, fish, eggs, vegetables, fruit, nuts. Excludes grains,
legumes, dairy, and refined sugar.

**The reasoning is weak** — the "ancestral" premise doesn't survive contact with
the archaeological record, human diets varied enormously by region, and human
genetics have demonstrably adapted since (lactase persistence, amylase copy
number).

**The practice is decent anyway.** Cutting refined food and increasing protein and
vegetables works. The downsides are the unnecessary exclusions: **legumes are
among the healthiest and cheapest foods available**, dairy is an excellent protein
source, and removing grains makes hitting carbohydrate needs harder for athletes.

### Carnivore

**What it is:** animal foods only. Zero plants.

**This one warrants an explicit caution.** There is **no long-term outcome data**
at all — the evidence base is testimonials and one survey of self-selected
enthusiasts. It provides **zero fibre**, no vitamin C from food, and an extremely
high saturated fat load, and reported **LDL increases are often dramatic**. The
elimination of all plant foods removes essentially every dietary factor associated
with lower chronic disease risk.

Some people report improvements, most plausibly because it's an extreme elimination
diet that removes trigger foods and ultra-processed food simultaneously. If that's
the mechanism, there are far less drastic ways to get there.

**Not recommended**, and if someone is doing it for suspected food intolerance,
that's a reason to see a gastroenterologist or dietitian.

### Whole-food plant-based / vegan

**What it is:** no animal products; emphasis on whole plant foods.

Strong evidence for **cardiovascular markers, blood pressure, and insulin
sensitivity**, and often the ethical or environmental motivation is the real
driver — which, notably, tends to make adherence *better* than diets adopted only
for appearance.

Requires deliberate planning for protein, B12, iron, omega-3, and more. That's the
subject of its own chapter.

### Intermittent fasting & time-restricted eating

**What it is:** restricting *when* you eat. Common versions: **16:8** (an 8-hour
eating window), **5:2** (two very-low-calorie days a week), alternate-day fasting.

**What the trials show:** when calories are matched, **IF performs about the same
as continuous restriction** — repeatedly, across multiple meta-analyses. The
**TREAT** trial (Lowe et al., *JAMA Internal Medicine* 2020) tested 16:8 against
three structured meals and found **no significant weight-loss advantage** — and,
notably, a **larger proportion of lean mass lost** in the fasting group.

**Where it genuinely helps:** as a **simplification tool**. Some people find "don't
eat before 12:00" far easier than counting anything. That's a real benefit.

**Where it hurts:** hitting protein targets and total calories in a compressed
window is hard, especially for athletes with high needs. Protein distribution
across the day is compromised. Training fasted at high intensity generally goes
badly. **Early** time-restricted eating (eating window earlier in the day) may
have a small metabolic edge over late windows, but it collides with most people's
social lives.

### IIFYM / flexible dieting

**What it is:** hit your calorie and macro targets; no food is off-limits.

Arguably the most honest framework, since it names the actual mechanism.
Excellent for people who like data and want no forbidden foods — the absence of
"cheating" removes a major driver of restrict-binge cycles.

**Two failure modes.** It can drift into a diet of technically-compliant junk that
meets macros and misses micronutrients and fibre entirely. And for some people,
**tracking every gram becomes its own problem** — see the next chapter.

### High-satiety / volumetrics

**What it is:** prioritise foods with low energy density and high satiety —
vegetables, fruit, lean protein, potatoes, soups, whole grains.

Rarely marketed because there's nothing to sell, but it directly targets the
actual difficulty of dieting (hunger) rather than restricting a macronutrient.
Combines well with almost anything else.

## How to actually choose

The useful questions aren't about metabolism. They're about you:

1. **What has failed before, and why?** If you've quit three diets because you
   were hungry, pick for satiety (protein, volume, fibre). If you quit because of
   social friction, don't pick something with rigid timing.
2. **Do you like structure or freedom?** Rules-based frameworks (keto, paleo, IF)
   suit people who find decisions exhausting. Flexible dieting suits people who
   resent being told no.
3. **What does your training demand?** High-intensity or team sport largely rules
   out keto. High energy needs largely rule out compressed eating windows.
4. **What can you eat in five years?** If the answer is "no chance," it's a
   temporary intervention with a rebound built in. That's not always wrong — but
   know it going in and plan the exit.
5. **What do you actually like eating?** Chronically underrated. Nobody sustains a
   diet built on food they dislike.

## Red flags in any framework

Regardless of the name on the front:

- **Eliminates a whole food group with no medical reason** — especially legumes,
  whole grains, or all plants.
- **Claims a mechanism independent of energy balance.** "Calories don't matter"
  is the reliable marker of someone selling something.
- **Requires proprietary products** — shakes, supplements, branded meals.
- **Moralises food** into "clean" and "dirty." This is where dieting turns into
  something worse.
- **Promises a fixed rate of loss** regardless of the individual.
- **Has no exit plan.** Any diet without an answer to "then what?" is a rebound
  waiting to happen.

## The takeaway

- **DIETFITS** settled the headline question: matched for support, low-fat and
  low-carb produce the **same 12-month weight loss**, and neither genotype nor
  insulin status predicts who does better.
- Once **calories and protein are matched**, macro ratio barely moves fat loss.
  Frameworks are **adherence tools**.
- **Mediterranean and Nordic** patterns have the strongest evidence for actual
  health outcomes, and in Sweden the **Nordic version is the more practical one**.
- **Keto** works for appetite and for specific clinical uses, but it **impairs
  high-intensity performance** — a poor fit for most athletes.
- **Intermittent fasting** matches continuous restriction, no better; its value is
  **simplicity**, and its cost is protein distribution.
- **Carnivore has no outcome data, zero fibre, and a dramatic LDL signal** — the
  one framework here worth actively discouraging.
- Choose on **your** failure history, structure preference, training demands, and
  what you'd happily eat in five years.
