# Supplements That Don't (or Barely) Work

The supplement industry is enormous, loosely regulated, and built on hope. For
every creatine there are dozens of products whose main active ingredient is
**marketing**. Knowing what *not* to buy saves money and protects you from
genuine risk. None of the below is a blanket "never" — but each is oversold
relative to its evidence.

## BCAAs

Covered in the amino-acids chapter, but they belong here too: **branched-chain
amino acids** were sold on the leucine/MPS story while omitting the other six
essential amino acids needed to actually build tissue. If you eat enough total
protein, they add **nothing**. Whole protein or full EAAs are strictly better.
One of the most profitable near-useless products in the category.

## "Testosterone boosters"

Tribulus, D-aspartic acid, fenugreek, ashwagandha blends, ZMA, and the rest.

- In men with **normal hormones**, these produce **little to no** meaningful rise
  in testosterone or performance in controlled trials.
- Where an ingredient *does* help, it's usually correcting a **deficiency** (e.g.
  zinc or vitamin D if you were low) — which a targeted approach handles better.
- **Ashwagandha** has some evidence for stress/cortisol and sleep, which can
  indirectly help — but it's not a testosterone drug.

If testosterone is genuinely low, that's a **medical** issue, not a supplement
one.

## Fat burners

Thermogenic "fat burners" are mostly **caffeine plus a stimulant stack** and a
long list of ingredients at doses too low to matter. The caffeine gives a real
but small metabolic bump and appetite blunting; the rest is filler. They do
**not** meaningfully "melt fat" — an energy deficit does. Some stimulant blends
carry real cardiovascular risk. **Green tea extract, L-carnitine, CLA,
raspberry ketones, garcinia** — all range from trivial to useless for fat loss.

## Glutamine

A popular recovery/immune supplement. Glutamine is genuinely important
physiologically — but the body makes plenty, and in healthy, well-fed athletes,
supplementing it does **little for muscle, recovery, or immunity**. (It has real
clinical uses in critically ill or gut-compromised patients — a different
context.)

## "Detox," "cleanses," and greens powders

- **Detox teas and cleanses** — your liver and kidneys already detoxify you;
  these products mostly deliver laxatives and lost water weight. No credible
  evidence of benefit, occasional real harm.
- **Greens powders** — not harmful, but an expensive, unstandardised way to
  half-replace vegetables. If you eat vegetables, you don't need them; if you
  don't, a proper multivitamin is cheaper and better characterised.

## The regulatory reality

In most countries (including under the US **DSHEA** framework), supplements are
**not** pre-approved for safety or efficacy the way drugs are. Manufacturers can
sell first and get challenged later. That means:

- **Label claims may not match contents** — doses off, ingredients missing, or
  undeclared additives present.
- **Proprietary blends** hide how little of each active ingredient you're getting.
- **Contamination** with banned or dangerous substances is documented and
  recurring.

## How to evaluate any new supplement

A quick filter before you spend:

1. **Is there a plausible mechanism** *and* human trials in trained people — not
   just a cell/rodent study or a mechanism story?
2. **What's the effect size** — is it big enough to notice, or just
   "statistically significant"?
3. **Is the dose in the product** the same as the dose that was studied?
4. **Who funded the studies**, and is it third-party tested?

If a product can't clear these, it's almost certainly the marketing talking.

## The takeaway

- **BCAAs, test boosters, fat burners, glutamine, detoxes, and greens powders**
  are oversold relative to their evidence.
- Fat loss comes from an **energy deficit**, not a pill.
- Supplements are **lightly regulated** — claims, doses, and purity aren't
  guaranteed.
- Filter every product by **mechanism + human evidence + correct dose + third-
  party testing**.
