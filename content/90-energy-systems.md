# Aerobic vs Anaerobic — The Energy Systems

Every muscle contraction — a max deadlift, a marathon, a walk to the fridge — is
paid for with the same currency: **ATP** (adenosine triphosphate). Your muscles
store only a few seconds of it, so the real question is *how you resupply it*. You
have three systems for that, and understanding how they hand off to each other
explains almost everything about endurance, sprinting, and the two body-signal
puzzles in a couple of chapters.

## The three energy systems

| System | Time frame | Fuel | Oxygen? | Power | Example effort |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Phosphagen (ATP–PCr)** | ~0–10 sec | Stored creatine phosphate | No | Highest | 1-rep max, short sprint, jump |
| **Glycolytic (anaerobic)** | ~10 sec–2 min | Carbohydrate → lactate | No | High | 400 m, hard set of 15, a steep hill |
| **Oxidative (aerobic)** | 2 min → hours | Carbs *and* fat | **Yes** | Lowest | Jogging, cycling, a long day |

Notice the trade-off running down the table: **power falls, capacity rises.** The
phosphagen system is explosive but empties in seconds. The aerobic system is
comparatively gentle but can run for hours. "**Anaerobic**" simply means *without
oxygen*; "**aerobic**" means *with oxygen*.

## They blend — they're not switches

The biggest misconception is that you flip from one system to another. You don't —
**all three run at once**, and *intensity and duration* set the mix.

- Roll out of bed and walk: almost entirely **aerobic**.
- Pick up the pace to a hard run: aerobic maxes out and the **glycolytic** system
  fills the gap.
- Sprint for the bus: the **phosphagen** system dominates for the first seconds,
  then glycolysis takes over as it fades.

At the **start** of any hard effort the aerobic system lags — it takes a minute or
two to ramp up oxygen delivery — so you borrow from the anaerobic systems to cover
the shortfall. That borrowing is called the **oxygen deficit**, and it's a big
reason you're breathing hard early in a climb before you've settled into a rhythm.

## The "burn," and the truth about lactate

Push into that glycolytic zone and the muscle starts to **burn**. For decades this
was blamed on "lactic acid" — an idea that's mostly wrong and worth correcting:

- **Lactate is not a waste product or a poison.** It's a *fuel*. Your heart and
  other muscles happily burn it, the liver recycles it back into glucose (the
  **Cori cycle**), and it even acts as a signalling molecule for adaptation.
- The **burn** comes from **hydrogen ions (H⁺)** and other metabolites
  accumulating faster than you can clear them — a temporary rise in acidity, not
  lactate "damaging" anything.
- Lactate is produced *all the time*, even at rest; it only **accumulates** when
  production outpaces clearance at higher intensities. That crossover point — the
  **lactate threshold** — turns out to be one of the best predictors of endurance
  performance (next chapters).
- And to kill a stubborn myth: lactate does **not** cause next-day muscle soreness.
  It's cleared within an hour or two; **DOMS** is a different, mechanical process
  entirely.

## Why this matters for you

- **Training targets specific systems.** Long easy rides build the aerobic engine;
  short all-out sprints build the phosphagen and glycolytic systems. What you
  practise is what you improve.
- **Fuel demand shifts with intensity.** The harder you go, the more you rely on
  **carbohydrate** (glycogen), because fat can't be burned fast enough at high
  outputs — exactly the point made in Carbohydrates & Performance. Easy aerobic
  work burns a higher *proportion* of fat; hard work runs on sugar.
- **The systems explain fatigue.** Different efforts fail for different reasons —
  running out of phosphocreatine, drowning in H⁺, or emptying glycogen — and that
  shapes how you pace, fuel, and recover.

## The takeaway

- All movement is powered by **ATP**, resupplied by three systems: **phosphagen**
  (seconds, explosive), **glycolytic** (up to ~2 min, the "burn"), and
  **oxidative/aerobic** (minutes to hours).
- **Anaerobic = without oxygen, aerobic = with oxygen** — and all three blend
  continuously; **intensity and duration** set the mix.
- The aerobic system **lags at the start** of hard efforts (the oxygen deficit) —
  a key reason you're winded early.
- **Lactate is a fuel, not a villain**; the burn is from **H⁺/acidity**, and
  lactate doesn't cause next-day soreness.
