# GLP-1 Medications

In under a decade, obesity treatment went from "diet and exercise, and good luck"
to drugs that reliably produce **15–25% body weight loss** — territory previously
reachable only through bariatric surgery. That's not hype; it's the largest shift
in this field in living memory.

It also raises a question this course has to answer: **if a drug handles appetite,
what's left for nutrition and training to do?** The short answer is *more, not
less* — because the thing these drugs are worst at is exactly the thing lifting
and protein are best at.

> **Not medical advice.** These are prescription medications with real
> contraindications. This chapter exists so you understand what they do and can
> have a better conversation with a doctor — not so you can self-prescribe.

## How they work

**GLP-1** is a hormone your gut already makes. L-cells in the intestine release it
after a meal, and it does four things: stimulates insulin, suppresses glucagon,
**slows gastric emptying**, and acts on **appetite centres in the hypothalamus**.

Natural GLP-1 is destroyed within minutes by the enzyme DPP-4. These drugs are
engineered analogues that resist that breakdown, so the signal persists for days
instead of minutes.

The subjective effect is what makes them work: **food noise goes quiet.** People
describe the constant background negotiation with food simply stopping. They feel
full sooner, stay full longer, and stop thinking about the next meal. The calorie
deficit isn't imposed by willpower — it's a side effect of not being hungry.

Newer drugs stack additional receptors:

| Target | What it adds |
| --- | --- |
| **GLP-1** | Appetite suppression, slowed gastric emptying, insulin response |
| **+ GIP** | Additional incretin effect; appears to improve fat-mass specificity |
| **+ Glucagon** | Raises energy expenditure and hepatic fat oxidation |
| **+ Amylin** | Separate satiety pathway, additive to GLP-1 |

## The drugs, as of 2026

| Drug | Brand(s) | Type | Route | Typical weight loss |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Liraglutide** | Saxenda, Victoza | GLP-1 | Daily injection | ~8% |
| **Semaglutide** | Wegovy, Ozempic, Rybelsus | GLP-1 | Weekly injection / daily oral | **~15%** (STEP 1, 68 wks) |
| **Tirzepatide** | Mounjaro, Zepbound | GLP-1 + GIP | Weekly injection | **~20–22.5%** (SURMOUNT-1, 72 wks) |
| **Orforglipron** | Foundayo | GLP-1 (oral small molecule) | **Daily tablet** | ~11–12% (ATTAIN-1) |
| **CagriSema** | — (filing expected) | GLP-1 + amylin | Weekly injection | ~22.7% (REDEFINE 1) |
| **Retatrutide** | — (in trials) | GLP-1 + GIP + glucagon | Weekly injection | **~28–29%** (phase 3) |

**Orforglipron** matters for a structural reason rather than an efficacy one: it's
a **small molecule, not a peptide**, so it can be manufactured at enormous scale
and taken as an ordinary tablet with no food or water restrictions. It was
approved in the US in **April 2026**. Manufacturing capacity, not efficacy, has
been the binding constraint on this drug class — and a pill changes that.

**Retatrutide's ~28%** would be the highest figure ever reported in a late-stage
obesity trial, approaching what gastric bypass achieves.

## It isn't only about weight

The outcome data has broadened considerably, and this is what moved these drugs
from "cosmetic" to "cardiometabolic":

- **SELECT** showed semaglutide reduced major adverse cardiovascular events by
  **~20%** in people with overweight/obesity and established cardiovascular
  disease **without diabetes** — a hard-endpoint result.
- **Obstructive sleep apnoea** — clinically meaningful improvement with
  tirzepatide.
- **Chronic kidney disease** — reduced progression in the FLOW trial.
- **Knee osteoarthritis**, **MASH** (fatty liver disease), and **heart failure with
  preserved ejection fraction** all show benefit.
- An intriguing, still-unproven signal on **reduced craving** for alcohol and
  nicotine, suggesting these pathways aren't specific to food.

## The muscle question, honestly

This is the part that matters most for a course about training, and it's usually
reported badly in both directions.

**The claim:** roughly **25–40% of the weight lost on GLP-1 drugs is lean mass**.
On semaglutide, lean mass has fallen ~10% from baseline, accounting for around 40%
of total weight lost. On tirzepatide the split is better — about **75% fat, 25%
lean**.

Those numbers are real. Now the two pieces of context that are usually missing:

**1. This is roughly what happens in *any* large weight loss.** Diet-induced
weight loss and bariatric surgery produce similar proportions. There's nothing
uniquely muscle-wasting about the mechanism — lose a lot of mass, and some of it
comes from lean tissue. The comparison that matters isn't "GLP-1 vs nothing," it's
"GLP-1 vs the alternative way of losing the same weight."

**2. "Lean mass" on a DXA scan is not "muscle."** DXA lumps together water,
glycogen, liver and gut mass, connective tissue, and **fat stored inside muscle**.
The SURPASS-3 MRI substudy — MRI can distinguish contractile muscle from
intramuscular fat, which DXA cannot — found tirzepatide **reduced intramuscular
fat**, a marker of metabolic dysfunction. DXA scores that reduction as lean mass
loss. Some of the alarming number is a good thing being mismeasured.

**But the concern is still legitimate.** Absolute muscle loss matters — especially
for older adults, anyone with sarcopenic obesity, and anyone who cares about
function rather than scale weight. And the drug creates a specific trap: **appetite
suppression makes it genuinely hard to eat enough protein**, at exactly the moment
protein matters most.

### What preserves muscle

The evidence here is encouraging and unsurprising — it's the same protocol as any
aggressive fat-loss phase:

| Lever | Target |
| --- | --- |
| **Resistance training** | **2–3 sessions/week**, all major muscle groups, taken close to failure |
| **Protein** | Clinical guidance says **1.2–1.6 g/kg**; if you're training, aim **1.6–2.2 g/kg** |
| **Dose escalation** | Start low, go slow — faster loss means more lean loss |
| **Rate of loss** | Slower is better for composition |
| **Monitoring** | Track strength and lean mass, not just scale weight |

Resistance training plus adequate protein reduces lean mass loss dramatically —
in some analyses close to zero. **The drug solves the hunger problem. It does
nothing about the muscle problem, and it makes the protein problem harder.** That's
precisely where everything else in this course earns its place.

There's also a pharmacological approach emerging: **bimagrumab**, an antibody that
blocks activin receptor signalling, tested with semaglutide in the **BELIEVE**
phase 2b trial. The combination produced **22.1% weight loss of which ~92% was
fat**, with lean mass down only **2.6%** versus **7.9%** for high-dose semaglutide
alone. That's an early-stage result, not an available treatment — but it signals
where the field is going: not just *how much* weight, but *what kind*.

### Practical protein strategy on a GLP-1

Standard advice ("eat more protein") is useless when you feel full after 200
calories. What actually works:

- **Protein first, literally.** Eat the protein portion of the meal before
  anything else. If you stop early, you've stopped after the part that mattered.
- **Liquid protein when solids won't go down.** Shakes, kvarg smoothies, milk — the
  protein-snacks chapter is unusually relevant here.
- **Smaller, more frequent feedings** rather than three large meals you can't
  finish.
- **Watch fibre and fat volume** close to meals — both compound the fullness and
  crowd out protein.
- **Fluid and fibre deliberately.** Constipation is extremely common, and reduced
  food intake means reduced fibre intake unless you're intentional.
- **Consider a multivitamin.** Eating 1 000–1 200 kcal a day for months makes
  micronutrient inadequacy a real risk.

## Side effects and risks

**Very common (dose-dependent, usually improving over weeks):** nausea, vomiting,
diarrhoea, constipation, reflux, burping, fatigue. Most discontinuation happens
here. Slow titration is the main mitigation.

**Less common but important:**

- **Gallstones** — rapid weight loss of any kind raises risk.
- **Pancreatitis** — rare; severe persistent abdominal pain needs urgent
  assessment.
- **Severe gastroparesis** — slowed emptying is the mechanism, but occasionally it
  becomes a problem in its own right.
- **Aspiration risk under anaesthesia** — retained stomach contents. **Tell your
  anaesthetist**; there is specific guidance about pausing before surgery.
- **NAION** (a rare optic neuropathy causing sudden vision loss) — a signal
  identified in observational data and under investigation. Sudden vision changes
  warrant immediate care.
- **Thyroid C-cell tumours** in rodents — contraindicated with a personal or family
  history of **medullary thyroid carcinoma** or **MEN2**.
- **Facial and body fat loss** ("Ozempic face") — cosmetic, and a consequence of
  substantial fat loss rather than the drug specifically.
- **Not for use in pregnancy**, and stop well before trying to conceive. Improved
  fertility with weight loss means unplanned pregnancy is a genuine consideration.

## What happens when you stop

This is the most consistently underappreciated fact about the entire drug class.

In the **STEP 1 extension**, participants regained roughly **two-thirds of the
weight they'd lost within a year of stopping**. Appetite returns, and so does the
weight.

The implication is not that the drugs fail — it's that **obesity behaves like a
chronic condition and these are chronic-condition medications**. Nobody expects
blood pressure to stay low after stopping antihypertensives. The same logic
applies, and it should be part of the decision from day one: this is likely a
long-term commitment, not a course of treatment.

That said, the muscle you build and the habits you establish while on the drug are
**yours**. Someone who spends 18 months on a GLP-1 lifting three times a week and
eating properly is in a categorically different position on discontinuation than
someone who just ate less.

## In Sweden

- **Prescription only**, from a licensed prescriber. Both Wegovy and Mounjaro are
  available.
- **Not subsidised.** TLV decided in **February 2026** that **Wegovy will not be
  included in högkostnadsskyddet**, citing the risk of "subventionsglidning" —
  prescribing drifting well beyond the intended patient group. Obesity treatment
  with these drugs is therefore paid out of pocket.
- **Cost, roughly:** **Wegovy ~1 600–2 700 kr/month**, **Mounjaro ~2 000–4 700
  kr/month**, depending on dose. Over a year that's a **20 000–55 000 kr**
  commitment — and, given the discontinuation data, potentially an ongoing one.
- **Medical criteria** generally follow **BMI ≥ 30**, or **BMI ≥ 27 with a
  weight-related comorbidity**. These are not intended for people at normal weight
  wanting to lose a few kilos, and prescribing them that way is where most of the
  ethical and safety concern sits.
- **Avoid the grey market.** Buying "semaglutide" from overseas websites or
  compounding sources without a prescription means unknown dosing, unknown purity,
  and no clinical oversight of a drug with real contraindications. Counterfeits are
  a documented and growing problem.

## For tested athletes

**GLP-1 receptor agonists are not currently on the WADA Prohibited List** — they're
permitted in and out of competition and require no TUE.

However, WADA placed **semaglutide on its Monitoring Program in 2024**, and added
**markers of tirzepatide from 1 January 2026**. The Monitoring Program is how WADA
collects misuse data on substances it's considering — the stated concern being
weight-class manipulation and weight-sensitive endurance sport. Substances
typically sit there for a few years before WADA either prohibits them or drops the
monitoring.

**If you're tested, verify the current list yourself before using anything.** This
status could change.

## The honest summary

These drugs are the most effective obesity treatment ever developed short of
surgery, with hard cardiovascular outcome data behind them. For people with
obesity and its complications, they're a genuine medical advance and the moralising
about "taking the easy way out" is both unkind and wrong — nobody says that about
statins.

They are also **not a shortcut around the material in this course**. They solve
appetite. They don't build muscle, don't teach you how to eat, don't create a
training habit, and don't work after you stop. Someone who uses the appetite
suppression as **cover to build the things that last** — a lifting habit, a protein
floor, a food environment that works — gets a fundamentally different outcome from
someone who just stops being hungry for a while.

## The takeaway

- GLP-1 drugs mimic a gut hormone to suppress appetite and slow gastric emptying.
  The subjective effect people describe is **food noise going quiet**.
- Efficacy ladder as of 2026: **semaglutide ~15%**, **tirzepatide ~20–22.5%**,
  **CagriSema ~22.7%**, **retatrutide ~28–29%** in trials. **Orforglipron** is the
  first true **oral** option, approved in the US in April 2026.
- Benefits extend past weight — **SELECT** showed a **~20% reduction in major
  cardiovascular events**.
- **25–40% of weight lost is lean mass** — but that's typical of *any* large weight
  loss, and DXA "lean mass" includes water and **intramuscular fat**, which
  tirzepatide reduces (a good thing).
- **Resistance training 2–3×/week plus 1.6–2.2 g/kg protein** is the countermeasure,
  and the appetite suppression makes hitting protein the hard part. **Protein
  first, liquid if needed.**
- **Roughly two-thirds of lost weight returns within a year of stopping.** Treat it
  as a chronic medication, not a course.
- In Sweden: **prescription only, not in högkostnadsskyddet since February 2026**,
  roughly **1 600–4 700 kr/month** out of pocket.
- **Not WADA-prohibited**, but on the **Monitoring Program** — check the current
  list if you're tested.
