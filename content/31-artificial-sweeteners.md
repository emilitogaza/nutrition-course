# Artificial & Low-Calorie Sweeteners

Few nutrition topics generate more heat and less light than sweeteners. Every year
a new headline declares that diet soda causes cancer, wrecks your gut, or is
secretly making you fat — usually from a single study, often taken far past what
it showed. This chapter is a proper deep dive: the **types**, the **evidence**,
and — most importantly — how to weigh a genuinely **unsettled** and fast-moving
literature without lurching from panic to panic.

A note on terms. "Artificial sweetener" is the popular label, but the useful
scientific umbrella is **non-sugar sweeteners (NSS)** — anything that sweetens
without the calories or glycemic load of sugar. They fall into three families:

- **High-intensity (non-nutritive) sweeteners** — hundreds to thousands of times
  sweeter than sugar, used in tiny amounts, essentially **zero calories**. Some
  synthetic (aspartame, sucralose), some plant-derived (stevia, monk fruit).
- **Sugar alcohols (polyols)** — bulk sweeteners roughly as sweet as sugar but
  **low-calorie** and low-glycemic (erythritol, xylitol, sorbitol).
- **Novel/rare sugars** — e.g. **allulose**, a sugar that's barely metabolised.

## The types at a glance

| Sweetener | Type | Sweetness vs sugar | Calories | Heat-stable? | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Saccharin** | Synthetic NNS | ~300–400× | ~0 | Yes | Oldest (1879); the debunked bladder-cancer scare |
| **Aspartame** | Synthetic NNS | ~200× | ~0* | **No** | Breaks down when heated; **unsafe in PKU** |
| **Sucralose** | Synthetic NNS | ~600× | ~0 | Mostly | Made from sugar; microbiome & metabolite questions |
| **Acesulfame-K** | Synthetic NNS | ~200× | 0 | Yes | Often blended with aspartame/sucralose |
| **Neotame / Advantame** | Synthetic NNS | ~7,000–20,000× | 0 | Yes | Aspartame derivatives; no PKU concern |
| **Steviol glycosides (stevia)** | Plant NNS | ~200–400× | 0 | Yes | Purified extract is GRAS; licorice-y aftertaste |
| **Monk fruit (luo han guo)** | Plant NNS | ~150–250× | 0 | Yes | Mogrosides; GRAS |
| **Erythritol** | Polyol | ~70% | ~0.2 kcal/g | Yes | Well-tolerated; the new cardiovascular question |
| **Xylitol** | Polyol | ~100% | ~2.4 kcal/g | Yes | Dental benefit; toxic to dogs; CVD question |
| **Sorbitol / maltitol** | Polyol | ~50–90% | ~2–2.6 kcal/g | Yes | Laxative effect / GI distress in quantity |
| **Allulose** | Rare sugar | ~70% | ~0.2–0.4 kcal/g | Yes | Barely metabolised; doesn't spike glucose |

\* Aspartame *is* 4 kcal/g, but it's so sweet that the few milligrams used carry
negligible calories.

Two practical facts fall straight out of the table: **aspartame is the only common
one that doesn't survive baking**, and **the sugar alcohols are the ones that
cause bloating and a laxative effect** in larger amounts — erythritol least (it's
absorbed and excreted in urine largely unchanged), sorbitol and maltitol most.

## Do they actually help you lose weight?

This is the question that matters most, and the answer hinges entirely on **what
the sweetener replaces**.

- **Versus sugar, they help — modestly.** In a meta-analysis of controlled trials,
  swapping sugar for low-energy sweeteners **reduced energy intake (~−94 kcal
  acutely) and body weight (~−1.35 kg over sustained trials)** (Rogers et al.,
  2016). A large systematic review reached the same place: no weight *gain*, and a
  small loss in people who were overweight (Toews et al., 2019).
- **Versus water, the benefit largely disappears.** In those same analyses, LNCS
  were no better than plain water. They are a **substitution tool**, not a weight-
  loss agent in their own right — the win comes from the calories they displace.

Then, in 2023, the **WHO issued a guideline advising against using non-sugar
sweeteners for weight control** or to reduce disease risk. Two things are essential
to read correctly here:

1. It's a **conditional recommendation based on low-certainty evidence** — WHO's
   own wording. Much of the "harm" signal comes from **observational** studies.
2. Those studies are wide open to **reverse causation**: people who already have
   higher weight or metabolic risk are exactly the ones who switch to diet drinks,
   which makes diet-drink drinkers *look* less healthy without the sweetener
   causing it. The randomised trials — which control for this — show the opposite:
   modest benefit when replacing sugar.

The honest read: **if a diet drink displaces a sugary one, that's almost certainly
a net positive; if unsweetened water or coffee would do the job, that's better
still.**

## Blood sugar, insulin, and the "metabolic confusion" idea

A popular claim is that sweet taste *without* calories "confuses" the body —
triggering an insulin release, or uncoupling sweetness from reward so you overeat
later. What the evidence shows:

- Most NNS **do not acutely raise blood glucose or insulin** on their own — that's
  their whole appeal for people managing diabetes.
- A small **cephalic-phase** (taste-triggered) insulin response can occur, but
  it's minor and inconsistent, and doesn't translate into meaningful glucose
  problems in controlled studies.
- The "sweetness makes you hungrier / eat more" hypothesis is **not well supported
  in human trials** — appetite and later intake are largely unaffected or slightly
  reduced when NNS replace sugar.

So the simple "they spike insulin and make you fat" story doesn't hold up. The
more interesting — and genuinely open — mechanism runs through the gut.

## The gut microbiome — the most credible open question

This is where the science is real and still moving.

- **Suez et al. (2014, *Nature*)** found that saccharin induced glucose
  intolerance in mice **by altering the gut microbiome** — and saw a similar effect
  in a small group of human volunteers. Provocative, but preliminary.
- **Suez et al. (2022, *Cell*)** followed up with a proper **randomised controlled
  trial**: 120 healthy adults took saccharin, sucralose, aspartame, or stevia for
  two weeks at doses **below** the acceptable daily intake. All four **shifted the
  gut and oral microbiome**; **saccharin and sucralose measurably impaired glucose
  tolerance**. Crucially, transplanting the humans' microbiomes into germ-free mice
  **reproduced** the glucose changes — evidence the microbiome was *causing* them.

The caveats matter as much as the finding: the effects were **person-specific**
(some responded, some didn't), the glucose changes were **modest and
short-term**, and we don't yet know the long-term health consequences. This is a
"watch closely" result, not a "never touch sucralose" one — and it fits the
course's recurring theme that **individual variation is large** (see the microbiome
and new-research chapters).

## Cancer — the scare that keeps coming back

Sweeteners have been linked to cancer in the headlines for fifty years. The track
record is instructive:

- **Saccharin** caused bladder tumours in rats in the 1970s and carried a warning
  label for decades. Then mechanistic work showed the tumours arose from a quirk of
  **rat urine chemistry that doesn't apply to humans**; human epidemiology found no
  consistent link. Saccharin was **delisted as a carcinogen in 2000** and the
  warning label repealed. A textbook case of a rodent finding that didn't
  translate.
- **Aspartame (2023)** got the biggest recent headline. Two WHO bodies reported the
  same month and reached what *sounds* like opposite conclusions:
  - **IARC** classified aspartame **"possibly carcinogenic to humans" (Group 2B)**
    on *limited* evidence (a weak signal for liver cancer). Group 2B is a **hazard**
    label — "we can't rule it out" — and shares the tier with aloe vera extract and
    pickled vegetables.
  - **JECFA** — which assesses actual **risk at real doses** — reviewed the same
    data and **kept the acceptable daily intake at 40 mg/kg/day** unchanged. That
    limit is enormous: a 70 kg adult would need roughly **9–14 cans of diet soda a
    day, every day**, to exceed it.
  - The distinction is the whole story: **hazard ("could it, ever?") is not risk
    ("does it, at the doses people actually consume?").**
- **Observational cohorts.** The French **NutriNet-Santé** study (Debras et al.,
  2022) reported higher overall **cancer** risk with higher artificial-sweetener
  intake (notably aspartame and acesulfame-K) across ~100,000 adults, and a
  companion paper linked them to **cardiovascular** risk. Large and carefully done,
  but **observational** — subject to the same reverse-causation and
  residual-confounding caveats as all diet-cohort work.
- **Sucralose-6-acetate (Schiffman et al., 2023).** A lab study reported that this
  impurity (present in trace amounts in sucralose and formed during digestion) was
  **genotoxic** and damaged gut-barrier cells *in vitro*. Worth following — but it's
  a cell/tissue study, was contested by the manufacturer, and needs replication
  before it changes practice.

The pattern across all of these: **strong rodent or cell-level or observational
signals, weak-to-absent evidence of real-world human harm at normal intakes.**
That doesn't mean "proven safe forever" — it means the burden of the alarming
headlines has repeatedly failed to survive scrutiny.

## Cardiovascular — the newest frontier (and a good lesson)

The freshest concern targets the **sugar alcohols** everyone assumed were the
safest:

- **Witkowski et al. (2023, *Nature Medicine*)** found that high blood levels of
  **erythritol** were associated with heart attack and stroke, and showed
  erythritol **promoted platelet clotting** in lab and short human-challenge
  experiments. A **2024** follow-up reported similar findings for **xylitol**.
- The **critical caveat**: your body *makes* erythritol internally from glucose, so
  high blood erythritol may be a **marker of existing metabolic dysfunction**
  (high blood sugar) rather than proof that dietary erythritol causes heart
  disease. The observational arm can't separate the two, and regulators (including
  the FDA) noted these limitations. It's a serious enough signal to warrant more
  study — and enough uncertainty that "erythritol causes heart attacks" is not yet
  a supportable claim.

This is exactly the kind of finding the **new-research chapter's checklist** is
built for: a striking result, a plausible mechanism, and a confound big enough that
the causal direction is still unknown.

## The one firm rule: aspartame and PKU

Amid all the maybes, there's one hard contraindication. Aspartame contains
**phenylalanine**, and people with the genetic disorder **phenylketonuria (PKU)**
cannot metabolise it — which is why aspartame-containing products carry a PKU
warning. For everyone else, phenylalanine from aspartame is trivial next to what's
in ordinary protein foods.

## Putting it together

- **They're a substitution tool.** Replacing sugar with a sweetener reliably cuts
  calories and helps weight modestly; replacing sugar with *water* is better still.
  Neither is a weight-loss drug.
- **Regulators consider approved sweeteners safe at normal intakes,** with large
  safety margins built into the ADIs — and normal use is far below them.
- **The credible open questions are the gut microbiome and (newly) cardiovascular
  effects of some polyols** — real, worth watching, not yet settled.
- **Most cancer headlines have not replicated;** learn to separate **hazard from
  risk** and **association from causation**.
- **Variety and moderation** apply here too. If you rely heavily on diet products,
  rotating sweeteners and not treating "zero-calorie" as "free license to consume
  endlessly" is sensible while the science matures.

The biggest-picture point: sweeteners are a **tool for the transition off sugar**,
not the destination. The gold standard remains a palate that's happy with less
sweetness overall — but for someone cutting a heavy sugar habit, a diet drink is a
reasonable rung on the ladder, and the evidence of harm at realistic intakes
remains thin.

## The takeaway

- Non-sugar sweeteners come in three families: **high-intensity NNS** (aspartame,
  sucralose, saccharin, stevia, monk fruit), **sugar alcohols** (erythritol,
  xylitol), and **rare sugars** (allulose).
- For weight, they help **when they replace sugar** (~−1.35 kg; Rogers 2016), but
  aren't better than **water**; the **WHO 2023** guidance against them rests on
  **low-certainty, mostly observational** evidence prone to reverse causation.
- The most credible open questions are **microbiome-driven glucose effects** (Suez
  2022 RCT) and **cardiovascular signals for erythritol/xylitol** (Witkowski
  2023–24) — both **unsettled**.
- **Aspartame's 2023 IARC "2B" label is a hazard, not a risk** — the ADI was left
  unchanged; **PKU** is the one firm contraindication.
- Read every sweetener headline through **hazard-vs-risk** and
  **association-vs-causation** — the alarms have a long history of not replicating.
