# Protein Types, Powders & Cost per Gram

The previous chapter covered protein *quality*. This one covers protein
*products* — what's actually in the tub, why two whey powders can differ by 60%
in price for the same amino acids, and how to work out which one is genuinely
cheaper.

There's one skill to take away: **stop comparing price per bag, start comparing
price per 100 g of protein.** Every number below is built on that.

## What the numbers on the label mean

Swedish protein powders are usually sold as **Whey-80** or **Whey-100**. The
number is *not* purity, freshness, or grade — it's the manufacturer's shorthand
for **grams of protein per 100 g of powder**.

| Product type | Abbrev. | Protein per 100 g | Lactose | Made by |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Whey concentrate** ("Whey-80") | WPC | 70–80 g | ~4–8 g | Basic ultrafiltration |
| **Whey isolate** ("Whey-100") | WPI | 86–92 g | < 1 g | Further micro-/ion-filtration |
| **Whey hydrolysate** | WPH | 80–90 g | Low | Isolate, pre-cleaved into peptides |
| **Micellar casein** | — | 78–85 g | Low–moderate | Milk protein, slow-clotting |
| **Milk protein isolate** | MPI | 80–88 g | Low | ~80/20 casein-to-whey, as in milk |
| **Soy protein isolate** | SPI | 88–92 g | None | Defatted soy, alcohol-washed |
| **Pea protein isolate** | — | 78–85 g | None | Yellow split pea |
| **Collagen / "protein" peptides** | — | 88–95 g | None | Hydrolysed hide, bone, or fish skin |

### The flavouring catch

Here's something the marketing never mentions: **a flavoured "Whey-80" is rarely
80 g of protein per 100 g.** Cocoa, sweeteners, thickeners, and flavour
compounds all displace protein. Check any chocolate whey and you'll typically
find **73–77 g per 100 g** on the nutrition panel, while the unflavoured
("naturell") version of the same product sits closer to 80.

That's a **5–8% hidden price increase** on every flavoured tub — and it's exactly
the kind of thing that only shows up when you calculate cost per gram of protein
rather than cost per kilo of powder.

## Whey-80 vs Whey-100 — is the upgrade worth it?

This is the single most common supplement question in Sweden, and the honest
answer is: **usually no.**

Both are whey. Both have the same complete amino acid profile, the same very high
**leucine** content (~10–11% of protein), and the same fast digestion kinetics.
Isolate is simply concentrate that's been filtered further, removing most of the
remaining lactose and fat. No study has shown isolate builds more muscle than
concentrate when **total daily protein is matched** — which is the condition that
actually determines results.

**Choose Whey-100 (isolate) if:**

- You're **lactose intolerant**. This is the real reason it exists. Isolate keeps
  lactose under ~1 g per serving; concentrate can carry 2–4 g, which is enough to
  cause problems for sensitive people.
- You're in a **hard cut** and want maximum protein for minimum calories. Isolate
  delivers roughly 100–105 kcal per 25 g of protein vs ~120–130 kcal for
  concentrate. Over a day that's negligible; over a very tight deficit it can be
  a convenience.
- You dislike the slight creaminess of concentrate — isolate mixes thinner.

**Choose Whey-80 (concentrate) if:** none of the above apply. You'll get the same
muscle for **20–30% less money**, and concentrate retains slightly more of the
bioactive milk fractions (immunoglobulins, lactoferrin) that filtering strips
out — a minor point, but it cuts against the "purer is better" intuition.

### What about hydrolysate?

**Whey hydrolysate** is isolate that's been enzymatically pre-broken into shorter
peptides. It absorbs marginally faster and tastes noticeably worse (bitter). For
hypertrophy, controlled trials find **no meaningful advantage** over intact whey.
It costs the most and does the least. Skip it unless you have a specific clinical
reason.

## The one powder that isn't a protein: collagen

**Collagen peptides** are sold in the protein aisle and score around **0 on
DIAAS.** Collagen contains **no tryptophan** at all — one of the nine essential
amino acids — so by definition it cannot support muscle protein synthesis on its
own. It's also very low in leucine (~2.5% vs whey's 10–11%).

That doesn't make it useless: there's reasonable evidence for collagen plus
vitamin C supporting **tendon and connective tissue** when taken before loading
(see the tissue-healing chapter). But **count it separately from your protein
target.** If a label says "20 g protein" and it's collagen, treat it as 0 g
toward your daily muscle-building total.

## Plant powders

- **Soy protein isolate** — the best plant option and, in Sweden, often by far the
  cheapest protein per gram of *anything*. DIAAS ~0.9, complete, ~8% leucine.
  The phytoestrogen worry is not supported by the evidence in men at normal
  intakes.
- **Pea protein** — low in methionine, ~8% leucine. Fine when total intake is
  high.
- **Pea + rice blends** — deliberately paired: rice covers pea's methionine gap,
  pea covers rice's lysine gap. The blend approaches animal-protein quality.
- **Practical adjustment:** with plant powders, use **~25–30% more per serving**
  (aim for 35–40 g of powder-protein rather than 25–30 g) to match whey's leucine
  dose.

Three things jump out:

1. **Buying the 4 kg bag instead of the 1 kg bag saves ~20%** — the single
   biggest lever, bigger than the choice between concentrate and isolate.
2. **Soy isolate is roughly half the price of whey** per gram of protein. If you
   tolerate it, it's the cheapest supplemental protein on the Swedish market.
3. **A protein bar costs about three times more per gram of protein than the most
   expensive powder**, and roughly six times more than a 4 kg bag of Whey-80.

### What that means over a year

Say you use powder for **40 g of protein a day**, every day:

| Choice | Cost per year |
| --- | --- |
| Soy isolate, 1 kg bags | **~3 500 kr** |
| Whey-80, 4 kg bags | **~6 100 kr** |
| Whey-100, 1 kg bags | **~8 300 kr** |
| One protein bar per day | **~19 700 kr** |

The gap between the top and bottom row is **over 16 000 kr a year** for an amino
acid profile that, in terms of what your muscles do with it, is close to
identical.

## Powder vs. food

Powder is often assumed to be the cheap option. Against real food, it isn't
always:

| Source | Typical price | Protein/100 g | **kr per 100 g protein** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Dried red lentils** | ~25 kr/kg | 25 g (dry) | **~10 kr** |
| **Oats** | ~22 kr/kg | 13 g | **~17 kr** |
| **Low-fat kvarg (budget brand)** | ~23 kr/900 g | 11 g | **~23 kr** |
| **Soy protein isolate** | 215 kr/kg | 90 g | **~24 kr** |
| **Eggs (24-pack, budget)** | 44 kr | ~6.4 g/egg | **~29 kr** |
| **Milk (1.5 l)** | ~17 kr | 3.4 g/100 ml | **~33 kr** |
| **Chicken breast, frozen** | ~85 kr/kg | 23 g (raw) | **~37 kr** |
| **Whey-80 (4 kg bag)** | 1 249 kr | ~75 g | **~42 kr** |
| **Cottage cheese / keso** | ~29 kr/500 g | 12.5 g | **~46 kr** |
| **Canned tuna in water** | ~17 kr/burk | 25 g (drained) | **~52 kr** |
| **Tofu** | ~35 kr/400 g | 12 g | **~73 kr** |
| **Minced beef, 12%** | ~155 kr/kg | 19 g | **~82 kr** |
| **Fresh salmon fillet** | ~180 kr/kg | 20 g | **~90 kr** |

> **On these numbers.** Powder and dairy prices were checked on gymgrossisten.com
> and matspar.se in July 2026; a few of the food entries are typical rather than
> verified, and everything moves with sales and store. Treat the table as a
> **method**, not a price list — the ranking is far more stable than the
> kronor.

The takeaway isn't that powder is a bad deal. It's that **kvarg, eggs, milk, and
frozen chicken are all in the same price band as whey** — so powder earns its
place on **convenience**, not economy. Meanwhile, dried legumes are so cheap
they're almost free protein, and red meat and fresh fish are what you're paying a
premium for (and buying for reasons other than protein).

## Quality and safety when buying powder

- **Third-party testing.** If you're drug-tested, only use **Informed Sport** or
  **NSF Certified for Sport** products. Contamination is a real, recurring cause
  of failed tests.
- **Heavy metals.** Independent testing has repeatedly found detectable lead and
  cadmium in some powders, with **plant-based and chocolate-flavoured** products
  averaging higher (plants concentrate soil metals; cocoa is a known source). The
  levels are generally low, but if you drink 100 g of powder a day, prefer a
  brand that publishes testing.
- **Amino spiking** is largely a solved problem in regulated EU markets, but a
  suspiciously cheap import with a vague amino profile is still worth avoiding.
- **Check the panel, not the front.** Protein per 100 g of *powder* is the number.
  "Protein per serving" with an undeclared scoop size tells you nothing.

## The takeaway

- The number in **Whey-80 / Whey-100** is grams of protein per 100 g of powder —
  **concentrate vs isolate**. Both are whey, with the same amino acids.
- **Isolate is worth it for lactose intolerance**, and little else. Concentrate
  builds the same muscle for 20–30% less.
- **Flavoured powders under-deliver** — a "Whey-80" chocolate is often 73–75 g
  protein per 100 g. Read the panel.
- **Collagen is not a muscle protein** (no tryptophan, DIAAS ~0). Don't count it
  toward your target.
- Compare everything on **kr per 100 g protein**. Bulk bags beat small bags by
  ~20%; **soy isolate is the cheapest powder**; **protein bars are the most
  expensive protein you can buy**, by a factor of three or more.
- Powder competes with **kvarg, eggs, milk, and frozen chicken** on price — so buy
  it for convenience, not savings.
