# Hydration & Electrolytes

Water is the nutrient people forget is a nutrient. It's the medium for nearly
every reaction in the body, the coolant that lets you dump heat during exercise,
and the carrier for electrolytes that run your nerves and muscles. Getting
hydration roughly right matters for performance; getting it badly wrong — in
either direction — can be dangerous.

## Why dehydration hurts performance

Lose enough water and blood volume drops, so the heart works harder, you can't
shed heat as well, and core temperature climbs. The classic finding is that a
fluid loss of about **2% of body weight** begins to measurably impair endurance
performance and raises perceived effort; larger losses impair strength, power, and
cognition, and raise heat-illness risk.

That 2% figure is a **guideline, not a cliff** — trained athletes acclimatised to
their sport tolerate some fluid loss well, and thirst is a reasonable guide for
most everyday activity. It becomes critical in **long, hot, high-sweat-rate**
efforts.

## How much to drink

Rigid daily targets (the "8 glasses" rule) aren't evidence-based — needs vary
enormously with size, climate, and activity. Better principles:

- **Everyday:** drink to thirst, aiming for pale-yellow urine. Food contributes
  water too.
- **Before exercise:** arrive **euhydrated** — a few hundred mL in the couple of
  hours prior, not a last-minute flood.
- **During exercise:** for sessions under ~60 minutes, water and thirst suffice.
  For longer/hotter work, drink to roughly **match sweat losses** without
  overdoing it.
- **After exercise:** replace fluid *and* sodium. A practical rule is ~**1.25–1.5
  L per kg** of body weight lost, since you keep sweating and producing urine
  afterward.

### Know your sweat rate

The precise way to personalise this: weigh yourself **nude before and after** a
training session (accounting for any fluid drunk). Each kilogram lost ≈ 1 litre of
sweat. Sweat rates range widely — from under 0.5 up to 2.5+ L/hour — so this is
worth measuring if you train long in the heat.

## Electrolytes — sodium is the main one

Sweat isn't just water; it carries **electrolytes**, chiefly **sodium**, plus
potassium, chloride, magnesium, and calcium. For most short sessions, food
replaces these fine. They matter when:

- Sessions run **long** (> 1–2 h) or in the **heat**.
- You're a **"salty sweater"** (visible salt crusts on skin/clothing) with high
  sweat sodium losses.
- You're doing repeated days of heavy training.

In those cases, adding **sodium** to fluids (a sports drink or electrolyte mix)
helps retain the fluid you drink and sustain performance. For everyday gym
sessions, dedicated electrolyte products are largely optional marketing.

## The overlooked danger: hyponatremia

You can drink **too much**. Over-drinking plain water during long endurance events
dilutes blood sodium — **exercise-associated hyponatremia** — which can cause
nausea, confusion, seizures, and, rarely, death. It has killed marathoners who
drank *more* than they needed. The guidance has shifted accordingly:

- **Don't over-drink.** More water is not safer.
- **Drinking to thirst** protects against both dehydration and hyponatremia for
  most people.
- In long events, **include sodium**, don't just chug water.

## The takeaway

- Performance falls around **~2% body-weight fluid loss** — most important in
  long, hot efforts.
- **Drink to thirst** day to day; arrive euhydrated, and replace fluid **and
  sodium** after big sweat sessions.
- Measure your **sweat rate** if you train long in the heat.
- **Overhydration is a real danger** (hyponatremia) — more water isn't safer;
  include sodium in endurance events.
