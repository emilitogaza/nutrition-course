# Energy Balance & Metabolism

Almost everything in body composition comes back to one equation: **energy in
versus energy out**. Eat more than you burn over time and you store the surplus
— mostly as fat, and with the right training, some as muscle. Eat less and you
draw the difference down. This is thermodynamics, and it isn't optional.

But the equation is far more interesting than "eat less, move more" makes it
sound, because *both sides adjust in response to each other*.

## Where your energy goes

Your total daily energy expenditure (**TDEE**) has four parts:

| Component | What it is | Rough share |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **BMR / RMR** | Baseline cost of staying alive — organs, brain, cellular upkeep | 60–70% |
| **TEF** | Thermic effect of food — the energy used to digest and process meals | ~10% |
| **EAT** | Exercise activity — deliberate training | 5–15% |
| **NEAT** | Non-exercise activity — walking, fidgeting, posture, everything else | Highly variable |

The surprises here are usually **TEF** and **NEAT**.

### Protein makes you burn more just by eating it

TEF isn't equal across macronutrients. Protein has a thermic effect of roughly
**20–30%** — a quarter of the calories are spent processing it — versus about
**5–10% for carbohydrate** and **0–3% for fat**. A high-protein diet quietly
raises your daily burn and is more filling per calorie. This is one reason two
diets with identical calories can produce different results.

### NEAT is the wildcard

Non-exercise activity can swing by **hundreds of calories a day** between people
of the same size — and within the same person over time. When you diet, NEAT
tends to drop: you move less without noticing. When you overfeed, some people
ramp NEAT up and resist fat gain. This unconscious adjustment is a big part of
why "just eat 500 fewer calories" rarely produces exactly the predicted loss.

## Metabolism adapts — in both directions

Your metabolic rate is not a fixed number. As you lose weight:

- You're a smaller body, so BMR drops (less tissue to maintain).
- You spend less energy moving that smaller body.
- **Adaptive thermogenesis** kicks in — the body becomes more efficient and
  burns somewhat *less* than its new size predicts, partly via lower thyroid
  output, leptin, and sympathetic tone.

This is why fat loss slows over time even when you're still in a deficit, and why
the same intake that drove loss eventually becomes maintenance. It is a normal,
expected response — not a broken metabolism. Later chapters on fat loss cover how
to work *with* it rather than fight it.

## Calories still count — even when you don't count them

You can ignore numbers entirely and still be governed by this equation. Appetite,
food choices, protein, sleep, and activity all move the two sides. Tracking is a
*tool* for awareness and precision, not a moral requirement. Some people thrive
counting; others do better with structure and habits. Both work if energy
balance ends up where it needs to be.

## The takeaway

- TDEE = **BMR + TEF + EAT + NEAT**, and every term is movable.
- **Protein** raises the thermic effect and satiety per calorie.
- **NEAT** is the hidden variable that makes real-world results differ from the
  math.
- Metabolism **adapts** as you diet; expect the pace to change and plan for it.
