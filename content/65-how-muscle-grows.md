# How Muscle Grows — and Gets Strong

The course has told you, repeatedly, to "lift weights with progressive overload."
This section finally explains *how* — starting with the biology of why a muscle
gets bigger and stronger at all. Nutrition supplies the building blocks (the
protein and amino-acid chapters); **training supplies the signal.** Here's what
that signal actually is.

## Hypertrophy is net protein balance, driven by a stimulus

Muscle is in constant turnover — built up by **muscle protein synthesis (MPS)**,
broken down by **muscle protein breakdown (MPB)** (recall the amino-acids chapter).
You grow when, summed over time, MPS exceeds MPB. Resistance training is the most
powerful lever on that balance: a challenging session **elevates MPS for ~24–48
hours**, and repeated over weeks — with enough protein and energy — the fibres
thicken. **Training is the stimulus; food is the material.** You need both.

## The primary driver: mechanical tension

Of the things a workout does to a muscle, **mechanical tension** is the one that
matters most. When a muscle fibre contracts hard against a meaningful load, that
tension is *sensed* by the cell (**mechanotransduction**) and switches on the
growth machinery (the mTOR pathway again). More high-tension, effortful
contractions → more growth signal.

Two other mechanisms get discussed, and it's worth knowing where they land:

- **Metabolic stress** — the "burn" and "pump" of accumulated metabolites. A
  possible *minor, secondary* contributor; not the main event.
- **Muscle damage** — micro-tears and the soreness that follows. **Not a driver of
  growth.** Damage is a *byproduct* of unaccustomed work, not the goal — which is
  why chasing soreness is a mistake (more in the recovery chapter). You do **not**
  need to be sore to have grown.

## Why both heavy and light loads work: motor units

Here's the piece that ties the practical rules together. Muscles are recruited by
**motor units** following the **size principle**: low-threshold (smaller) units
fire first, and higher-threshold (larger, more growth-prone) units join **only as
the effort ramps up.** To grow a fibre, you must **recruit it *and* fatigue it.**

- **Heavy loads** recruit the high-threshold units almost immediately — every rep
  is high-tension.
- **Lighter loads** recruit them too — but only as you approach failure and the
  smaller units tire out, forcing the big ones in.

This is exactly why the research finds **similar hypertrophy across a wide range of
loads when sets are taken close to failure** (Schoenfeld et al., 2017). Whether
it's 6 hard reps or 20, what the muscle "sees" near the end is high-threshold units
under tension. The takeaway: **effortful reps close to failure are the currency of
growth**, across a broad range of loads.

## Strength is related to size — but not the same thing

Getting **bigger** and getting **stronger** overlap, but they're distinct
adaptations:

| | Hypertrophy | Strength |
| --- | --- | --- |
| What it is | More muscle protein / bigger fibres | More **force production** |
| Built by | Effortful volume across many rep ranges | **Heavy** loads + practising the lift |
| Also depends on | Mostly muscle size | **Neural factors** — recruitment, firing rate, coordination, skill |

Strength is **muscle size *plus* the nervous system's ability to use it.** That's
why beginners get much stronger in the first weeks with little size change — those
are largely **neural** gains (learning the movement, recruiting more units). And
it's why strength is **specific**: to get strong at a heavy squat you must *squat
heavy* — you're training a skill, not just building tissue (the **SAID** principle —
Specific Adaptations to Imposed Demands). Bigger muscle raises your strength
*ceiling*; heavy practice realises it.

## A note on the plumbing: myonuclei and muscle memory

Growing fibres add **myonuclei** (the control centres that direct protein
production). Usefully, these **appear to persist even when you detrain and lose
size** — which is a leading explanation for **muscle memory**: previously built
muscle comes back faster than it was first earned (as noted in the body-recomp and
building-muscle chapters).

## The takeaway

- You grow when **MPS > MPB over time**: training is the **signal**, food is the
  **material**.
- **Mechanical tension** is the primary driver; **metabolic stress** is minor and
  **muscle damage/soreness is not required** for growth.
- Via **motor-unit recruitment**, **effortful reps near failure build muscle across
  a wide load range** (Schoenfeld 2017) — heavy *or* light.
- **Strength = size + neural skill**, and it's **specific**: train heavy to get
  strong, train effortful volume to get big.
