# Protein-Rich Snacks & Everyday Foods

Knowing you need 1.6–2.2 g/kg is one thing. Hitting it on a Tuesday afternoon
between meetings is another. This chapter is the practical layer: what to
actually reach for, how much protein it actually contains, and what it costs.

The core problem is that **most snack food carries almost no protein.** A banana
has 1.3 g. A handful of crisps has 2 g. Two hours of grazing can add 400 calories
and 5 g of protein — which is why people who "eat all day" often still land at
80 g total.

## The protein density test

Before the lists, one filter that sorts almost everything: **grams of protein per
100 kcal.**

| Ratio | Verdict | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **> 15 g/100 kcal** | Outstanding | Egg white, white fish, shrimp, low-fat kvarg |
| **10–15 g/100 kcal** | Excellent | Chicken breast, keso, tuna in water, skyr |
| **6–10 g/100 kcal** | Good | Salmon, minced beef, Greek yoghurt, tofu, edamame |
| **3–6 g/100 kcal** | Mediocre | Milk, lentils, oats, bread, most protein bars |
| **< 3 g/100 kcal** | Not a protein food | Nuts, cheese-heavy snacks, fruit, crisps |

This single ratio explains why nuts and cheese *feel* like protein foods but
function as fat foods, and why kvarg is the workhorse of Swedish snacking.

## The dairy tier — cheapest and most convenient

Swedish supermarkets are unusually well stocked here, and this is where most
people's easiest wins are.

| Food | Portion | Protein | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Low-fat kvarg (0.2%)** | 250 g | **~28 g** | The single best protein-per-krona snack |
| **Skyr** | 250 g | ~27 g | Same idea, slightly thicker |
| **Cottage cheese / keso** | 200 g | ~25 g | ~13 g/100 g; savoury option |
| **Greek yoghurt (10%)** | 200 g | ~18 g | Watch the fat calories |
| **Milk** | 500 ml | ~17 g | Underrated; also carbs and fluid |
| **Protein pudding (ready-made)** | 200 g | ~20 g | Convenient, ~4× the price of kvarg |
| **Cheese (28%)** | 30 g (2 slices) | ~7 g | Fat food that happens to have protein |

**The kvarg trick:** stir **15–20 g of whey powder** into 250 g of kvarg. You get
**~42–45 g of protein** in one bowl, it thickens into something like a mousse, and
it costs roughly 15 kr. This is the highest-leverage habit in this entire
chapter.

## The egg tier

| Food | Portion | Protein | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Boiled eggs** | 3 eggs | **~19 g** | Batch-boil a dozen on Sunday |
| **Liquid egg white** | 200 ml | ~22 g | ~11 g/100 ml, almost zero calories |
| **Egg muffins** (egg + veg, baked in a tin) | 3 | ~15 g | Freeze well, reheat in 60 s |

Eggs are near the top on cost-effectiveness (~29–39 kr per 100 g protein) and
they're the most portable unprocessed protein there is.

## The tinned-fish tier

Chronically overlooked, and it doubles as your **omega-3** intake (see the
omega-3 chapter — this is the cheapest way to hit 2–3 servings of fatty fish a
week).

| Food | Portion | Protein | Omega-3 (EPA+DHA) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Tuna in water** | 1 tin (~130 g drained) | **~32 g** | ~0.3 g |
| **Mackerel in tomato sauce** | 1 tin (~375 g) | ~50 g | **~2.5 g** |
| **Sardines in olive oil** | 1 tin (~100 g drained) | ~25 g | **~1.5 g** |
| **Pickled/smoked herring** | 100 g | ~19 g | **~2 g** |
| **Smoked salmon** | 100 g | ~21 g | ~1.5 g |

**Mackerel in tomato sauce** is arguably the best value item in the Swedish
grocery store: ~25 kr for 50 g of protein *and* a full week's worth of omega-3 in
a single tin.

## The meat & poultry tier

| Food | Portion | Protein | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Chicken breast** | 150 g cooked | **~46 g** | ~37 kr/100 g protein frozen |
| **Turkey/chicken cold cuts** | 60 g | ~12 g | Check for added water and starch |
| **Biltong / air-dried beef** | 50 g | ~25 g | Very high density, very high price |
| **Beef jerky** | 50 g | ~22 g | Often heavily sugared — read the label |
| **Pork tenderloin** | 150 g cooked | ~33 g | Cheaper than chicken breast on sale |

Batch-cooking 1 kg of chicken on a Sunday and keeping it sliced in the fridge is
the difference between a plan and a wish.

## The plant tier

| Food | Portion | Protein | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Edamame (frozen, in pod)** | 150 g | **~17 g** | Boil 4 min, salt; excellent snack |
| **Tofu (firm)** | 150 g | ~18 g | Press and pan-fry |
| **Tempeh** | 100 g | ~19 g | Highest-protein soy food |
| **Roasted chickpeas** | 100 g | ~19 g | Crunchy crisp replacement |
| **Cooked lentils** | 200 g | ~18 g | ~10 kr per 100 g protein dried — the cheapest |
| **Soy nuts** | 50 g | ~18 g | Dense, salty, satisfying |
| **Peanuts** | 50 g | ~13 g | But ~290 kcal — a fat food |
| **Oats** | 80 g dry | ~11 g | Base, not a protein source |

Plant snacks come with fibre, which helps satiety but reduces digestibility —
another reason plant-based eaters should aim toward the **higher end** of the
daily protein range.

## What 30 g of protein looks like

The per-meal dose that reliably maximises muscle protein synthesis in most adults
is **~0.4 g/kg**, or roughly 30 g. Here's that dose, six ways, with no cooking:

| Snack | Protein | Approx. kcal | Approx. cost |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 250 g kvarg + 15 g whey | ~43 g | ~290 | ~15 kr |
| 1 tin tuna + 2 knäckebröd + keso | ~40 g | ~320 | ~30 kr |
| 250 g keso + berries | ~31 g | ~230 | ~18 kr |
| 5 boiled eggs | ~31 g | ~390 | ~10 kr |
| Whey shake in 300 ml milk | ~33 g | ~250 | ~20 kr |
| 150 g edamame + 200 g skyr | ~39 g | ~300 | ~35 kr |

## The protein bar problem

Protein bars are the most heavily marketed and least cost-effective protein on
the shelf. A typical 55 g bar gives **~20 g of protein for ~27 kr** — about
**135 kr per 100 g of protein**, versus ~23 kr for kvarg and ~42 kr for bulk
whey.

They're also frequently **candy with a protein claim**: many carry 200–230 kcal,
a large share from fat and sugar alcohols, and the sugar alcohols (maltitol,
erythritol) are a common cause of the bloating people blame on whey.

**That doesn't make them useless.** A bar in a gym bag that you actually eat beats
a perfect meal you didn't prepare. Just price it honestly: you're paying roughly
a **5–6× premium for portability**, not buying a better protein.

## Building the habit

Three rules that do most of the work:

1. **Anchor protein to every eating occasion.** Not "get more protein" — instead,
   *every* time you eat, something on the plate is a protein source. This alone
   usually adds 40–60 g a day.
2. **Make the default visible.** Boiled eggs at eye level, kvarg at the front of
   the fridge, tinned fish in the desk drawer. Snacking is decided by what's
   nearest, not by what's best.
3. **Front-load breakfast.** Breakfast is the meal where most people fall
   shortest — a typical Swedish breakfast lands at 8–12 g. Getting it to 30 g
   (kvarg, eggs, or a shake) closes most of the daily gap before 09:00.

One more benefit worth knowing: protein has the **highest satiety per calorie** of
any macronutrient and the **highest thermic effect** — roughly **20–30% of its
calories are burned in digestion**, versus 5–10% for carbs and 0–3% for fat. A
high-protein snack is doing two jobs at once.

## The takeaway

- Filter snacks by **protein per 100 kcal**. Above 10 g/100 kcal is a protein
  food; below 3 g/100 kcal isn't, regardless of the packaging.
- **Kvarg, skyr, keso, eggs, and tinned fish** are the backbone — cheap, no
  cooking, 20–30 g a portion.
- **Kvarg + a scoop of whey** is the highest-leverage single habit: ~43 g of
  protein for ~15 kr.
- **Mackerel in tomato sauce** delivers protein *and* a week's omega-3 in one tin.
- **Protein bars cost 5–6× more per gram of protein** than the alternatives — buy
  them for portability, knowingly.
- Anchor protein to **every** eating occasion, and fix **breakfast** first.
