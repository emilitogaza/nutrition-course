# Creatine — The Complete Guide

Creatine got a short entry in the ergogenic aids chapter because it belongs at
the top of that list. It gets a full chapter because it's the one supplement
where the evidence is deep enough to have real detail worth knowing — and because
almost every popular claim about its risks is wrong.

Over **1 000 published studies** and several decades of use put it in a category
of its own. Nothing else on the shelf is close.

## How it actually works

Your muscles run on **ATP**, but they only store enough for a few seconds of
maximal effort. **Phosphocreatine (PCr)** is the immediate backup: it donates a
phosphate group to regenerate ATP almost instantly, without oxygen and without
waiting for glycolysis.

A 70 kg person holds roughly **120–140 g of creatine**, about 95% of it in
skeletal muscle, and turns over **1–2 g per day** into creatinine, which the
kidneys excrete. On a normal mixed diet, muscle stores sit at about **60–80% of
capacity**. Supplementation fills the remaining 20–40%.

The result: **more available phosphocreatine**, so you can produce more force for
slightly longer before fatiguing. That means **an extra rep or two per set**,
week after week — and it's that accumulated extra training volume, not creatine
itself, that drives most of the muscle gain. Creatine doesn't build muscle. It
lets you do the work that builds muscle.

There are secondary mechanisms too: **cell volumisation** (drawing water into the
muscle cell, a mild anabolic signal), improved **calcium handling**, reduced
protein breakdown, and possible effects on satellite cell activity.

## What you get from food

Creatine occurs almost exclusively in animal flesh, at roughly **3–5 g per kg of
raw meat or fish**:

| Food | Creatine per 100 g raw |
| --- | --- |
| Herring | ~0.65–1.0 g |
| Pork | ~0.5 g |
| Beef | ~0.45 g |
| Salmon | ~0.45 g |
| Chicken | ~0.35 g |
| **Any plant food** | **~0 g** |

A typical omnivore gets **1–2 g/day** from food; the body synthesises another
**~1 g/day** from glycine, arginine, and methionine. **Vegetarians and vegans get
essentially none from food**, run measurably lower muscle stores, and
consistently show the **largest responses** to supplementation — including the
clearest cognitive effects.

## Forms: why it's always monohydrate

The supplement industry has produced a long list of "advanced" creatines. None of
them has beaten plain monohydrate in a head-to-head trial.

| Form | Claim | Reality |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Monohydrate** | — | ~99% bioavailable. The form used in nearly every study. **Use this.** |
| **Micronised monohydrate** | Dissolves better | True, and genuinely nicer to drink. Same molecule, tiny price premium. Fine. |
| **Creatine HCl** | Better solubility, smaller dose | More soluble, yes. **No evidence** of better muscle saturation at lower doses. |
| **Buffered ("Kre-Alkalyn")** | Survives stomach acid | Monohydrate already survives stomach acid. Directly compared, it performed **no better** — and in one trial worse. |
| **Ethyl ester** | Better absorption | **Degrades to creatinine** in the gut. Measurably *worse*. Avoid. |
| **Liquid creatine** | Convenience | Creatine is unstable in solution. Largely degraded before you drink it. |
| **Gummies** | Convenience | The idea is fine; independent testing has repeatedly found products **badly underdosed** or degraded. Verify before trusting. |
| **Nitrate, magnesium chelate, etc.** | Various | Unproven, more expensive. |

**Creapure®** is a branded German monohydrate with tight purity specifications.
It's a genuine quality marker — creatine manufactured cheaply can carry
**dicyandiamide, dihydrotriazine, and creatinine** impurities — but it costs
roughly **2.5× more** than commodity monohydrate. Reputable EU brands with
third-party testing are generally fine.

## Dosing

**The simple version: 3–5 g per day, every day, forever.** That's it.

### Loading or not?

| Protocol | Time to saturation | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Loading:** 0.3 g/kg/day (~20–25 g), split into 4 doses, for 5–7 days, then 3–5 g/day | **~1 week** | Faster. Higher chance of GI upset. |
| **No loading:** 3–5 g/day from the start | **~3–4 weeks** | Same end point. Gentler. |

The two protocols reach **identical muscle creatine levels**. Loading only buys
you three weeks. Load if you have a competition in a month; otherwise don't
bother.

### Dosing by body size

The 5 g figure comes from studies on average-sized men. Creatine is stored in
**muscle**, so the right dose scales with lean mass:

| Body weight | Maintenance dose |
| --- | --- |
| Under ~60 kg | 3 g/day |
| 60–90 kg | 5 g/day |
| Over ~90 kg, or high lean mass | 5–10 g/day |

Very active people and those training in heat may sit at the higher end, since
sweat and higher turnover increase daily losses.

### Timing

Largely irrelevant. A few studies favour **post-workout** by a small margin,
possibly due to elevated blood flow, but the effect is minor and inconsistent.
**Daily consistency dominates timing completely** — creatine works by keeping
stores saturated, not by acute action.

Taking it **with carbohydrate and/or protein** modestly increases uptake via
insulin-mediated transport. Useful during a loading phase; unnecessary for
maintenance. The old worry that **caffeine blunts creatine** traces to a single
1996 study and has not held up — plenty of pre-workouts contain both.

## Non-responders

Roughly **20–30% of people show little or no response**. This isn't mysterious:
the main reason is that their **muscle stores were already near saturation**,
typically because they eat a lot of meat and fish. There's a ceiling, and if
you're already at it, adding more does nothing.

Other predictors of a **large** response: vegetarian or vegan diet, high
proportion of **type II (fast-twitch) fibres**, larger initial muscle mass, and
lower baseline creatine.

If four weeks of consistent 5 g/day produces nothing — no weight change, no extra
reps — you may simply be a non-responder. That's a legitimate reason to stop.

## Safety: the four myths

Creatine has one of the strongest safety records of any supplement, with studies
running up to **five years** of continuous use. The persistent worries don't
survive contact with the data.

**"It damages your kidneys."** No. Long-term studies in healthy people show no
adverse effect on renal function. The confusion comes from a real but harmless
artefact: creatine raises **serum creatinine**, which is the marker used to
*estimate* kidney function. A routine blood test can therefore make a creatine
user look like they have mildly reduced kidney function when they don't.

> **Worth doing:** tell your doctor you take creatine before any blood work. It
> prevents an unnecessary workup — or, worse, a real kidney problem being blamed
> on the supplement. People with pre-existing kidney disease should discuss it
> before starting.

**"It causes hair loss."** This traces to a **single 2009 study of 20 rugby
players** that found an increase in DHT after a loading phase. It was never
replicated, and it never measured hair at all. A **12-week randomised controlled
trial published in 2025** finally tested it directly: no difference in DHT, no
difference in the DHT-to-testosterone ratio, and no difference in hair density,
thickness, or follicle health versus placebo. The trial was small (~38 completers)
and didn't screen for genetic predisposition, so it isn't the last word — but the
weight of evidence now points clearly against the claim.

**"It causes cramps and dehydration."** The opposite, if anything. Controlled
trials in athletes training in heat find creatine users experience **fewer**
cramps and no impairment in thermoregulation.

**"The weight gain is bloating."** The typical **1–2 kg** gained in the first
weeks is water drawn **inside the muscle cell** (intracellular), not under the
skin. It makes muscle look fuller, not softer. It's also why creatine can be
awkward for **weight-class athletes** near a weigh-in — the one genuine downside.

Reported side effects at normal doses are essentially limited to **mild GI
discomfort**, almost always during loading and almost always fixed by splitting
the dose or dropping to 5 g/day.

## The brain: the interesting frontier

The brain uses creatine too, and takes it up slowly across the blood-brain
barrier — which is why brain effects generally need **higher doses** than muscle
effects.

The clearest finding so far is under **stress and sleep deprivation**. A crossover
trial gave a **single high dose (0.35 g/kg — around 25 g for an 70 kg adult)**
during sleep deprivation and found improved memory, attention, and processing
speed — effects appearing within ~3.5 hours, peaking at 4 hours, and lasting up
to 9 — alongside measurable changes in cerebral high-energy phosphates. Follow-up
work is now testing whether lower doses (~0.2 g/kg) achieve the same thing.

Beyond that:

- Benefits are **largest in vegetarians and vegans**, consistent with lower
  baseline stores.
- Effects in well-rested, well-fed omnivores are **small and inconsistent**.
- Research in **depression** (as an adjunct, particularly in women), **traumatic
  brain injury**, and **age-related cognitive decline** is promising but early.

**Interpretation:** worth knowing about, not yet worth restructuring your dosing
around. Standard 3–5 g/day is not a brain dose; the emerging cognitive protocols
use far more.

## Women, older adults, and adolescents

- **Women** respond to creatine the same way men do, with the same strength and
  lean mass benefits, and typically start with ~70–80% of men's baseline stores.
  Additional interest exists around **mood, fatigue, and perimenopause**, though
  that research is still developing.
- **Older adults** are arguably the population with the most to gain. Combined
  with resistance training, creatine consistently produces **greater gains in lean
  tissue mass and strength** than training alone in people over 60 — directly
  relevant to sarcopenia and fall risk.
- **Adolescents:** major sports-nutrition bodies consider monohydrate acceptable
  for adolescent athletes who are training seriously, eating well, and using it
  under supervision. Food and training come first.

## Practical protocol

1. **Buy micronised monohydrate**, 500 g or 1 kg, from a reputable brand. If
   you're drug-tested, choose **Informed Sport** or **NSF Certified for Sport**.
2. **Take 5 g every day** — training days, rest days, holidays. Adjust to 3 g if
   you're small, 10 g if you're large.
3. **Skip loading** unless you need saturation within a week.
4. **Mix into anything** — a shake, juice, coffee, water. It's not heat-sensitive
   in the way it's often claimed to be, though it does degrade slowly once
   dissolved, so drink it rather than leaving it out.
5. **Don't cycle it.** There's no receptor downregulation, no tolerance, no need
   for a break. Stopping simply lets stores drift back to baseline over about
   **4–6 weeks**.
6. **Mention it to your doctor** before blood work.

## The takeaway

- Creatine works by topping up **phosphocreatine**, letting you do slightly more
  work — the extra work is what builds the muscle.
- **Monohydrate, 3–5 g/day, indefinitely.** Every "advanced" form is equal or
  worse, and more expensive.
- **Loading is optional** — it saves three weeks, nothing more.
- **20–30% are non-responders**, usually because they already eat a lot of meat.
  **Vegetarians respond most.**
- It does **not** harm kidneys (though it raises the *creatinine* marker — tell
  your doctor), does **not** cause cramping, and a **2025 RCT found no effect on
  DHT or hair**.
- Weight gain of **1–2 kg is intracellular water**, not fat or bloat.
- **Brain effects are real but need higher doses** than the standard 5 g, and are
  most pronounced under sleep deprivation and in vegetarians.
- **Older adults may benefit most of all** when combined with lifting.
- Cost: **under 2 kr per day**.
