# Body Recomposition

**Body recomposition** — "recomp" — is losing fat and building muscle *at the same
time*. It sounds like it violates the energy-balance rules, and for a while it was
dismissed as impossible. The nuance: fat and muscle are **separate tissues drawing
on separate accounting**, so under the right conditions you can subtract one while
adding the other.

## How it's possible

Overall energy balance governs your *total* stored energy, but your body can
**partition** where energy goes. In a modest deficit, fat can be pulled from
adipose tissue to fund muscle growth elsewhere — muscle is built from **amino
acids and a training signal**, and the *energy* for that construction can come
from body fat. So the scale can hold steady (or fall) while your composition
shifts. It's not free — it's slower than dedicated bulking or cutting — but it's
real.

## Who recomps well

Recomp works best when the body is **primed to build muscle**, so it's strongly
population-dependent:

- **Beginners** — untrained muscle grows readily; "newbie gains" happen even in a
  deficit.
- **Returning trainees** — "muscle memory" lets previously-built muscle come back
  fast.
- **Detrained or overweight individuals** — plenty of stored fuel (body fat) and
  high growth potential.
- **People on great programs after a lay-off.**

It works **poorly** for **lean, advanced trainees**, whose muscle-building
potential is nearly tapped and who have little fat to spare. For them, the
separate-phases approach is more efficient.

## How to set up a recomp

1. **Eat around maintenance** (or a very small deficit) — enough energy to support
   muscle, little enough to nudge fat down.
2. **Protein high** — ~**2.0–2.4 g/kg**. This is the non-negotiable that makes
   recomp possible; it fuels muscle building while the deficit pulls fat.
3. **Train hard and progressively** — resistance training with genuine
   **progressive overload** is the signal that directs energy toward muscle.
4. **Nutrient timing helps at the margins** — protein around training, carbs to
   fuel hard sessions (see the timing chapters).
5. **Be patient and track composition, not weight** — the scale may barely move.
   Use measurements, photos, and strength gains.

## When to bulk and cut instead

For lean, experienced lifters chasing maximum muscle, **dedicated phases** are
usually more efficient than perpetual recomp:

- **Bulk (lean gaining):** a modest surplus (~**5–15%** over maintenance, roughly
  200–400 kcal) to maximise muscle growth while limiting fat gain. Big "dirty"
  bulks add mostly fat and a longer cut later.
- **Cut:** a deficit (previous chapters) to strip the fat while high protein and
  lifting hold the muscle you built.

The classic cycle is a **lean bulk** to build, then a **controlled cut** to
reveal it — alternating so you're never gaining or losing for too long.

## The takeaway

- **Recomp is real** — fat and muscle answer to partly separate accounting, and
  body fat can fund muscle growth.
- It works best for **beginners, returners, and higher-body-fat individuals**;
  poorly for **lean advanced** lifters.
- Set it up with **maintenance-ish calories, high protein, and progressive
  training** — and judge by composition, not the scale.
- Advanced lifters usually do better **bulking and cutting** in dedicated phases.
